Mechanisms for Mineralocorticoid-Driven Age-Related Hypertension: Potential Therapeutic Role of Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists and Aldosterone Synthase Inhibitors.

Revue : Circulation Research | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-16

Resultats principaux : This comprehensive review examines the pathophysiological mechanisms underlying mineralocorticoid-driven hypertension in the aging population, drawing on data from multiple preclinical and clinical studies. The authors identify three key age-related pathological processes: autonomous aldosterone production by adrenal cells (compounded in obesity by adipose-derived pro-aldosterone factors), increased mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) expression driven by oxidative stress and inflammation-activated transcription factors, and aldosterone-independent MR activation via Rac1 signaling, angiotensin II, and declining expression of the cortisol-inactivating enzyme 11beta-HSD2. Three pharmacological classes targeting this pathway are reviewed: traditional steroidal MR antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone), newer nonsteroidal MR antagonists (finerenone, esaxerenone), and aldosterone synthase inhibitors (lorundrostat, baxdrostat), each compared for MR selectivity, potency, and capacity to block both aldosterone-mediated and ligand-independent MR activation. All three drug classes raise serum potassium proportional to the degree of renal MR inhibition, representing a clinically significant safety consideration that must be monitored in elderly patients with pre-existing renal impairment or concurrent use of other potassium-sparing agents. The review identifies a critical evidence gap: no head-to-head trials have compared aldosterone synthase inhibition versus MR antagonism specifically in elderly or very old individuals, limiting evidence-based therapeutic selection in this high-prevalence, high-cardiovascular-risk group.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review reinforces the strategic importance of nonsteroidal MR antagonists (finerenone) and emerging aldosterone synthase inhibitors as next-generation antihypertensive agents for elderly patients, a segment poorly addressed by standard first-line regimens. The three compounding mechanistic drivers in aging patients create a compelling scientific narrative for sequential or combination targeting of the RAAS in this demographic, supporting KOL discussions around precision RAAS modulation beyond conventional ACE inhibitor and ARB use. The absence of geriatric head-to-head trials is a white space for investigator-initiated studies or phase IV programs that could differentiate product positioning, generate comparative effectiveness data, and anchor KOL engagement around unmet needs in elderly hypertension management.

Waist circumference versus BMI: A cross-sectional study comparing Cardiometabolic Risk association in the Nepalese Population.

Revue : PloS One | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-16

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study enrolled 678 Nepalese adults to compare the predictive value of body mass index (BMI) and waist circumference (WC) across six cardiometabolic outcomes: hypertension, type 2 diabetes, thyroid disease, fatty liver disease, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, and dyslipidemia. WC showed a stronger correlation with triglycerides (Spearman rho = 0.35) compared to BMI (rho = 0.24), and WC was the only measure significantly and independently associated with hypertension in multivariable logistic regression, highlighting the primacy of central adiposity as a hypertension driver in this South Asian population. BMI demonstrated higher odds ratios per unit increase for fatty liver disease, NAFLD, and dyslipidemia compared to WC, suggesting the two anthropometric measures provide complementary rather than redundant information for cardiometabolic risk stratification. ROC analyses showed comparable but modest predictive performance for both measures (AUC 0.6-0.7) across most outcomes, yet discrimination improved substantially (AUC >0.8) when BMI and WC were combined with age and sex, underscoring the value of integrated anthropometric-demographic risk models. The study is limited by its cross-sectional design (precluding causal inference), single-center scope, reliance on international anthropometric cutoffs that may not reflect locally validated South Asian-specific thresholds, and relatively small sample size given the six outcomes examined.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings underscore that WC is a more sensitive hypertension screening tool than BMI in the Nepalese population, where the characteristic South Asian 'thin-fat' phenotype causes central adiposity to be disproportionately elevated relative to BMI, with direct relevance to cardiovascular prevention programs in emerging markets. From a Medical Affairs perspective, the data support advocacy for locally validated anthropometric cutoffs in regional guideline development for South Asian countries, creating opportunities to engage national hypertension societies and contribute to country-specific guideline updates. KOL engagement in Nepal and broader South Asia should integrate these epidemiological insights into positioning narratives around central obesity-driven hypertension, reinforcing awareness of the need for region-adapted screening protocols as a foundation for antihypertensive treatment initiation.

Lifestyle and metabolic determinants of early- and late-onset colorectal cancer: insights from a tertiary medical center in Alabama.

Revue : Cancer Causes and Control | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-16

Resultats principaux : This retrospective study analyzed 3,209 colorectal cancer (CRC) patients treated at a tertiary center in Alabama between 2010 and 2023, examining associations between lifestyle and clinical factors (smoking, BMI, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia) and age at CRC diagnosis, categorized as early-onset CRC (EOCRC, <50 years) or late-onset CRC (LOCRC, ≥50 years). Hypertension was significantly associated with LOCRC with an adjusted OR of 2.46 (95% CI: 1.65-3.66), alongside hyperlipidemia (OR 3.32), type 2 diabetes (OR 1.92), and former smoking status (OR 1.89), all pointing to shared cardiometabolic pathways underlying late-onset colorectal carcinogenesis. Conversely, obesity showed an inverse association with LOCRC (OR 0.61; 95% CI: 0.48-0.77), suggesting obese patients were more likely to present with EOCRC, which may reflect earlier metabolic disruption and altered tumor biology in younger patients with excess adiposity. Race-stratified analyses revealed that hypertension was significantly associated with LOCRC specifically in White patients, while hyperlipidemia showed a stronger association with LOCRC in Black patients, underscoring the importance of race-conscious screening and risk stratification strategies in CRC prevention. The study is limited by its retrospective, single-center design, potential misclassification of clinical risk factors from electronic health records, absence of dietary and physical activity data, and lack of prospective validation of the observed associations.

Implications Medical Affairs : The consistent association of hypertension with late-onset colorectal cancer reinforces the systemic multimorbidity burden of uncontrolled blood pressure extending well beyond traditional cardiovascular endpoints, providing Medical Affairs teams with a compelling narrative around comprehensive cardiometabolic risk management. These findings create opportunities for collaborative cross-specialty KOL engagement at the interface of cardiology and oncology, as they demonstrate that hypertension management may reduce risk in a high-prevalence cancer indication, strengthening the broader value proposition of antihypertensive therapy. Demonstrating that blood pressure control addresses a modifiable CRC risk factor could enhance value dossiers and population health messaging in multimorbid patient segments, particularly in markets where cardio-oncology programs are gaining prominence.

Assessment of markers of primary aldosteronism in systemic sclerosis and their relationships with renal and cardiovascular outcomes.

Revue : RMD Open | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-15

Resultats principaux : This single-center prospective cohort study analyzed 112 patients with systemic sclerosis (SSc) and 48 normotensive healthy controls to evaluate the prevalence of primary aldosteronism (PA) and its associations with hypertension, renal, and cardiovascular outcomes in SSc over a median follow-up of 5.1 years. Hypertension was present in 37% of SSc patients at baseline and increased to 52% at last follow-up, demonstrating the progressive and substantial cardiovascular burden of this autoimmune condition over time. PA screening using the aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR) revealed positive results in 8% of all SSc patients and 12% of the hypertensive subset, rates comparable to those reported in general hypertensive populations, supporting the clinical rationale for routine PA screening in hypertensive SSc patients. Multivariable regression modelling found no significant associations between PA biomarkers (renin, aldosterone, ARR, and hybrid steroid 18-hydroxycortisol) and SSc-related renal or cardiovascular outcomes, suggesting that PA is not a dominant mechanistic driver of organ complications in this specific autoimmune population. Key limitations include the single-center design, small sample size (112 SSc patients), significant sex imbalance (79% female), a 17-year age difference between SSc patients and healthy controls potentially confounding comparisons, and the absence of confirmatory aldosterone suppression testing beyond initial ARR screening.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings are directly relevant to Medical Affairs teams working in rare disease or autoimmune cardiovascular overlap conditions, as they indicate that hypertension in SSc is highly prevalent (up to 52%) and that PA screening at rates similar to the general hypertensive population appears justified, potentially broadening the target population for aldosterone-targeted diagnostics and therapeutics. The lack of association between PA markers and SSc organ outcomes suggests that the pathophysiology of cardiovascular damage in SSc likely involves non-RAAS-mediated mechanisms such as vasculopathy and fibrosis, a nuance important for accurate KOL scientific communication to avoid overstatement of MR antagonist utility in this population. Engaging rheumatology and clinical immunology KOLs in hypertension workup protocols for systemic autoimmune diseases represents an emerging Medical Affairs initiative, supporting guideline updates that would formalize PA screening in SSc-associated hypertension and potentially opening new patient segments for antihypertensive portfolio products.

Targeting the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System: Preclinical and Clinical Developments.

Revue : Circulation Research | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-16

Resultats principaux : This comprehensive review in Circulation Research systematically summarizes the pharmacology and clinical development of agents targeting both the classical and the protective renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) for hypertension, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease treatment. The classical RAAS pharmacology is organized across five established therapeutic classes: mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (steroidal and nonsteroidal), ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), direct renin inhibitors, and next-generation agents including RNA-based therapies (antisense oligonucleotides and small interfering RNA) targeting hepatic angiotensinogen synthesis to achieve durable blood pressure reduction with once-monthly dosing. Preclinical and early clinical data on RNA-based angiotensinogen-targeting agents suggest the potential for sustained and titratable blood pressure reduction without the hypotension risk associated with direct vasodilators, potentially overcoming adherence barriers inherent to daily oral antihypertensive regimens. The review also dedicates substantial attention to the alternative or protective RAAS axis, centered on ACE2, angiotensin-(1-7), alamandine, and their receptors (Mas, MrgD), which exert vasodilatory, anti-inflammatory, antifibrotic, and cardioprotective effects, counterbalancing classical RAAS-driven vasoconstriction and organ damage. Safety considerations across drug classes are discussed, including hyperkalemia associated with MR antagonists, hypotension, and renal dysfunction, with newer nonsteroidal MR antagonists and RNA-based agents designed to minimize these risks through improved specificity and pharmacokinetic profiles.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review provides Medical Affairs teams with a comprehensive and up-to-date scientific landscape of the RAAS therapeutic space, confirming that the field remains highly active with multiple drug classes at different developmental stages, necessitating clear and evidence-based differentiation strategies for each agent across mechanism of action, selectivity, and safety profile. The emergence of RNA-based angiotensinogen-targeting therapies represents a paradigm shift in long-acting antihypertensive treatment with the potential to disrupt current standard-of-care algorithms, requiring early and proactive KOL education, scientific exchange programs, and strategic positioning of existing ACE inhibitors and ARBs in combination regimens with these novel agents. Medical Affairs should prioritize scientific exchange activities around the ACE2/Ang-(1-7) protective axis as an emerging area with therapeutic potential extending into metabolic, fibrotic, and inflammatory disorders, broadening portfolio relevance and creating differentiation opportunities beyond blood pressure-lowering endpoints.

Comparative Efficacy and Safety of Clevidipine Versus Nicardipine in Hypertensive Emergencies: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.

Revue : Clinical Cardiology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07

Resultats principaux : This systematic review and meta-analysis searched PubMed, Cochrane Library, and ClinicalTrials.gov through July 3, 2026, and included 9 comparative studies (8 retrospective cohort studies and 1 randomized controlled trial) involving 1,378 patients receiving intravenous clevidipine or nicardipine for hypertensive emergencies. No statistically significant differences were observed in the primary outcomes of time to target systolic blood pressure (SBP) and percentage of time within the target SBP range between the two dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers. Secondary outcomes including ICU length of stay, hospital length of stay, incidence of hypotension, tachycardia, acute kidney injury, need for rescue antihypertensive therapy, and in-hospital mortality were also statistically comparable between clevidipine and nicardipine. Subgroup analyses revealed that clevidipine achieved significantly faster target SBP attainment in stroke and neurocritical care populations, as well as in general hypertensive crisis settings, suggesting potential advantages for clevidipine in specific acute neurological contexts requiring rapid and precise blood pressure titration. Clevidipine was associated with significantly lower infusion drug volumes overall; however, the study is substantially limited by the predominance of retrospective designs (8/9 studies), heterogeneity in patient populations and target SBP definitions, and the very small sample contribution from the single RCT, making definitive clinical superiority claims premature.

Implications Medical Affairs : This meta-analysis provides a Level II evidence base supporting the overall comparable clinical efficacy and safety of clevidipine and nicardipine for hypertensive emergencies, which can inform non-inferiority positioning in acute care educational materials and hospital formulary presentations. The subgroup-level advantage of clevidipine in stroke and neurocritical care patients is a clinically differentiating finding that could be strategically leveraged in targeted KOL and formulary committee discussions with neurologists, neurosurgeons, and intensivists managing hypertensive emergencies in the neurocritical care setting. The association of clevidipine with lower infusion volumes offers a potential pharmacoeconomic argument for formulary positioning and hospital budget discussions, though a dedicated cost-effectiveness analysis and confirmatory RCT data would substantially strengthen this value argument before inclusion in formal health technology assessment submissions.

Contemporary Concepts in the Onset and Maintenance of Atrial Fibrillation: Mechanisms, Substrates, and Clinical Implications.

Revue : Heart Rhythm | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-16

Resultats principaux : This narrative review provides a comprehensive mechanistic analysis of atrial fibrillation (AF) initiation and progression from Columbia University and Imperial College London, examining the transition from trigger-dominated paroxysmal AF to substrate-driven persistent, longstanding persistent, and permanent forms. Low-voltage substrate regions reflecting underlying fibrotic scar are identified as a hallmark of advanced AF, with the atrial milieu progressively developing electrical uncoupling, fibrosis, adipose deposits, calcification, and wall thickness gradients as AF becomes increasingly disorganized and refractory to drug, ablative, or surgical therapy. The review discusses ablation strategies, noting that targeting disorganized low-voltage areas beyond standard pulmonary vein isolation may improve procedural success rates for persistent and longstanding persistent AF, though the quantitative benefit from substrate-targeted ablation remains under active investigation. Critically for hypertension-related Medical Affairs, the review explicitly identifies lifestyle-based modifiable factors—including patient diet, exercise, and weight management—as important preventive and disease-control measures, noting that anti-inflammatory interventions may halt or partially reverse structural and electrical remodeling of the atrium. The review is largely narrative without original quantitative data, limiting direct evidence-grading; emerging therapeutic modalities discussed include novel anti-remodeling pharmacological agents and advanced pacing methods for AF prevention, which remain at preliminary stages of development.

Implications Medical Affairs : Although primarily an electrophysiology review, this article is directly relevant to hypertension-focused Medical Affairs as hypertension is the single most prevalent modifiable risk factor for AF development, and the lifestyle interventions identified (diet, exercise, weight loss) overlap substantially with antihypertensive lifestyle recommendations, reinforcing integrated cardiovascular risk management messaging. For portfolio strategy, the mechanistic link between hypertensive heart disease, atrial remodeling, and AF substrate reinforces the value of comprehensive blood pressure control in arrhythmia prevention, a scientific narrative that can be shared with electrophysiology and general cardiology KOLs to position antihypertensive agents within a broader cardiovascular protective framework. The emerging focus on anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic interventions to reverse atrial remodeling opens potential collaborative research opportunities for antihypertensive agents with demonstrated anti-fibrotic properties, an area Medical Affairs could explore in cross-indication scientific exchange forums with arrhythmia specialists.

Non-pharmacological interventions for older adults with comorbid hypertension and type 2 diabetes: A systematic review of randomized controlled trials.

Revue : Geriatric Nursing | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-16

Resultats principaux : This PRISMA 2020-compliant systematic review searched PubMed, Embase, and Cochrane CENTRAL through October 2024 and included 16 RCTs evaluating non-pharmacological interventions in adults aged 60 years or older with comorbid hypertension and type 2 diabetes, the most prevalent multimorbidity combination in older adult populations. Interventions were classified into five categories: exercise (n=3), diet (n=2), self-monitoring (n=1), multi-strategy (n=3), and comprehensive lifestyle modification (n=7), delivered via hospital (n=6), community (n=6), or telehealth (n=4) settings, providing a broad overview of the non-pharmacological landscape. Approximately 75% of trials reported statistically significant effects on their prespecified primary outcomes—most commonly blood pressure reduction or HbA1c improvement—demonstrating meaningful therapeutic potential for behavioral and lifestyle approaches in managing this multimorbid population alongside pharmacotherapy. Comprehensive lifestyle modification programs, particularly those combining individualized behavioral planning with technology-assisted support (telehealth), demonstrated the most consistent and sustained effects, outperforming single-component interventions such as diet or exercise alone. Major limitations include the absence of post-intervention follow-up in 14 of 16 trials, high risk of bias in several studies assessed via the RoB 2 tool, heterogeneity between interventions preventing formal meta-analysis, and the overall small number of trials specifically targeting this high-risk elderly dual-diagnosis population.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings reinforce the guideline-concordant role of comprehensive lifestyle modification—especially telehealth-integrated individualized programs—as an effective and scalable adjunct to pharmacotherapy for elderly patients with hypertension and diabetes multimorbidity, with direct implications for integrated care models endorsed in current major hypertension guidelines. The near-universal absence of long-term follow-up data across included trials represents a major evidence gap that Medical Affairs could help address by promoting registry-linked cohort studies or embedding structured non-pharmacological protocol components within clinical trials of antihypertensive agents in elderly populations. Engaging geriatric medicine, endocrinology, and primary care KOLs in discussions about technology-assisted multimorbidity management would support Medical Affairs positioning around holistic hypertension control in the elderly, a critical and growing healthcare priority as aging populations place increasing pressure on cardiovascular disease management systems.

Arsenic Exposure During Pregnancy: Impacts to Maternal and Offspring Cardiovascular Health.

Revue : Medical Research Archives | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06

Resultats principaux : This narrative review from Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health synthesizes current evidence on the adverse cardiovascular health impacts of gestational inorganic arsenic (iAs) exposure on both maternal and offspring outcomes, contextualizing them within the setting of rising cardiovascular disease-related maternal mortality globally. Inorganic arsenic contaminates food and water supplies for over 140 million people worldwide, primarily in South Asia, parts of Latin America, and Southeast Asia, and gestational exposure is associated with increased maternal risk of hypertension, preeclampsia, and structural cardiac changes that may persist or worsen postpartum. For offspring, prenatal iAs exposure is linked to preterm delivery, low birth weight, and congenital heart defects, as well as predisposition to lifelong cardiovascular disease through epigenetic programming and developmental vascular dysfunction occurring during critical fetal developmental windows. The review highlights that traditional maternal CVD risk factors (advanced maternal age, obesity, diabetes, hypertension) do not fully account for observed trends in rising maternal cardiovascular mortality, implicating environmental exposures such as iAs as an additive and underappreciated contributor to the global maternal health burden. Key limitations of the evidence base include reliance predominantly on observational and epidemiological study designs, significant heterogeneity in arsenic exposure quantification methods, limited dose-response characterization across populations, and a near-complete absence of interventional studies specifically targeting arsenic reduction to improve maternal or offspring cardiovascular outcomes.

Implications Medical Affairs : Arsenic-mediated gestational hypertension represents a significant and underrecognized public health concern, with direct relevance to Medical Affairs strategies targeting emerging markets with high environmental arsenic burden—particularly South Asia, parts of Latin America, and Southeast Asia—where antihypertensive therapy in pregnancy may address a substantial and underserved unmet need. The biological plausibility of arsenic-induced endothelial dysfunction and RAAS dysregulation provides mechanistic rationale for investigating whether RAAS-targeting antihypertensive agents could attenuate arsenic-driven maternal and fetal cardiovascular complications, representing an innovative and differentiated research hypothesis for Medical Affairs-supported investigator-initiated studies. Strategic KOL engagement with obstetric cardiologists, environmental health specialists, and maternal-fetal medicine experts would position Medical Affairs at the forefront of an emerging scientific domain—environmental risk factors and hypertensive disorders of pregnancy—that intersects cardiovascular medicine, toxicology, and global health equity priorities.
