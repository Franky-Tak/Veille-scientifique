Sociodemographic determinants of Pulmonary Hypertension in a Tertiary Care Hospital in Southern India.

Revue : La Clinica terapeutica | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study enrolled 114 patients diagnosed with pulmonary hypertension (PH) by echocardiographic criteria at a tertiary care hospital in Southern India, with a mean age of 57.1 +/- 16 years and a male predominance of 64.9%. Group 2 PH (left heart disease-related, 36%) was the most common etiology, followed by Group 1 PH (28.9%), while diabetes mellitus (68.4%) and systemic hypertension (56.1%) constituted the most frequent comorbidities. Right ventricular function was markedly impaired across all WHO groups, with mean TAPSE of 14.9 +/- 4.42 mm, fractional area change of 34.2 +/- 10.6%, and RV global longitudinal strain of 13.9 +/- 5.2%, with RV tissue Doppler velocity differing significantly between groups (p < 0.001). Health-related quality of life assessed via the EmPHasis-10 questionnaire showed substantial impairment (mean score 42.2 +/- 6.22), indicating significant functional burden across the cohort. Older patients carried greater than threefold odds of severe disease compared to the youngest group (OR = 3.3, 95% CI: 1.0-10.6; p = 0.048); the study is limited by its single-center cross-sectional design and sample size of 114 patients.

Implications Medical Affairs : The finding that systemic hypertension is present in over half of PH patients underscores the need for proactive pulmonary hypertension screening within broader hypertension management frameworks, potentially informing future guideline updates that integrate PH risk stratification into routine antihypertensive care. For Medical Affairs, this evidence creates an opportunity to position optimal antihypertensive treatment as part of a PH prevention narrative in the Indian subcontinent, a market where epidemiological data are historically scarce. Engagement with KOLs in cardiology and pulmonology in India could support educational initiatives that bridge systemic and pulmonary hypertension management, reinforcing the value of integrated cardiovascular risk assessment programs.

Family support mediates the relationship between medication adherence and quality of life among patients with hypertension in Ghana: A cross-sectional study.

Revue : PloS one | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-24

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study enrolled 365 hypertensive patients attending a university hospital in Kumasi, Ghana, using validated instruments including the Short Form-36 for quality of life, the APGAR scale for family support, and the Morisky Medication Adherence Scale. Higher medication adherence was significantly associated with better quality of life (total effect beta = 1.07, 95% CI: 0.63-1.50, p < 0.001), confirming the primary hypothesis. Family support was identified as a significant partial mediator of this relationship (Average Causal Mediation Effect = 0.09, 95% CI: 0.02-0.19, p = 0.008), accounting for approximately 8% of the total effect, with this mediation persisting after adjustment for sociodemographic factors, knowledge scores, healthcare access, and lifestyle variables (mediated proportion 8.9%, p = 0.038). Patients from highly functional families demonstrated better quality of life with increasing medication adherence scores, highlighting the psychosocial dimension of hypertension self-management. The study is limited by its cross-sectional design, which precludes causal inference, and by reliance on self-reported adherence measures that may be subject to social desirability bias.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings provide real-world evidence from sub-Saharan Africa that adherence-support programs must incorporate psychosocial and family engagement components to be effective, with direct implications for Medical Affairs teams designing patient support programs for antihypertensive agents in emerging markets. Pharmaceutical companies could leverage this evidence to develop and communicate adherence-support tools that include family engagement modules, potentially differentiating products in West African markets where psychosocial support infrastructure is limited. This study also opens KOL engagement opportunities with health policy stakeholders in Ghana and West Africa interested in integrating family-based interventions into national hypertension control programs.

Age at menarche and adverse pregnancy and perinatal outcomes: triangulating evidence from multivariable and Mendelian randomization analyses.

Revue : International journal of epidemiology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-24

Resultats principaux : This study used a triangulation approach combining observational multivariable regression in the ALSPAC cohort (N = 9,441) and two-sample Mendelian randomization (MR) using the MR-PREG collaboration (77,683 to 707,797 pregnancies) to assess whether age at menarche causally influences 13 pre-specified pregnancy outcomes including hypertensive disorders. In observational analyses, older age at menarche was associated with lower risk of hypertensive disorders of pregnancy, gestational hypertension, and preeclampsia (OR = 0.91, 95% CI: 0.87-0.94 per one-year increase). However, MR analyses substantially attenuated these associations after adjustment for genetically-proxied adiposity (OR = 0.95, 95% CI: 0.90-1.01 in multivariable MR), suggesting the relationship is primarily mediated by adiposity rather than a direct causal effect of menarche timing. No clear causal effects were found for small-for-gestational-age, low birthweight, post-term birth, or perinatal depression. The key limitation is reduced statistical power in multivariable MR due to sample size constraints, and sensitivity analyses showed inconsistent results for several secondary outcomes.

Implications Medical Affairs : This rigorous causal inference study establishes that the apparent link between early menarche and hypertensive pregnancy complications is largely driven by adiposity, shifting clinical focus toward weight management as the primary modifiable target for gestational hypertension risk reduction, with direct implications for guideline-aligned preconception care programs. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this finding reinforces the rationale for upstream obesity management as a prevention strategy for gestational hypertension, creating a scientific narrative applicable to companies with obesity or antihypertensive portfolios. KOL engagement should target reproductive medicine specialists and maternal-fetal medicine experts who lead guideline committees addressing gestational hypertension risk stratification and prevention.

Intersection of Pediatric Hypertension and Cardiovascular-Kidney-Metabolic Disease.

Revue : Current hypertension reports | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-23

Resultats principaux : This narrative review examines the intersection of pediatric hypertension and cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic (CKM) syndrome in children and adolescents, highlighting that CKM syndrome is highly prevalent in the pediatric population with onset influenced by antenatal risk factors including maternal hypertensive disorders of pregnancy. The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) is identified as the key mechanistic mediator linking antenatal risk, early life course exposures, and the development of hypertension and obesity in children. Food insecurity and social determinants of health emerge as major drivers of childhood obesity that advance CKM staging, and the combination of hypertension and higher CKM staging is strongly associated with increased atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease risk. Effective management strategies highlighted include attention to early life course nutrition, access to healthy foods, and pharmacological interventions targeting the RAAS pathway, all of which are critical to slowing CKM stage advancement. The review's narrative rather than systematic design limits the strength of evidence synthesis and the ability to draw firm quantitative conclusions.

Implications Medical Affairs : RAAS activation as the central mechanistic link between antenatal risk and pediatric hypertension provides a strong biological rationale for early pharmacological intervention with RAAS-blocking agents in pediatric populations, where regulatory frameworks for such use are gradually expanding and Medical Affairs teams should proactively map the evolving evidence and guideline landscape. This review identifies an emerging multidisciplinary field at the intersection of pediatric nephrology, cardiology, and endocrinology, creating strategic opportunities to build KOL networks focused on early cardiovascular disease prevention that include pediatric hypertension as a core theme. Companies with RAAS portfolio assets should monitor guideline evolution for pediatric hypertension, as the growing evidence base for CKM syndrome in children may drive expanded treatment indications and new comparative effectiveness research needs.

High systolic blood pressure and stroke: evidence from the NHANES 1999-2023 and global burden of disease 2021.

Revue : Experimental biology and medicine (Maywood, N.J.) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-08

Resultats principaux : This study integrated Global Burden of Disease (GBD) 2021 data with individual-level NHANES data (1999-2023) to characterize the relationship between high systolic blood pressure (HSBP) and stroke burden in the United States using a multidimensional epidemiological approach. GBD 2021 analysis confirmed HSBP as the leading risk factor for stroke burden in the US in 2021, with an increasing attributable burden since 2010, disproportionately affecting the elderly and women. At the individual level, NHANES multivariable logistic regression showed that HSBP significantly increased stroke risk (fully adjusted OR = 1.33, 95% CI: 1.17-1.51), and elevated SBP was also associated with increased all-cause mortality in stroke survivors (HR = 1.01). A novel U-shaped dose-response relationship was identified, with stroke risk decreasing below an SBP threshold of 100 mmHg but increasing sharply above this level, underscoring the importance of precise blood pressure target selection. Key limitations include the cross-sectional design of NHANES data, which precludes causal inference, and potential residual confounding despite multivariable adjustment.

Implications Medical Affairs : Confirmation of HSBP as the leading modifiable risk factor for the increasing stroke burden in the US provides compelling epidemiological justification for intensified blood pressure control targets and directly supports guideline discussions around intensive SBP lowering strategies, particularly in elderly women who bear disproportionate burden. Medical Affairs teams can leverage this multi-database evidence to reinforce the stroke prevention positioning of antihypertensive agents, particularly those with robust randomized trial data on stroke outcomes, in both scientific publications and KOL engagement programs. The U-shaped dose-response relationship also creates a scientifically rich discussion point for engagement with hypertension guideline committee members around optimal SBP targets, an area of ongoing debate that Medical Affairs-supported educational symposia could help advance.

Oral Health and Cardiovascular Disease Among Hypertensive Medicated Adults With Uncontrolled Blood Pressure.

Revue : Journal of clinical hypertension (Greenwich, Conn.) | Tier : Tier 3 | IF estime : ~3-5

Date de publication : 2026-06

Resultats principaux : This prospective cohort study followed 5,484 middle-aged and older hypertensive patients on antihypertensive medication and free of prior cardiovascular disease (mean age 67.5 +/- 8.0 years) for a mean of 9.9 +/- 2.7 years to examine associations between oral health status and incident cardiovascular disease. Cox proportional hazards analyses stratified by blood pressure control status revealed that in the poorly controlled BP group (SBP >= 130 mmHg or DBP >= 80 mmHg), tooth loss and non-use of dentures were significantly associated with increased CVD risk, with hazard ratios of 1.79 (95% CI: 1.28-2.48) for 10-19 remaining teeth and 2.17 (95% CI: 1.30-3.64) for 0-9 teeth without denture use compared to >=20 teeth. A total of 567 individuals (10.3%) experienced CVD events over follow-up, and the interaction between BP control status and oral health was statistically significant (p for interaction = 0.016). These findings demonstrate that the oral health-CVD relationship is specifically amplified in patients with uncontrolled hypertension, confirming a meaningful effect modification by BP control status. Limitations include the observational design with potential residual confounding from unmeasured dietary and socioeconomic factors, and limited generalizability beyond the predominantly Japanese cohort.

Implications Medical Affairs : This large prospective study establishes oral health as an independent cardiovascular risk modifier specifically in uncontrolled hypertensive patients, providing novel evidence that integrated oral-cardiovascular care programs could reduce CVD event rates in patients not at blood pressure target, reinforcing the clinical and public health argument for optimal BP control. For Medical Affairs, this finding strengthens the broader cardiovascular risk messaging around antihypertensive treatment intensification by demonstrating that systemic risk accumulates through multiple pathways in inadequately treated patients. KOL collaboration with dental medicine and preventive cardiology societies to advance interdisciplinary care models for hypertensive patients represents an area where Medical Affairs-supported educational programs can add distinct scientific value and reach diverse prescriber audiences.

Systolic Blood Pressure Burden: A Novel Metric for Predicting Cardiovascular Outcomes in High Cardiovascular Risk Patients - Insights from the SPRINT Study.

Revue : Pulse (Basel, Switzerland) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-04-16

Resultats principaux : This post hoc analysis of the landmark SPRINT trial enrolled 9,017 high-risk, nondiabetic participants (median age 67.0 [61.0-76.0] years; 64.7% men) to develop and validate a novel metric termed SBP burden, defined as the proportion of over-target time multiplied by the proportionate SBP area under the curve during that period, calculated from months 0-6 using a target of 130 mmHg. Over a median follow-up of 3.89 years, 568 major adverse cardiovascular events (MACEs) occurred, including cardiovascular death, myocardial infarction, stroke, and heart failure hospitalization. SBP burden showed an independent linear association with MACEs after adjusting for traditional risk covariables (HR = 1.17, 95% CI: 1.09-1.26, p < 0.01) and remained significant even after further adjustment for SBP standard deviation and average real variability. In head-to-head comparison with five other SBP metrics (mean SBP, SD, ARV, AUC, and time-in-target-range), SBP burden achieved the greatest improvement in discrimination and reclassification (NRI = 0.12 [0.03-0.22]; IDI = 0.0032 [0.0006-0.0076]) and ranked first in feature importance analysis using both relative informativeness and LASSO ranking. The primary limitation is the post hoc design and restriction to high-risk nondiabetic patients, limiting generalizability to diabetic or lower-risk populations.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study introduces a compelling new SBP monitoring metric that outperforms all existing measures for cardiovascular risk prediction in the SPRINT cohort, with important implications for clinical guidelines that currently rely on mean SBP or time-in-range metrics, and Medical Affairs teams should monitor its potential adoption into international hypertension management guidelines. If SBP burden becomes guideline-endorsed, it would create new clinical data needs that could differentiate antihypertensive products demonstrating superior SBP burden reduction in future trials, representing a first-mover advantage for companies that invest in generating this endpoint. This novel metric also serves as a basis for high-impact scientific exchange with hypertension guideline committee members and biostatistics-oriented KOLs, and may represent a relevant secondary endpoint in upcoming phase III trials targeting high-cardiovascular-risk hypertensive patients.

AGTR1 rs5186 polymorphism and inflammatory biomarkers are associated with cardiovascular disease risk in an admixed Mexican population.

Revue : Scientific reports | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-24

Resultats principaux : This case-control study enrolled 356 participants from Nayarit, Mexico, classified into three groups: cardiovascular disease (CVD, n=118), hypertension without CVD (HTN, n=119), and healthy controls (n=119), with genotyping of the AGTR1 rs5186 (A1166C) polymorphism performed via real-time PCR and logistic regression models adjusted for sociodemographic, clinical, and lifestyle variables. The A1166C genotype was independently associated with CVD risk (OR = 3.42, 95% CI: 1.16-10.08), representing a more than threefold elevated risk in this genetically admixed Mexican population. Elevated serum amyloid A (OR = 1.01, 95% CI: 1.01-1.02) and homocysteine levels (OR = 3.20, 95% CI: 1.93-5.33) emerged as significant independent predictors of CVD alongside the genetic polymorphism, and these associations persisted after adjusting for potential confounders. The study represents one of the first investigations of this AGTR1 variant in a genetically admixed Latin American population, highlighting the importance of population-specific genetic risk assessment beyond European-origin cohorts. Key limitations include the case-control design, relatively limited statistical power due to the sample size of 356 participants, and the absence of comprehensive medication use and dietary data.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings highlight differential genetic susceptibility to hypertension-related cardiovascular disease in admixed Latin American populations, supporting the emerging field of pharmacogenomics in hypertension management and the need for population-specific preventive strategies that go beyond European-derived risk models. Medical Affairs teams with global or pan-Latin American antihypertensive portfolios should take note of the growing evidence linking AGTR1 polymorphisms to differential response to angiotensin receptor blockers, creating potential opportunities for precision medicine messaging in markets with large admixed populations. KOL engagement should focus on Latin American cardiovascular genetics researchers and preventive cardiology societies, particularly to advocate for genetic profiling alongside inflammatory biomarker screening in CVD risk stratification programs in the region.

Date de publication : 2026-06-24

Resultats principaux : This narrative review synthesizes epidemiological and experimental evidence on the cardiovascular effects of traffic-related environmental noise in Europe, identifying it as a widespread and clinically relevant risk factor for a substantial burden of cardiovascular disease. Epidemiological studies consistently demonstrate dose-dependent associations between long-term traffic noise exposure and increased risks of arterial hypertension, ischemic heart disease, myocardial infarction, heart failure, and arrhythmias, with these associations persisting after adjustment for co-exposure to air pollution. The authors advocate for stronger consideration of traffic noise as a modifiable risk factor in cardiovascular disease prevention strategies, though the review's narrative methodology limits systematic evidence synthesis across heterogeneous study designs.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review reinforces the mechanistic overlap between environmental noise-induced stress pathways and core hypertension pathophysiology, supporting a broader environmental risk factor narrative that Medical Affairs teams can integrate into hypertension prevention and risk stratification messaging beyond traditional lifestyle factors such as diet and physical activity. Revue : Frontiers in cardiovascular medicine | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-08

Resultats principaux : This 10-year prospective rural cohort study from Tianjin, China, included 3,924 low-income participants with systematic metabolic and anthropometric assessments, recording 1,024 all-cause mortality cases over a median follow-up of 8.82 years. In hypertensive participants, the Atherogenic Index of Plasma (AIP) and its body composition-adjusted variants were significantly negatively associated with all-cause mortality (all p < 0.001), with AIP-WHtR showing the strongest protective association (HR = 0.21, 95% CI: 0.18-0.25 per one-unit increase), and AIP alone associated with a 57% reduction in mortality risk (HR = 0.43, 95% CI: 0.39-0.47). Key limitations include population restriction to rural low-income Chinese adults, limiting generalizability, and potential for reverse causation given the observational design.

Implications Medical Affairs : The blood-pressure-stratified differential in AIP-mortality associations highlights the critical need to account for hypertension status when interpreting lipid-based cardiovascular risk metrics, which has important implications for how Medical Affairs teams frame clinical evidence around cardiometabolic risk prediction in heterogeneous patient populations.
