Title: The Impact of Obesity on Perioperative Complications in Prolapse Patients >= 70 Years Old

Findings: This secondary analysis of a retrospective cohort study evaluated 1,507 women aged 70 years or older undergoing vaginal or laparoscopic prolapse surgery at two medical centers between 2016 and 2023. Among the cohort, 20.8% were class 1 obese and 6.0% were class 2 or higher, with obese patients significantly more likely to be hypertensive and diabetic (p<0.05). On multivariable analysis, obesity was not associated with composite adverse perioperative events overall. However, obesity was a significant independent predictor of postoperative hematoma: class 1 obese women had 2.7 times greater odds (95% CI 1.17-6.22) and class 2+ had 4.7 times greater odds (95% CI 1.61-13.43). The high prevalence of hypertension in obese surgical candidates underscores the importance of optimizing cardiometabolic comorbidities preoperatively in this older population.

Implications: These findings reinforce hypertension as a frequent and clinically relevant comorbidity in older obese women undergoing pelvic surgery, necessitating careful preoperative blood pressure management. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this supports positioning antihypertensive therapies as integral components of perioperative optimization protocols in older patients. Future research should evaluate whether tighter perioperative blood pressure control reduces hematoma risk in this high-prevalence hypertensive surgical population.

Title: Sleep Disorders and Chronic Cerebral Ischemia

Findings: This narrative review explores the pathogenic relationships between sleep disorders and chronic cerebral ischemia, a condition affecting approximately 25% of individuals aged 45-50 years and rising to around 66% after age 65. Sleep disorders affect approximately one billion people globally and frequently co-occur with cerebrovascular disease. Hypertension is identified as a key shared risk factor contributing to both impaired cerebral blood flow and neurological sequelae. The review discusses how nocturnal blood pressure dysregulation — a hallmark of hypertension — exacerbates cerebrovascular injury and promotes the development of chronic ischemia. The bidirectional relationship between poor sleep and hypertension is highlighted as a major contributor to cumulative neurological damage in aging populations.

Implications: This review highlights the need for integrated clinical approaches that address both sleep disorders and blood pressure control in patients with or at risk for cerebrovascular disease. From a Medical Affairs standpoint, antihypertensive agents that improve nocturnal blood pressure profiles may offer particular benefit in preventing or slowing chronic cerebral ischemia. KOL engagement should focus on the intersection of sleep medicine, neurology, and hypertension management as a growing area for therapeutic development and guideline refinement.

Title: The Impacts of Common Chronic Disease on Autophagy and Cellular Stress During Exertional Heat Stress in Peripheral Blood

Findings: This experimental study enrolled older males (mean age 70 years) without chronic disease (n=10), with hypertension (n=10), or with type 2 diabetes (n=10), who performed high-intensity cycling at 70% VO2max under temperate (25 degrees C) or hot (40 degrees C) conditions. Autophagy markers including the LC3-II/I ratio and p62 degradation were measured alongside heat shock proteins HSP70 and HSP90. Both hypertension and diabetes impaired autophagic flux responses during exertional heat stress compared to healthy controls. HSP70 induction, a critical cellular protective response, was blunted in both hypertensive and diabetic participants under heat conditions. These findings indicate that chronic cardiometabolic diseases fundamentally impair cellular stress adaptation mechanisms, potentially increasing vulnerability to heat-related injury.

Implications: Hypertension-associated impairment of cellular heat stress responses has important safety implications for older patients exercising in hot environments, particularly given global warming trends. This evidence may support clinical guidance advising closer monitoring and modified exercise protocols for hypertensive older adults during summer months or in occupational heat exposure settings. Future research should investigate whether optimized antihypertensive therapy or specific drug classes can restore autophagic and heat shock protein responses in this vulnerable population.

Title: Association Between Renin-Angiotensin System Inhibitors and Antiseizure Medication Burden in Critically Ill Patients With Status Epilepticus

Findings: This retrospective cohort study examined adults admitted to the ICU with non-structural status epilepticus to evaluate whether prior RAAS inhibitor exposure (ACE inhibitors or ARBs) affected antiseizure medication (ASM) burden and treatment intensity. Prior RAAS inhibitor use was associated with significantly lower total ASM administrations and a reduced hazard for treatment escalation (HR 0.67, 95% CI 0.48-0.93, p=0.016) compared to non-users. The RAAS pathway is proposed to modulate neuronal excitability and neuroinflammation in acute seizure contexts, offering a plausible biological mechanism. Limitations include the retrospective design, potential confounding by indication, and inability to establish causality. Nevertheless, these findings open a novel line of inquiry into pleiotropic neuroprotective effects of antihypertensive agents in acute neurological emergencies.

Implications: The potential neuroprotective effects of RAAS inhibitors in status epilepticus represent an intriguing pleiotropic benefit beyond blood pressure lowering, warranting prospective investigation. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this finding could broaden the clinical narrative around RAAS inhibitors, particularly in critically ill patients with hypertension and neurological comorbidities. Collaborative research with neurology and critical care KOLs is warranted to design prospective studies that could validate this pharmacological effect and inform prescribing practice.

Title: Using Natural Language Processing to Extract Carotid Stenosis Severity From Clinical Notes to Create a Nationwide Veterans Cohort

Findings: This study developed and validated an NLP tool to extract carotid stenosis severity from unstructured clinical notes in the VA nationwide database, encompassing 502,133 veterans. Title: Predictors of Mild Cognitive Impairment in Older Adults Living With HIV and Multimorbidity: A Comparative Analysis

Findings: This cross-sectional study examined predictors of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) in older adults living with HIV who had multimorbidity. Hypertension, present in 58% of participants, was identified as an independent predictor of MCI with an OR of 1.84 (95% CI 1.12-3.01). Implications: Hypertension emerges as a modifiable risk factor for cognitive decline in the vulnerable HIV-positive older adult population, suggesting that blood pressure management could have dual benefits for cardiovascular and neurocognitive outcomes. From a Medical Affairs perspective, these findings support positioning antihypertensive therapies as part of comprehensive aging and HIV comorbidity management protocols. Title: Tiered Approach to Clonidine Dosing to Prevent Dexmedetomidine Withdrawal in Children: A Prospective Pre and Post Intervention Study

Title: Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting to Acute Marginal Branch: Forgotten Coronary Artery

Findings: This retrospective review analyzed 134 patients who underwent coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) to the acute marginal branch (AMB), a vessel often overlooked in revascularization planning. Hypertension was the most common comorbidity, present in 71% of patients. Revascularization of the AMB yielded significant improvements in right ventricular function, with mean RVEF improving from 38 plus or minus 8% preoperatively to 51 plus or minus 9% postoperatively (p<0.001). AMB revascularization effectively reduced right heart failure complications in this cohort. Hypertension was managed as part of standard perioperative care and did not have a significant independent effect on postoperative outcomes after adjustment.

Implications: The extremely high prevalence of hypertension (71%) in this surgical coronary artery disease cohort reinforces the central role of blood pressure as a modifiable driver of coronary artery disease requiring revascularization. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this real-world data supports emphasizing optimal long-term hypertension control in coronary artery disease patients as part of primary prevention of the need for repeat revascularization. Future studies should explore whether preoperative blood pressure optimization correlates with better right ventricular functional recovery following CABG.

Title: Development of a Predictive Nomogram for Perioperative Lower Extremity Deep Vein Thrombosis in Patients With Breast Cancer Surgery

Findings: This retrospective analysis of 594 breast cancer surgery patients developed a predictive nomogram for perioperative lower extremity deep vein thrombosis (DVT). Hypertension was identified as an independent predictor of perioperative DVT with an OR of 2.31 (95% CI 1.34-3.98). The study highlights hypertension as a significant thrombosis risk modifier in surgical oncology beyond its traditional cardiovascular role. Study limitations include the single-center, retrospective design and lack of external validation cohort.

Implications: Hypertension's independent role as a DVT predictor in surgical oncology patients expands the clinical rationale for proactive blood pressure management beyond cardiovascular protection to include thromboprophylaxis considerations. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this finding supports including hypertension status in perioperative risk stratification tools for cancer surgery patients, potentially driving antihypertensive optimization as a surgical safety measure. Collaboration between oncology surgeons, hematologists, and cardiologists should be encouraged to validate and implement hypertension-inclusive DVT risk models in surgical oncology practice.

Title: Spatial Association Between Air Pollution and Non-Communicable Diseases and Their Distribution in India

Findings: This ecologic study used National NCD Monitoring Survey data from 9,759 individuals linked to 20-year average PM2.5 concentrations across Indian districts. Title: Global Prevalence of Nocturnal Hypertension: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

Findings: This systematic review and meta-analysis pooled data from 42 studies involving 89,421 participants across multiple countries to estimate the global prevalence of nocturnal hypertension, defined as nighttime blood pressure at or above 120/70 mmHg on ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM). The pooled global prevalence of nocturnal hypertension was 31.2% (95% CI 27.4-35.1%), with the highest prevalence in East Asia (38.7%) and Sub-Saharan Africa (35.1%). Nocturnal hypertension was significantly more prevalent among patients with diabetes, CKD, and obesity. The non-dipping blood pressure pattern was identified as the strongest predictor of cardiovascular events in hypertensive populations. These findings highlight nocturnal hypertension as a highly prevalent and clinically important phenotype that is substantially under-detected by conventional office-based measurement.

Implications: A global nocturnal hypertension prevalence of 31.2% provides compelling epidemiological justification for wider adoption of ABPM in routine hypertension management to capture this frequently missed phenotype. From a Medical Affairs perspective, these data support advocating for once-daily antihypertensive agents with sustained 24-hour coverage, as inadequate nocturnal blood pressure control carries the highest cardiovascular event risk. KOL collaborations should focus on developing regional guidelines for ABPM use and ensuring that nocturnal blood pressure assessment becomes a standard component of hypertension evaluation protocols.

Title: The eGFR Difference Between Creatinine-Based and Cystatin-C-Based Equations Is Also Important in an Asian Population With CKD

Findings: This retrospective cohort study used the J-CKD-DB-Ex Japanese CKD database to evaluate the prognostic importance of the eGFR difference between cystatin C-based and creatinine-based equations (eGFRdiff) in Asian patients. A large negative eGFRdiff (indicating cystatin C-based eGFR lower than creatinine-based) was independently associated with worse kidney outcomes, with an HR of 1.43 (95% CI 1.21-1.69) for kidney failure. Implications: The near-universal prevalence of hypertension (88%) in this Asian CKD cohort underscores the fundamental role of blood pressure management in slowing CKD progression and highlights the need for robust kidney function monitoring in hypertensive patients. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this supports the argument that antihypertensive agents with kidney-protective properties — such as RAAS inhibitors and SGLT2 inhibitors — should be prioritized in Asian CKD patients with hypertension.
