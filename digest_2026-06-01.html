Prediction of low birth weight using machine learning-based analysis of environmental and maternal risk factors: insights from the Korean CHildren's ENvironmental health Study (Ko-CHENS).

Revue : Environmental Research | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-05-30

Resultats principaux : This nationwide prospective birth cohort study (Ko-CHENS) enrolled 4,893 Korean mother-infant pairs to develop machine learning (ML) models predicting low birth weight (LBW), a condition well-established as a downstream risk factor for hypertension and metabolic disease in later life. Nine ML algorithms including linear models and tree-based models were trained on 47 variables capturing maternal characteristics alongside environmental exposures; random forest achieved the highest discriminative performance with an AU-ROC of 0.94 and an AU-PRC of 0.768. Feature importance analysis identified gestational age as the most influential predictor of LBW, with environmental factors ranking prominently among the secondary predictors. Trimester-specific analyses demonstrated statistically significant positive associations between third-trimester exposure to nitrogen dioxide (OR 1.81, 95% CI 1.15-2.84) and fine particulate matter PM2.5 (OR 1.49, 95% CI 1.03-2.17) and LBW risk. Study limitations include the observational design precluding causal attribution, reliance on regional environmental monitoring rather than individual-level exposure measurement, and restricted generalizability beyond the Korean context.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings reinforce the developmental origins of health and disease (DOHaD) framework, providing quantitative evidence that prenatal air pollution exposure substantially elevates LBW risk — a condition that creates a phenotype predisposed to hypertension and cardiovascular disease in adulthood. Medical Affairs teams with antihypertensive portfolios should monitor this convergence of perinatal environmental epidemiology and long-term cardiometabolic risk, as it supports the scientific rationale for early intervention narratives targeting at-risk birth cohorts. Engaging KOLs in environmental medicine, perinatal epidemiology, and preventive cardiology around the DOHaD pathway could strengthen publication strategies and position the company as a thought leader in the upstream prevention of hypertension.

Heat exposure and cardiovascular risk factors among migrant and seasonal farmworkers in the United States: data from the National Agricultural Workers Survey (1999-2020).

Revue : Journal of Exposure Science & Environmental Epidemiology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-05-30

Resultats principaux : This repeated cross-sectional study analyzed data from 44,388 U.S. farmworkers surveyed through the National Agricultural Workers Survey (NAWS) over a 21-year period (1999-2020), combining individual health data with publicly available regional temperature datasets to characterize heat exposure and cardiovascular risk factor prevalence in this occupationally vulnerable population. The workforce was predominantly Hispanic (81%), male (73%), economically disadvantaged (33% below poverty level), and aging over the study period. Joinpoint regression analyses documented overall increasing trends in seasonal temperatures across all six NAWS regions, with all regions recording days exceeding absolute heat thresholds of 32.2 to 40.6 degrees Celsius. Prevalence of high blood pressure, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease generally increased over the 21-year surveillance period, with some diminishing trends for diabetes and high blood pressure noted after 2017, possibly reflecting improved awareness and care access. Key limitations include the repeated cross-sectional design which precludes causal inference, reliance on self-reported health conditions, regional rather than individual-level temperature exposure estimates, and likely underrepresentation of undocumented farmworkers who may face greater health disparities.

Implications Medical Affairs : This large-scale surveillance study establishes a clear epidemiological signal that rising environmental heat is co-occurring with an increasing burden of hypertension and cardiovascular risk factors in a medically underserved and racially diverse workforce, strengthening the case for targeted public health interventions in occupational settings. Medical Affairs teams should recognize farmworker and Hispanic populations as underserved hypertension communities where disease management gaps are substantial and where patient support programs or real-world evidence initiatives could generate differentiated value. Partnering with occupational health KOLs and community health center networks serving migrant workers could support awareness activities and evidence generation for antihypertensive therapies in this high-risk, under-treated population.

Nonlinear association between serum selenium and all-cause mortality in U.S. adults with NAFLD: a cohort study.

Revue : Scandinavian Journal of Gastroenterology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-05-30

Resultats principaux : This cohort study leveraged NHANES 2017-2018 data to evaluate the relationship between serum selenium concentrations and all-cause mortality in 1,787 U.S. adults with non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), a metabolic condition frequently comorbid with hypertension and cardiovascular disease. Serum selenium was categorized into quartiles (Q1-Q4: 104.73-376.80 micrograms per liter), and Cox proportional hazards models were applied with full adjustments for demographics, lifestyle factors, comorbidities including hypertension, and biomarkers. Multivariable analysis revealed that participants in the highest selenium quartile (Q4: 204.39-376.80 micrograms per liter) had a 64% lower all-cause mortality risk compared to the lowest quartile (HR = 0.36, 95% CI 0.13-0.99), a statistically significant result. Restricted cubic spline analysis confirmed a nonlinear dose-response relationship with mortality risk plateauing at serum selenium levels above approximately 200 micrograms per liter, suggesting a threshold rather than linear protective effect. Subgroup analyses indicated amplified protective associations in older adults aged 60 and above and in hypertensive individuals, though formal interaction tests were statistically nonsignificant, warranting caution in interpreting these differential effects.

Implications Medical Affairs : The identification of hypertensive NAFLD patients as a potential subgroup deriving amplified mortality benefit from adequate selenium status highlights the complexity of managing cardiometabolic comorbidities and points to micronutrient status as an underexplored modulator of outcomes in hypertensive patients with liver disease. Medical Affairs teams with portfolios addressing hypertension in metabolically complex patients should track research at the intersection of nutritional status, hepatic dysfunction, and cardiovascular risk, as this population increasingly represents a distinct and challenging therapeutic segment. Engaging hepatology and cardiometabolic KOLs in scientific exchange on micronutrient-cardiovascular interactions in NAFLD could enhance the company's thought leadership at the growing nexus of hypertension, metabolic disease, and precision nutrition.
