New-onset Diabetes among Japanese University Enrollees: Role of Preceding Non-communicable Disorders.

Revue : Internal Medicine (Tokyo, Japan) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-05-23

Resultats principaux : This retrospective cohort study analyzed data from 3,242 newly enrolled university students aged 18 to 20 years at Gifu University in Japan over a 12-year period from April 2010 to March 2022, selecting individuals who were diabetes-free at admission and re-examined four years later at the time of their progression to a master's program. The primary endpoint was new-onset diabetes, defined as either probable diabetes (elevated fasting plasma glucose or HbA1c, or both) or definite diabetes (elevation of both markers simultaneously). At the 4-year follow-up, five participants (0.15%) met criteria for probable diabetes and three (0.09%) for definite diabetes, representing a low but clinically meaningful incidence in this young adult population. Logistic regression analysis revealed a striking crude odds ratio of 53.7 for hypertension predicting probable diabetes and 35.0 for definite diabetes, establishing hypertension as the most powerful antecedent non-communicable disorder associated with incident diabetes in this cohort. Crude odds ratios for obesity, liver dysfunction, and hyperuricemia were also calculated for probable diabetes, confirming that clustering of metabolic-syndrome components in young adults substantially amplifies diabetes risk. A key limitation of the study is the single-university setting with a predominantly young male student population, a small absolute number of diabetes cases (n=5 probable, n=3 definite), and the absence of dietary intake, physical activity, family history, and genetic data, which may limit statistical power and broader generalizability.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings reinforce the scientific rationale for integrating early blood pressure screening and antihypertensive management into young adult preventive care pathways, supporting guideline positioning that targets cardiovascular-metabolic risk clusters well before traditional age thresholds. For pharmaceutical companies with antihypertensive portfolios — particularly those including agents with established metabolic neutrality or benefit such as ARBs and ACE inhibitors — this study provides an opportunity to strengthen the product narrative around diabetes risk reduction as a secondary benefit of optimal blood pressure control in young high-risk individuals. From a KOL strategy and real-world evidence perspective, this Japanese academic cohort design opens actionable collaboration opportunities with university health centers and nephrology-endocrinology teams to co-develop longitudinal screening programs that could generate prospective evidence substantiating antihypertensive therapy as an upstream lever in diabetes prevention.
