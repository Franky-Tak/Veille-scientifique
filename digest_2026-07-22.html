Adverse pregnancy outcomes and long-term risk of peripheral artery disease: A cohort study.

Revue : PLoS Medicine | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-21

Resultats principaux : This national cohort study from Sweden enrolled 2,201,446 women with singleton deliveries between 1973 and 2015, with follow-up of up to 46 years for development of peripheral artery disease (PAD), accumulating 54 million person-years of observation. Cox regression models adjusted for maternal age, year of delivery, parity, socioeconomic factors, BMI, smoking, and prior history of hypertension, diabetes, or hyperlipidemia were used to compute hazard ratios for five major adverse pregnancy outcomes. Among the 13,211 women (0.6%) diagnosed with PAD at a median age of 62 years, all five adverse pregnancy outcomes were significantly associated with elevated long-term PAD risk: gestational diabetes carried the highest risk (HR 3.83; 95% CI 3.20-4.59; p<0.001), followed by small for gestational age (HR 1.74; 95% CI 1.65-1.83; p<0.001), other hypertensive disorders (HR 1.61; 95% CI 1.21-2.15; p=0.001), preterm delivery (HR 1.58; 95% CI 1.48-1.70; p<0.001), and preeclampsia (HR 1.28; 95% CI 1.20-1.37; p<0.001). Co-sibling analyses demonstrated that shared familial genetic or environmental factors did not substantially explain these associations, providing evidence of a direct relationship rather than confounding. Women with multiple adverse pregnancy outcomes had additive increases in PAD risk, and the study's main limitations include its restriction to Sweden, potential misclassification from diagnostic codes, and the observational design precluding definitive causal inference.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study underscores the need to integrate obstetric history — particularly hypertensive pregnancy disorders such as preeclampsia and gestational hypertension — into cardiovascular risk stratification frameworks, reinforcing the potential for guideline updates to formalize these as long-term PAD risk indicators alongside traditional factors. From a Medical Affairs standpoint, these findings strengthen the rationale for positioning antihypertensive therapies not only as acute blood pressure management tools but as cornerstone elements of a broader, long-term vascular prevention strategy targeting women with hypertensive pregnancy histories. This evidence provides a compelling foundation for KOL engagement strategies built around multidisciplinary care pathways linking obstetrics, cardiology, and vascular medicine, and supports advocacy for dedicated post-partum cardiovascular surveillance programs in women with adverse pregnancy outcomes.
