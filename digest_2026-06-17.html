Beyond PVR: dynamic physiologic assessment enhances prognostic evaluation in pulmonary hypertension associated with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease.

Revue : BMJ Open Respiratory Research | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-16

Resultats principaux : This single-center retrospective study evaluated 67 patients with pulmonary hypertension associated with COPD (PH-COPD) at a tertiary US referral center, who underwent right heart catheterization alongside cardiopulmonary exercise testing, echocardiography, and pulmonary function tests between 2019 and 2024. The primary composite endpoint of heart failure hospitalization or all-cause mortality occurred in 47.1% of patients over a median follow-up of 518 days (range 229–897 days). Key non-invasive dynamic physiologic variables significantly associated with adverse outcomes included lower gas exchange-derived pulmonary vascular capacitance (Gx; HR 0.997, 95% CI 0.995–0.999, p=0.013), lower oxygen uptake efficiency slope (HR 0.983, p=0.030), lower submaximal VO2 (HR 0.978, p=0.022), and lower end-tidal CO2 (HR 0.886, p=0.002). In the survival tree model, a Gx below 243 mL/mmHg provided the primary prognostic split, and patients with both Gx below 243 mL/mmHg and estimated right ventricular systolic pressure above 45 mmHg represented the highest-risk subgroup with a relative HR of 4.4. Adding pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) did not improve the prognostic performance of the model, challenging its traditional use as the primary prognostic metric in PH-COPD. The study is limited by its small single-center retrospective design, absence of repeat hemodynamic assessments, and limited generalizability to broader PH-COPD populations.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings suggest that dynamic exercise-derived markers could supplement or replace standard resting hemodynamics as prognostic tools in PH-COPD, potentially influencing upcoming guideline updates on risk stratification algorithms for this heterogeneous population. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this evidence supports educational initiatives around non-invasive functional assessment tools that may be used adjunctively with current pharmacological approaches, broadening the clinical narrative for treatments targeting PH patients beyond PVR-based endpoints alone. Engaging KOLs in pulmonary vascular disease to fund prospective validation studies of dynamic physiologic markers would be a high-priority strategic action to accelerate this paradigm shift and differentiate evidence-based product positioning.

SREBP gene polymorphisms and ambulatory blood pressure parameters in Chinese adolescents: a cross-sectional study.

Revue : European Journal of Pediatrics | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-16

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study enrolled 510 Chinese college students from a single university to investigate associations between SREBP1 (rs11868035) and SREBP2 (rs2228314) gene polymorphisms and 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure parameters. After adjustment for sex, age, weight status, ethnicity, socioeconomic factors, dietary habits, smoking, alcohol use, and hypertension history, SREBP1/rs11868035 was suggestively associated with higher 24-hour systolic blood pressure under the additive model (beta=1.39, p=0.045) and with daytime SBP under the recessive model (beta=1.97, p=0.026), though neither survived Bonferroni correction (threshold p less than 0.0167). SREBP2/rs2228314 was positively associated with daytime diastolic blood pressure under the recessive model (beta=2.44, p=0.033), also without significance after multiple testing correction. These exploratory findings collectively suggest potential genetic influences of lipid metabolism regulators on blood pressure regulation in youth, specifically through sterol regulatory mechanisms. Limitations include the exploratory cross-sectional design, small single-institution sample size, and the lack of replication after multiple comparison correction.

Implications Medical Affairs : These preliminary pharmacogenomic findings open a pathway for research into lipid metabolism-linked hypertension mechanisms beginning in adolescence, which may eventually inform patient stratification criteria for preventive antihypertensive therapies targeting genetically predisposed youth populations. From a Medical Affairs standpoint, these data reinforce the value of funding research into genetic risk stratification tools that could one day guide earlier pharmaceutical intervention in high-risk adolescent patients before overt hypertension develops. Engaging KOLs in pediatric hypertension and genetic epidemiology to co-design larger, well-powered pharmacogenomic studies would position the company at the forefront of precision medicine approaches in hypertension prevention.

Sex Differences in the Effect of Long-Term Blood Pressure Trajectories and Variability on the Atrial Fibrillation Risk in a Korean Cohort.

Revue : Journal of Korean Medical Science | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-15

Resultats principaux : This retrospective cohort analysis included 45,663 adults aged 40 years or older from the Korean National Health Insurance database, following participants for incident atrial fibrillation (AF) from 2012 to 2019. Systolic blood pressure (SBP) trajectories over the prior decade were classified as Class 1 (stable-low BP) or Class 2 (sustained-elevated BP), and visit-to-visit SBP variability was quantified using the Variability Independent of the Mean (VIM) index stratified into quartiles. Overall, sustained-elevated SBP trajectory was independently associated with a 42% higher AF risk (adjusted HR 1.42, p=0.015), and the highest VIM quartile conferred a 25% higher AF risk (adjusted HR 1.25, p=0.007), alongside established risk factors including male sex, older age, diabetes, congestive heart failure, and myocardial infarction. Sex-stratified analyses demonstrated that both sustained-elevated SBP trajectory (adjusted HR 1.47, p=0.025) and high SBP variability (adjusted HR 1.25, p=0.038) were significantly associated with AF incidence in men only, with no significant associations observed in women. These sex-specific findings suggest differential pathophysiological pathways linking blood pressure patterns to AF development between sexes. Study limitations include its retrospective design, potential AF misclassification in insurance claims, absence of echocardiographic data, and unmeasured genetic or structural cardiac confounders.

Implications Medical Affairs : The sex-specific impact of sustained elevated blood pressure and BP variability on AF incidence has direct implications for how upcoming antihypertensive guidelines may incorporate sex-stratified cardiovascular risk thresholds, particularly for AF prevention as a complementary cardiovascular endpoint to stroke and heart failure. This evidence supports Medical Affairs messaging around the value of achieving sustained and consistent blood pressure control—beyond merely reaching target levels—especially in male hypertensive patients, strengthening the clinical narrative for antihypertensives with favorable 24-hour coverage profiles. Supporting prospective studies with sex-stratified endpoints examining whether specific antihypertensive classes differentially reduce BP variability and AF incidence in men would provide differentiating evidence for product positioning and KOL engagement in cardiology.

Association between dietary thiamin, riboflavin and niacin intake and hypertension and increased pulse pressure: A cross-sectional study of the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey 1999-2023.

Revue : Medicine (Baltimore) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-12

Resultats principaux : This large cross-sectional analysis used NHANES data from 1999 to 2023, including 17,484 US adults aged 20 and older, to examine associations between total B vitamin intake (thiamin, riboflavin, and niacin from diet plus supplements) and hypertension (SBP 140 mmHg or higher or DBP 90 mmHg or higher) and increased pulse pressure (PP of 60 mmHg or higher). After full multivariable adjustment for demographic, socioeconomic, and clinical factors, the highest quartile of total thiamin (OR 1.17, 95% CI 1.04–1.31), riboflavin (OR 1.23, 95% CI 1.10–1.39), and niacin (OR 1.16, 95% CI 1.03–1.30) intakes were all positively associated with hypertension prevalence, with significant trend tests across all three. Conversely, riboflavin was inversely associated with increased pulse pressure (OR 0.84, 95% CI 0.75–0.94), suggesting a potential protective effect against arterial stiffness. Restricted cubic spline analysis revealed a J-shaped nonlinear association between niacin intake and hypertension (p-nonlinear=0.041), with the lowest odds observed at an intake of 30–40 mg/day and a suggestive inflection point at approximately 46.2 mg/day. Study limitations include the cross-sectional design precluding causal inference, self-reported dietary data, potential residual confounding, and reliance on dietary records from a heterogeneous multi-decade dataset.

Implications Medical Affairs : The counterintuitive positive association between high B vitamin intake and hypertension, alongside the J-shaped niacin-hypertension relationship, highlights a clinically important interaction between dietary supplementation patterns and antihypertensive therapy outcomes that could influence patient counseling recommendations in forthcoming nutritional guidelines for hypertension management. Medical Affairs teams can leverage these findings to reinforce educational messaging that emphasizes optimizing rather than simply maximizing micronutrient intakes as part of comprehensive hypertension management, complementing pharmacological treatment. Engaging nutrition science and preventive cardiology KOLs to support controlled intervention trials examining B vitamin dose effects on blood pressure would clarify causal directionality and inform dietary guidance that positions pharmacotherapy within a broader, evidence-based lifestyle management framework.

The Association Between Plasma Atherogenic Index Control Level and the Onset of Hypertension in Chinese Children and Adolescents.

Revue : Journal of Clinical Hypertension | Tier : Tier 3 | IF estime : 3–10

Date de publication : 2026-06

Resultats principaux : This prospective multi-center school-based study followed 12,800 Chinese students participating in a national obesity intervention program over one year, examining the association between atherogenic index of plasma (AIP; calculated as log[TG/HDL-C]) control trajectory and incident hypertension. AIP control was categorized into five trajectory classes based on baseline and one-year follow-up values, and logistic regression with restricted cubic splines assessed both linear and non-linear associations with hypertension risk. Among the 12,800 students, 830 (6.5%) developed hypertension within one year, with significantly elevated odds in Class 4 (poor AIP control: OR 1.75, 95% CI 1.31–2.33, p less than 0.001) and Class 5 (worst AIP control: OR 2.43, 95% CI 1.69–3.50, p less than 0.001) relative to optimal AIP control (Class 1). Non-linear associations were detected for the overall cohort and specifically in Class 4 (p for non-linear less than 0.05), while other trajectory classes showed linear dose-dependent relationships. Subgroup analyses by sex, age, and weight status were not reported, which limits understanding of heterogeneity in the association, and the observational school-based design precludes causal inference.

Implications Medical Affairs : The robust dose-dependent relationship between atherogenic dyslipidemia trajectory and incident hypertension in children underscores the value of AIP as an early risk screening tool in pediatric populations, which could influence preventive cardiology recommendations in forthcoming childhood hypertension guidelines toward lipid-based risk stratification. Medical Affairs teams can leverage these findings in educational outreach aimed at pediatric cardiologists and endocrinologists, emphasizing that combined management of atherogenic dyslipidemia may concurrently reduce hypertension onset risk in at-risk youth and supporting co-prescribing or lifestyle intervention strategies. Partnering with pediatric hypertension KOLs to design longer-term interventional studies testing lipid-lowering and antihypertensive combination strategies in high-AIP-risk youth would represent a compelling scientific investment with potential guideline-shaping impact.

Diastolic Morning Surge Improves Risk Stratification for Subclinical Left Ventricular Diastolic Dysfunction in Ambulatory-Defined Isolated Diastolic Hypertension.

Revue : Journal of Clinical Hypertension | Tier : Tier 3 | IF estime : 3–10

Date de publication : 2026-06

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study evaluated 737 untreated adults (353 with ambulatory-defined isolated diastolic hypertension [IDH] and 384 normotensive controls) who underwent standardized 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring and echocardiography to assess subclinical left ventricular diastolic dysfunction (LVDD), adjudicated using 2016 ASE/EACVI criteria. IDH, defined as 24-hour mean diastolic BP of 80 mmHg or higher with systolic BP below 130 mmHg, was independently associated with LVDD in adjusted analyses (OR 2.79, 95% CI 1.79–3.77). Incorporation of diastolic morning surge (DMS) attenuated but did not eliminate the IDH association and provided significant independent incremental value (OR 1.44 per 5 mmHg increase, 95% CI 1.09–1.90). Adding DMS to the base model improved the AUC from 0.76 to 0.81 (p=0.041), strengthened calibration, reduced overall prediction error, and provided greater net clinical benefit across intermediate risk thresholds on decision curve analysis. The primary limitations include the cross-sectional design, single-center setting introducing potential selection bias, use of echocardiographic surrogates rather than invasive hemodynamic measures of diastolic function, and lack of longitudinal follow-up to confirm LVDD progression.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings challenge the prevailing view of isolated diastolic hypertension as a relatively benign phenotype and suggest that ABPM protocols should systematically incorporate DMS measurement to reveal true subclinical myocardial remodeling risk, with direct implications for guideline revisions on how ambulatory monitoring is interpreted and acted upon clinically. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this evidence supports positioning antihypertensive therapies that effectively attenuate circadian blood pressure patterns—particularly morning surge—as specifically beneficial in IDH patients, differentiating products with superior 24-hour pharmacodynamic profiles and favorable morning BP reduction profiles. Establishing collaborations with echocardiography and hypertension KOLs for prospective interventional studies assessing whether DMS reduction translates to measurable LVDD regression would create high-value data supporting both guideline uptake and product differentiation strategies for the IDH market segment.

The Economic Impact of Cumulative Metabolic Comorbidity Burden in Schizophrenia: A Claims-Based Analysis of Medical Costs in the United States.

Revue : Neurology and Therapy | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-16

Resultats principaux : This retrospective cohort study used the STATinMED Real World Data Insights database (2018–2024) to assess the economic burden of cumulative metabolic comorbidities—obesity, hyperlipidemia, hypercholesterolemia, diabetes, and hypertension—in 122,248 US adults with schizophrenia. After propensity score matching to balance baseline characteristics, individuals with five metabolic comorbidities incurred mean annual medical costs of US$34,441 per person, compared to US$8,396 for those with no metabolic comorbidities, representing a greater than 4-fold cost increase. Outpatient, inpatient, and stay-related costs increased by 3.5-, 2.3-, and 1.7-fold respectively with each additional metabolic comorbidity burden level. Hypertension was one of the five tracked metabolic comorbidities, and the cost gradient was consistent across all subgroups regardless of the specific combination of conditions present. The study is limited by claims-data proxy diagnoses, potential coding inaccuracies, absence of clinical severity measures, and a retrospective design precluding causal interpretation of the comorbidity-cost relationship.

Implications Medical Affairs : These real-world economic data strongly demonstrate the compounding cost impact of metabolic comorbidities including hypertension in schizophrenia patients, supporting advocacy for earlier, more aggressive metabolic screening and management protocols that could influence psychiatry and cardiometabolic guidelines for co-management of this high-risk population. From a Medical Affairs standpoint, this health economics evidence strengthens the value proposition of antipsychotics with favorable metabolic profiles and antihypertensives that are compatible with antipsychotic co-administration, providing compelling arguments for formulary positioning and managed care negotiations targeting the psychiatric-cardiometabolic comorbidity space. Strategic engagement with psychiatry-cardiology KOLs and health economists to disseminate this burden-of-illness evidence through peer-reviewed publications and payer-facing materials would amplify awareness and help establish early intervention in metabolic management as both a clinical imperative and an economic priority.

Association of heavy metal exposure, C-reactive protein, and stroke risk in US adults: A cross-sectional analysis (NHANES 2005-2008).

Revue : Medicine (Baltimore) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-12

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional analysis included 8,297 US adults aged 20 years or older from the NHANES 2005–2008 survey, examining the independent and combined associations of blood lead, cadmium, mercury, and C-reactive protein (CRP) with self-reported stroke history, with hypertension as a key covariate. After weighted multivariate logistic regression adjusting for age, sex, race, BMI, smoking, alcohol use, hypertension, and diabetes, each unit increase in blood lead was associated with a 7% higher odds of stroke (OR 1.07, 95% CI 1.00–1.14), while each unit increase in CRP was associated with a 19% higher odds (OR 1.19, 95% CI 1.07–1.32); blood cadmium and mercury showed no significant independent associations. A 3D interaction plot revealed synergistically elevated stroke risk when both blood lead and CRP were simultaneously elevated, particularly in older adults. In age-stratified subgroup analyses, cadmium was specifically associated with stroke in the 40–60-year age group (OR 1.39), while mercury showed a negative correlation in the 60 and older group; CRP showed increasing associations in both middle-aged and older subgroups. The study is substantially limited by its cross-sectional design, self-reported stroke ascertainment, single time-point metal measurements, and inability to establish causality or temporal sequence between exposures and outcomes.

Implications Medical Affairs : The synergistic association of heavy metal exposure and systemic inflammation with stroke risk—both potentiated by hypertension—reinforces the importance of integrating environmental and inflammatory risk factor assessment into comprehensive cardiovascular prevention programs, which could influence occupational health guidelines and public health strategies for environmentally exposed populations. Medical Affairs teams can leverage this evidence to reinforce multifactorial cardiovascular risk messaging to hypertension specialists, emphasizing that optimal blood pressure control alone may be insufficient in environmentally or occupationally at-risk patients, supporting holistic patient management narratives that go beyond pharmacotherapy. Strategic collaboration with environmental medicine, neurology, and preventive cardiology KOLs to support prospective longitudinal studies examining the interplay of heavy metal burden, systemic inflammation, and blood pressure in stroke-prone populations would build a stronger scientific foundation for integrated cardiovascular risk management strategies.

Cardiovascular disease and risk management in patients with nephrotic syndrome.

Revue : Kidney Research and Clinical Practice | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2025-12-10

Resultats principaux : This narrative review synthesizes current evidence on cardiovascular risk and management in nephrotic syndrome (NS), defined by proteinuria of 3.5 g/24 hours or more, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia, and edema, highlighting the substantially elevated risk of both arterial and venous thromboembolic complications. Large cohort studies cited demonstrate up to a two-fold increase in myocardial infarction and stroke incidence and an eight-fold increase in venous thromboembolism in NS patients, with highest risk in membranous nephropathy. The heightened cardiovascular vulnerability arises from interrelated mechanisms including severe dyslipidemia, urinary loss of natural anticoagulants alongside elevated procoagulant factors and platelet activation, and neurohormonal activation of the renin-angiotensin system and sympathetic pathways that exacerbates hypertension and alters fluid homeostasis. Recommended management combines lifestyle modifications with tailored pharmacotherapy: lipid control, RAS inhibition to reduce blood pressure and proteinuria, anticoagulation for patients with serum albumin below 2.5 g/dL and high venous thromboembolism risk, and low-dose aspirin where arterial thrombosis prophylaxis is indicated. Limitations of the current evidence base include the absence of NS-specific cardiovascular risk prediction models, heterogeneity across available observational studies, and very limited clinical trial data on novel agents such as PCSK9 inhibitors and SGLT2 inhibitors in this population.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review identifies a critical evidence gap in cardiovascular risk prediction tools for NS patients, and highlights the dual role of RAS inhibition—central to hypertension management—in simultaneously controlling blood pressure and reducing proteinuria, which has direct implications for positioning RAS inhibitors and SGLT2 inhibitors in the underserved renal-cardiovascular patient population. Medical Affairs teams should consider supporting KOL-led research into NS-specific cardiovascular risk scoring tools and real-world evidence studies evaluating SGLT2 inhibitor and PCSK9 inhibitor outcomes in this population, creating meaningful opportunities for evidence generation that expands the clinical narrative of existing products into the cardiorenal space. Engaging nephrologists and cardiologists with expertise in cardiorenal syndrome through advisory boards and scientific congress symposia would help advance this area and identify concrete positioning opportunities for comprehensive cardiovascular protection strategies in patients with renal disease.
