Detection of previously undiagnosed conditions in midlife preventive health examinations.

Revue : Scientific Reports | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-03

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study enrolled 1,040 middle-aged adults (mean age 52.93 years, 61% male) attending preventive health examinations in Germany, applying clinical thresholds to detect suspected diagnoses of hypertension (BP ≥140/90 mmHg), hypercholesterolemia (LDL >116 mg/dL), and dysglycemia (HbA1c >6.0%). In total, 421 participants (41%) had at least one newly identified suspected diagnosis, with elevated LDL cholesterol being the most frequent finding (28%) and hypertension the second most prevalent at 9%. Male sex was independently associated with significantly higher odds of any suspected diagnosis (OR 1.42; 95% CI 1.09–1.85) and hypertension specifically (OR 2.24; 95% CI 1.31–3.99). Higher BMI was independently associated with hypertension (OR 1.15 per unit; 95% CI 1.08–1.21) and weakly associated with elevated cholesterol (OR 1.04; 95% CI 1.00–1.08). Having had a recent blood test was associated with lower odds of any new suspected finding (OR 0.65; 95% CI 0.50–0.84), highlighting the value of routine proactive testing, while family history of dyslipidemia predicted elevated cholesterol (OR 1.55; 95% CI 1.01–2.40); no consistent associations were found for self-reported lifestyle factors, and the study is limited by its cross-sectional design and single-site recruitment in Germany.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings reinforce the value of structured preventive screening programs for detecting occult hypertension in midlife adults, particularly men with elevated BMI who lack recent laboratory contact, aligning directly with guideline calls for opportunistic case-finding in primary care. From a Medical Affairs perspective, a 9% rate of newly detected hypertension in adults already in routine healthcare contact underscores a significant unmet diagnostic need, supporting positioning of antihypertensive products within preventive care pathways and population-based screening initiatives. Engaging primary care KOLs and health system stakeholders around structured detection programs is a concrete Medical Affairs strategy to improve patient identification, expand treatment-eligible populations, and generate real-world evidence on screening yield and clinical outcomes.

Tianjin Health and Chronic Disease Study (THCDS): study design and baseline characteristics of the cohort — cohort profile.

Revue : BMJ Open | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-03

Resultats principaux : The Tianjin Health and Chronic Disease Study (THCDS) is a prospective longitudinal cohort established in 2022 in Tianjin, China, enrolling 14,324 participants (mean age 53.48 years, 34.8% female) through comprehensive multi-organ phenotyping including anthropometrics, ECG, multi-organ Doppler ultrasound, chest and abdominal imaging, and biobank sampling of plasma, urine, and feces. Baseline data reveal marked sex differences in NCD prevalence: males had substantially higher rates of hypertension (46.60% vs. 34.23%), type 2 diabetes (17.39% vs. 9.54%), and gout (33.47% vs. 15.59%), while females showed higher prevalence of hyperlipidemia (18.26% vs. 15.47%), insomnia (10.00% vs. 5.42%), and cancer (3.23% vs. 1.75%; all p<0.05). A key exploratory finding showed combined elevation of fasting glucose and serum uric acid correlated significantly with non-alcoholic fatty liver disease even among participants without self-reported diabetes. Planned annual follow-ups over at least 15 years will capture longitudinal trajectories of risk factors and incident disease, with cognitive assessments (Montreal Cognitive Assessment) in participants over 60. The cross-sectional baseline design precludes causal inference, and urban hospital-based recruitment may limit generalizability to rural Chinese populations.

Implications Medical Affairs : The THCDS cohort provides a large, deeply phenotyped longitudinal database of hypertension and NCD comorbidity patterns in a rapidly urbanizing Chinese population, directly relevant to global burden of disease analyses and emerging market access strategies in one of the world's largest pharmaceutical markets. The high baseline hypertension prevalence exceeding 46% in males confirms a massive unmet treatment and control need in this demographic, reinforcing the positioning of antihypertensive drugs within primary care and cardiovascular prevention frameworks in China. Medical Affairs teams should monitor THCDS publications for emerging real-world evidence on treatment patterns, complication trajectories, and lifestyle intervention efficacy to inform KOL engagement, local regulatory submissions, and market shaping activities.

High Temperature, PM2.5, Greenspace and Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy: Exploring Interactive Effects during Pregnancy.

Revue : Environmental Research | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-02

Resultats principaux : This large cross-sectional study analyzed 48,160 pregnant women who delivered in Chongqing, China between January 2021 and December 2023, examining the interactive effects of ambient temperature, PM2.5 exposure, relative humidity, and green space on risk of hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP) using generalized linear mixed models with interaction terms. Elevated temperature significantly potentiated the PM2.5–HDP association, yielding an adjusted odds ratio (aOR) of 1.11 (95% CI: 1.02–1.20) for PM2.5 under high-temperature conditions; when stratified, the PM2.5 effect remained significant in both heat-exposed (aOR 1.10; 95% CI 1.07–1.15) and cold-exposed (aOR 1.08; 95% CI 1.02–1.13) groups. The first trimester was identified as the critical exposure window for PM2.5-related HDP risk, a key finding for timing preventive interventions during prenatal care. Green space and relative humidity further modified the PM2.5–HDP relationship in complex, synergistic ways, suggesting that urban environmental planning could mitigate pregnancy hypertension risk. Secondary analyses confirmed that the combined effect of multiple environmental stressors substantially exceeds individual exposure effects. The study is limited by cross-sectional design, ecological exposure assignment, and potential residual confounding by individual socioeconomic and behavioral factors.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study adds to the mounting evidence that environmental exposures — particularly the synergistic combination of heat and PM2.5 air pollution — are significant modifiable risk factors for hypertensive disorders of pregnancy, a group at elevated long-term cardiovascular risk, with direct relevance for guideline development on antihypertensive use in the prenatal and peripartum setting. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this supports expanding the clinical narrative around pregnancy hypertension beyond pharmacotherapy alone to include environmental risk factor counseling and public health advocacy, positioning a company's therapeutic portfolio within a broader integrated prenatal cardiovascular care narrative. Collaboration with obstetric and environmental health KOLs to generate further mechanistic and real-world evidence in this space could differentiate a company's Medical Affairs agenda in maternal cardiovascular health and support label extension discussions in reproductive-age women.

Ultrasound common hepatic artery denervation (CHA-D) as a novel neuromodulatory strategy for cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic syndrome: first-in-human experience.

Revue : Clinical Research in Cardiology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-03

Resultats principaux : This first-in-human report describes ultrasound-guided common hepatic artery denervation (CHA-D) performed in a single patient with recurrent uncontrolled hypertension refractory to prior renal denervation and maximal pharmacological therapy, with baseline mean 24-h BP of 179/88 mmHg and office BP 176/116 mmHg. The procedure was technically successful with no intraprocedural or follow-up hepatobiliary, pancreatic, or renal safety signals through 6 months. Mean 24-h BP decreased to 151/87 mmHg at 1 month and 139/83 mmHg at 6 months, corresponding to systolic reductions of 28 and 40 mmHg respectively, without any intensification of antihypertensive medication; office BP fell to 125/85 mmHg at 6 months. Remarkably, concurrent cardiometabolic benefits were observed: hepatic fat fraction declined from 16.2% to 11.5%, HbA1c from 6.2% to 5.9%, insulin requirements decreased, and body weight decreased by 8 kg, with improved patient-reported quality of life on SF-36 and EQ-5D-5L. The study is fundamentally limited by its single-case design (n=1) with no control group, and placebo effects and regression to the mean cannot be excluded.

Implications Medical Affairs : CHA-D represents a novel interventional neuromodulation approach targeting the hepatic sympathetic axis for resistant hypertension and cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic syndrome, extending the therapeutic landscape beyond renal denervation and representing a potentially disruptive convergent therapy that Medical Affairs teams in the cardiovascular-metabolic space must closely monitor. While this first-in-human case requires cautious interpretation, the magnitude of BP reduction combined with concurrent improvements in hepatic steatosis, glycemic control, and weight positions CHA-D as a compelling candidate for further prospective investigation in resistant hypertension, particularly in patients with overlapping cardiometabolic comorbidities. Medical Affairs should proactively map emerging KOLs in device-based neuromodulation for hypertension, consider supporting prospective registries or feasibility trials, and develop evidence frameworks addressing the overlap between resistant hypertension and cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic syndrome.

Pregnancy and Stroke Risk.

Revue : Continuum (Minneapolis, Minn.) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-03

Resultats principaux : This comprehensive narrative review covers the epidemiology, pathophysiology, acute management, and prevention of pregnancy-associated stroke, with a specific focus on hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP) as the primary modifiable risk factor, in the context of rising incidence driven by older maternal age and increasing cardiometabolic comorbidities. Stroke affects approximately 30 per 100,000 pregnancies, with highest risk during the peripartum and early postpartum periods; recent data highlight an increasing contribution from ischemic strokes and cerebral venous thrombosis alongside traditional hemorrhagic subtypes. Physiological contributors to pregnancy-related stroke include hypercoagulability, venous stasis, and vascular injury from hypertensive disease, with multiple pathways converging to increase cerebrovascular vulnerability. Current evidence now supports the safety of thrombolysis and endovascular thrombectomy in carefully selected pregnant patients, and prevention strategies with the strongest evidence include low-dose aspirin in high-risk women, tight blood pressure control, and preconception counseling for women with prior HDP or thrombophilia. The review acknowledges publication bias toward high-income settings and significant evidence gaps in LMIC populations, particularly regarding optimal blood pressure thresholds and antihypertensive drug choice during pregnancy.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review underscores that hypertensive disorders of pregnancy remain among the most clinically impactful modifiable risk factors for pregnancy-related stroke, reinforcing the urgent need for optimal antihypertensive management in pregnant women and validating the therapeutic importance of safe antihypertensive agents with regulatory approval for use in pregnancy. Medical Affairs teams should leverage this review to support HDP-focused educational programs targeting obstetricians, neurologists, and cardiologists, and to engage KOLs around the multidisciplinary intersection of hypertensive pregnancy, stroke prevention, and long-term cardiovascular risk. The review also highlights an evidence gap in LMIC settings around antihypertensive management during pregnancy that could form the basis of Medical Affairs-sponsored research initiatives aligned with a company's global access and health equity strategy.

Adverse Pregnancy Outcomes and Risk of Subarachnoid Hemorrhage: A 50-Year Population-Based Cohort Study in Sweden.

Revue : Stroke | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-03

Resultats principaux : This nationwide cohort study followed 1,785,088 primiparous Swedish women from first birth (1973–2014) through December 2023 — up to 50 years of follow-up — examining long-term risk of subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes (APOs) using Cox regression with sibling-pair analyses to control for shared familial and genetic confounding. Over the follow-up period, 42.6% of women experienced at least one APO and 5,751 (0.32%) developed SAH; hypertensive disorders of pregnancy conferred a hazard ratio of 1.58 (95% CI 1.41–1.77) for subsequent SAH, and placental abruption carried the highest risk (HR 1.62; 95% CI 1.29–2.04). Gestational diabetes was also associated with increased SAH risk (HR 1.40; 95% CI 1.04–1.90), and these associations were consistent in sibling analyses, substantially reducing the likelihood of familial confounding as the sole explanation. The excess SAH risk was greatest in the first years after delivery and attenuated over time, though it persisted for decades; increased risks of aortic aneurysm rupture or dissection after HDP (HRs 1.43–1.66) added further evidence of underlying arteriopathy. Limitations include reliance on register-based outcome ascertainment without clinical validation and potential diagnostic misclassification in earlier calendar periods.

Implications Medical Affairs : This landmark 50-year cohort definitively establishes HDP as a persistent long-term cerebrovascular and cardiovascular risk marker lasting decades beyond delivery, providing the strongest population-based epidemiological evidence to date for sustained antihypertensive surveillance and treatment in this post-pregnancy population. For Medical Affairs, this evidence powerfully supports the development of a post-pregnancy cardiovascular risk monitoring narrative that positions long-term antihypertensive therapy as a lifelong risk reduction strategy in women with prior HDP, a compelling framing for label extension discussions and health technology assessments. KOL strategy should target a multidisciplinary network — obstetricians, cardiologists, and neurologists — for co-development of integrated care pathways incorporating postpartum cardiovascular screening, and the 50-year dataset creates exceptional epidemiological foundations for guideline update submissions in Europe and globally.

Prevalence and Determinants of Cardiovascular Disease Risk Factors among Adults Attending Primary Health Care Centers in Makkah City, Saudi Arabia.

Revue : Saudi Journal of Medicine & Medical Sciences | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-04-28

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study included 38,301 adults attending primary health care centers of the Makkah Health Cluster, Saudi Arabia between 2021 and 2024, examining the prevalence and sociodemographic determinants of hypertension, diabetes, and obesity using multivariable logistic regression. Hypertension prevalence was 7%, diabetes 9%, and overweight/obesity 64% in this primary care-attending population, with older age and marital status (ever-married) as the strongest predictors of both hypertension (age OR 1.06 per year; 95% CI 1.06–1.07; ever-married OR 1.61; 95% CI 1.31–1.99) and diabetes (age OR 1.06; 95% CI 1.05–1.06; ever-married OR 2.04; 95% CI 1.67–2.50). Female sex was associated with higher odds of hypertension (OR 1.10; 95% CI 1.00–1.22) but lower odds of diabetes (OR 0.64; 95% CI 0.58–0.70), while higher education was a significant protective predictor for both conditions (hypertension OR 0.63; 95% CI 0.46–0.88; diabetes OR 0.82; 95% CI 0.71–0.96). Private employees had moderately higher odds of both hypertension (OR 1.24) and diabetes (OR 1.22) relative to other occupational categories, and Saudi nationals had greater odds of diabetes than non-nationals. Limitations include cross-sectional design, healthcare-seeking attendance bias, and reliance on physician-confirmed diagnoses without standardized screening protocols.

Implications Medical Affairs : The study reveals a substantial and likely underestimated burden of cardiometabolic risk factors in primary care-attending adults in Makkah — with educational attainment emerging as a key modifiable protective factor — data that directly inform the design of Medical Affairs disease awareness and patient education programs in the Gulf region. The relatively low hypertension detection rate (7%) in an already healthcare-attending population strongly suggests widespread underdiagnosis rather than truly low disease prevalence, confirming a commercially significant unmet need for improved detection and treatment uptake in the Middle East. Medical Affairs teams should leverage these findings to engage regional KOLs and Ministry of Health stakeholders around hypertension awareness campaigns aligned with Saudi Arabia Vision 2030 health transformation goals, positioning antihypertensive therapy within a national cardiovascular risk reduction agenda.

Qualitative study of barriers and facilitators to Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) on the Caribbean island of Montserrat.

Revue : BMJ Public Health | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-05-28

Resultats principaux : This qualitative study conducted semi-structured interviews with 16 adults diagnosed with hypertension in Montserrat, Caribbean, recruited through primary care providers, using purposive sampling to ensure representation across sex and birthplace, with interviews lasting 20–40 minutes analyzed using the Theoretical Domains Framework through deductive thematic analysis. Five themes emerged shaping DASH adherence: understanding of hypertension and DASH, food access, cooking and self-efficacy skills, cultural practices and family dynamics, and readiness to change. While participants recognized hypertension as urgent and expressed motivation to improve diet, structural barriers — particularly the cost, seasonal unavailability, and limited variety of DASH-recommended fruits, vegetables, and fish — were the most consistently cited constraints on adherence. Cooking and local food cultivation skills supported adherence, but food label literacy was uniformly low across participants, and strong cultural taste preferences for high-salt, high-fat traditional foods presented persistent barriers. Participants proposed practical interventions including structured nutrition education, financial or agricultural subsidy support, community food coordination, and peer support programs as priority enablers. The study is limited by its small qualitative sample (n=16), single-island setting, and risk of social desirability bias in interview responses.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study confirms that pharmacotherapy alone is insufficient for hypertension management in the Caribbean, and that culturally tailored, multi-level non-pharmacological support — including dietary education, food systems access improvements, and peer support — is essential for sustained blood pressure control, a finding that informs how antihypertensive therapy should be positioned within broader integrated care frameworks in small island developing states. For Medical Affairs, this evidence supports the development of patient support programs combining pharmacotherapy with culturally appropriate lifestyle tools, potentially through partnerships with community health organizations and primary care KOLs in underserved Caribbean and LMIC populations. The study also identifies a clearly articulated need for real-world evidence on DASH feasibility across diverse Caribbean communities that could anchor a company's Medical Affairs research agenda and disease awareness activities in this region.

Implementing a lifestyle toolkit in individuals with cardiometabolic multimorbidity: study protocol for a cluster randomised, type III effectiveness-implementation trial (SHRADDHA-CMM).

Revue : Trials | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-02

Resultats principaux : This paper describes the protocol of the SHRADDHA-CMM trial, a two-arm, non-blinded cluster randomised controlled trial (c-RCT) targeting 2,000 Indian adults with cardiometabolic multimorbidity (CMM, defined as two or more of diabetes mellitus, hypertension, stroke, or coronary heart disease) distributed across 18 clusters within the ENDIRA cohort in Ernakulam, Kerala, India. The SHRADDHA lifestyle toolkit integrates culturally tailored dietary guidance, structured physical activity, stress management techniques (including yoga and mindfulness), tobacco and alcohol cessation support, and medication adherence coaching, delivered by frontline health workers using a digital health platform aligned with the WHO-HEARTS framework. Primary outcomes include blood pressure, HbA1c, and body weight measured at 24 months, with statistical analysis using repeated-measures ANOVA and multilevel models on an intention-to-treat basis, and an intergenerational preventive dimension also incorporated. A minimum 15% reduction in systolic blood pressure in the intervention arm is the primary efficacy target based on sample size calculations. The non-blinded design is an inherent limitation for evaluating behavioral outcomes, and community-based loss to follow-up may reduce statistical power; the trial is registered on the Clinical Trial Registry of India (CTRI/2025/08/092529).

Implications Medical Affairs : The SHRADDHA-CMM trial represents a critical real-world test of whether a structured, WHO-aligned culturally tailored lifestyle intervention can meaningfully improve blood pressure control and metabolic outcomes in Indian patients with cardiometabolic multimorbidity — a population where pharmacotherapy adherence is systematically compromised without supporting behavioral infrastructure. For Medical Affairs, monitoring this trial's outcomes is essential for understanding how non-pharmacological approaches complement and potentiate antihypertensive therapy in South Asian populations with high multimorbidity burden, informing clinical narratives around combination lifestyle-pharmacotherapy approaches in emerging markets. Proactive engagement with SHRADDHA-CMM investigators offers a concrete Medical Affairs opportunity to co-develop companion real-world evidence studies or health economics analyses that could support guideline updates on multimorbidity management in India and comparable LMIC settings.
