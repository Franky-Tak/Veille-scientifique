Scaling up community-based non-communicable disease research into practice in Pokhara Metropolitan City of Nepal (SCALE-NCD) trial: study protocol for a cluster-randomized controlled trial.

Revue : Trials | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-03

Resultats principaux : This study presents the protocol for SCALE-NCD, an open-label, two-armed cluster-randomized controlled trial targeting adults with hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and/or tobacco smoking in Pokhara Metropolitan City, Nepal, the country's second-largest urban center, with 30 clusters randomized 1:1 to intervention or usual care. The intervention package comprises home-based monitoring, referral, and counseling for self-management of hypertension, diabetes, and tobacco smoking delivered by Female Community Health Volunteers (FCHVs), combined with weekly mobile phone messages to promote healthy lifestyle. Primary outcomes include change in systolic blood pressure (required sample size: 405 per arm), change in fasting plasma glucose (105 per arm), and smoking cessation (525 per arm), reflecting the multi-condition nature of the intervention. Importantly, FCHVs in Nepal are community health workers whose usual roles are restricted to maternal and child health, and this protocol extends their scope substantially to urban NCD primary care for the first time in a scaled setting. Key limitations include the open-label design, reliance on volunteer-based community workers with expanded training needs, and the urban context of Pokhara, which may limit generalizability to other LMIC urban environments with different health system infrastructure.

Implications Medical Affairs : This protocol is directly relevant to Medical Affairs strategy in LMICs, where hypertension and NCD co-morbidity burden is accelerating, and supports the positioning of antihypertensive therapies within integrated, community-delivered NCD management programs that align with global health system strengthening agendas. The multi-condition design mirroring real-world comorbidity patterns (hypertension, diabetes, tobacco) strengthens the case for combination pharmacological approaches and may generate implementation science data that can be leveraged in real-world evidence submissions and health technology assessments. Engaging the leading investigators from Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health and Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health represents a high-value KOL partnership opportunity, particularly for advancing collaborative research on task-sharing models and adherence support tools in South and Southeast Asian markets.
