Incidence and predictors of stroke among hypertension patients in Awi Zone public hospitals, Northwest Ethiopia: Retrospective follow-up study.

Revue : BMC Cardiovascular Disorders | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This hospital-based retrospective follow-up study enrolled 510 adult hypertensive patients from Awi Zone public hospitals in Northwest Ethiopia, followed from April 2016 to March 2023, using computer-generated simple random sampling and Cox proportional hazard regression. The overall incidence rate of stroke was 5.53 per 100 person-years (95% CI: 4.60, 6.65), reflecting a high stroke burden in this low-resource setting. Rural residence (AHR 1.88, 95% CI 1.13-3.12), family history of stroke (AHR 2.52, 95% CI 1.33-4.77), atrial fibrillation (AHR 3.10, 95% CI 1.62-5.90), total cholesterol ≥200 mg/dl (AHR 4.42, 95% CI 2.71-7.23), overweight (AHR 1.82, 95% CI 1.16-2.87), and LDL-cholesterol ≥100 mg/dl (AHR 1.77, 95% CI 1.11-2.83) were independent predictors of stroke. Elevated cholesterol was the strongest predictor, with a more than fourfold increase in risk, highlighting the compounding role of dyslipidemia in stroke risk among hypertensive patients in Africa. Key limitations include the retrospective single-region design and potential for incomplete medical records limiting generalizability across Ethiopia.

Implications Medical Affairs : These data from sub-Saharan Africa underscore a critical need to address guideline gaps in low-resource settings where cardiovascular risk factor management remains substantially undertreated, reinforcing the importance of multi-risk-factor intervention programs. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this finding supports advocacy for hypertension management programs in Africa as a strategic high-priority area, potentially informing KOL partnerships with academic centers operating in low-income settings. The identification of modifiable predictors such as dyslipidemia and overweight alongside hypertension provides a compelling case for combination cardiovascular risk management and polypharmacy approaches in underserved populations.

Prevalence and factors associated with uncontrolled systolic blood pressure among treated older adults in Sri Lanka: a nationwide multicenter cross-sectional study.

Revue : BMC Cardiovascular Disorders | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This nationwide multicenter cross-sectional study enrolled 1,339 adults aged ≥65 years receiving antihypertensive therapy for at least 6 months from 10 tertiary care hospitals representing all 9 provinces of Sri Lanka, with SBP measured using a standardized 3-reading protocol. Uncontrolled SBP (≥130 mmHg per contemporary guidelines) was present in 71.2% of treated older adults, revealing a major treatment gap; only 34.4% received dual antihypertensive therapy while 42.2% remained on monotherapy. In multivariable analysis, higher income (OR 1.96, 95% CI 1.20-3.20, p=0.007), low physical activity (OR 1.41, 95% CI 1.07-1.86, p=0.015), and higher neck circumference (OR 1.01, 95% CI 1.01-1.02, p=0.014) were independently associated with uncontrolled SBP. Uncontrolled SBP was significantly associated with major adverse cardiovascular events (OR 1.31, 95% CI 1.01-1.69, p=0.044), confirming the direct clinical consequences of inadequate BP control in this population. Limitations include the cross-sectional design preventing causal inference and potential selection bias from tertiary center recruitment.

Implications Medical Affairs : A 71% rate of uncontrolled blood pressure among treated elderly patients highlights a pervasive treatment gap and a substantial opportunity to position intensified antihypertensive therapy, including combination fixed-dose products, in South Asia. This data provides Medical Affairs with robust evidence to engage cardiovascular KOLs on the need for systematic treatment intensification algorithms and digital blood pressure monitoring tools in older populations. The association between uncontrolled SBP and MACE reinforces the cardiovascular outcome burden, supporting health-economic arguments for enhanced adherence programs and more aggressive upward titration strategies.

Cigarette smoking, e-cigarette use, and hypertension: a population-based cross-sectional study.

Revue : Journal of Human Hypertension | Tier : Tier 3 | IF estime : ~3.5

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This population-based cross-sectional study analyzed pooled data from 22,187 adults aged ≥16 years from the Scottish Health Survey (2017-2022), classifying participants into five groups: never users, former smokers, current cigarette-only smokers, exclusive e-cigarette users, and dual users. Overall hypertension prevalence was 26.5%, ranging from 19.0% among exclusive e-cigarette users to 33.5% among former smokers. Compared with never users, current exclusive e-cigarette use was associated with lower odds of hypertension (OR 0.77, 95% CI 0.63-0.93), while former smokers had higher odds (OR 1.10, 95% CI 1.02-1.20). Current cigarette-only smoking and dual use showed no significant association. In propensity score-matched analyses, the inverse association for e-cigarette use was attenuated and no longer statistically significant, suggesting that findings may partly reflect healthy switcher bias and residual confounding rather than a protective effect. The study is limited by cross-sectional design, self-reported hypertension diagnosis, and reliance on survey data.

Implications Medical Affairs : The finding that exclusive e-cigarette use is not associated with higher hypertension risk compared to conventional smoking adds nuance to the cardiovascular risk landscape of tobacco harm reduction, though healthy switcher bias requires cautious interpretation in communications. Medical Affairs teams should actively monitor this evolving evidence base on vaping-related cardiovascular risk to ensure patient education materials and risk communication tools remain aligned with the most current data. This type of population-level epidemiological evidence may influence future guideline revisions on tobacco harm reduction and hypertension, providing a framing opportunity to position antihypertensive therapies in conjunction with smoking cessation strategies.

Impact of a Telehealth Navigator Intervention on Hypertension Control Among Black Patients Served by Federally Qualified Health Centers.

Revue : Journal of General Internal Medicine | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This observational study using a staggered difference-in-differences design evaluated a federally qualified health center (FQHC) telehealth navigator intervention combining enhanced care coordination and remote patient monitoring (RPM) for Black patients with hypertension in Massachusetts. The intervention group comprised 5,175 person-months (mean age 60 years, 44.3% female) and the comparison group 328,950 person-months (mean age 57 years, 61% female). Enrollment in the intervention was associated with a 31.4 percentage point increase (95% CI 13.2-49.1, p=0.001) in the probability of having controlled blood pressure (<140/90 mmHg), representing a clinically and statistically significant effect. Event study analyses showed significant increases in primary care visits during the first 2 months of the intervention, though overall primary care engagement was unchanged. No changes were found in total visit frequency overall, suggesting that care quality improvements — rather than increased visit volume — drove BP control gains. Limitations include the observational design at a single FQHC network and potential selection bias toward more engaged patients.

Implications Medical Affairs : A 31 percentage point improvement in BP control through a telehealth navigator program provides compelling real-world evidence supporting the integration of digital health tools into hypertension management, particularly for underserved minority populations. Medical Affairs can leverage this data to advocate for hybrid care delivery models combining pharmacological therapy with technology-enabled adherence support, reinforcing a health equity narrative that resonates with payers and integrated health systems. Engaging primary care and health disparities KOLs around this evidence creates strategic opportunities to position pharmaceutical programs within broader patient support ecosystems addressing cardiovascular disparities.

Urban ambient air pollution and blood pressure in schoolchildren in Accra, Ghana.

Revue : Scientific Reports | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional analysis examined 919 schoolchildren aged 7-14 years from 90 elementary schools in Accra, Ghana, assessing associations between ambient PM2.5, black carbon (BC), and NO2 exposures and blood pressure estimated using spatiotemporal land-use regression models from 146 measurement sites. Notably, one-third of children had elevated blood pressure (≥90th percentile for age, sex, and height), signaling a substantial pediatric hypertensive burden. PM2.5 at homes and schools was 5-8 times the WHO annual guideline of 5 µg/m3, while NO2 exceeded the WHO guideline 11-fold at some locations. Contrary to the study hypothesis, none of the pollutants were positively associated with blood pressure; after covariate adjustment, associations were null or slightly inverse (e.g., a 10-unit increase in BC was associated with -4.61 mmHg SBP, 95% CI -9.59, 0.37 at homes). The study found no evidence of a pro-hypertensive effect of PM2.5, BC, or NO2 in this pediatric population, though the already high prevalence of elevated BP suggests other factors are dominant. Limitations include cross-sectional design, self-reported lifestyle covariates, and potential exposure misclassification.

Implications Medical Affairs : The strikingly high prevalence of elevated blood pressure (33%) in a pediatric population in Ghana represents a critical signal for the future cardiovascular disease burden in West Africa, warranting early intervention strategies beyond standard adult hypertension programs. While air pollution did not drive pediatric BP in this cohort, the finding underscores the multifactorial nature of hypertension risk in rapidly urbanizing sub-Saharan African settings and the need for comprehensive risk surveillance in children. Medical Affairs can use this evidence to support engagement with pediatric cardiology and preventive medicine KOLs, and to advocate for the inclusion of early-life hypertension prevention as a component of integrated cardiovascular disease strategies.

Non-linear U-shaped association between serum zinc concentration and the risk of elevated blood pressure in adults: A 9-year longitudinal study.

Revue : Journal of Trace Elements in Medicine and Biology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This prospective longitudinal study followed 2,117 normotensive adults from 2009-2011 to 2018-2022 to investigate the association between serum zinc (SZn) concentration and incident elevated blood pressure (SBP 120-129 mmHg and DBP below 80 mmHg). Elevated BP developed in 15.5% of participants over the 9-year follow-up. Spline regression and multivariable Cox proportional hazard analysis revealed a non-linear U-shaped association: both low SZn (<84 µg/dL: HR 1.65, 95% CI 1.16-2.36) and high SZn (≥131 µg/dL: HR 1.50, 95% CI 1.06-2.13) were independently associated with increased risk of elevated BP compared to the reference range (84-131 µg/dL). Sex-stratified analyses revealed that men were more susceptible to zinc deficiency-related BP elevation (HR 2.05, 95% CI 1.19-3.51), while women showed greater sensitivity to zinc excess (HR 1.73, 95% CI 1.10-2.71). These sex-specific patterns suggest differential mechanisms linking zinc homeostasis to vascular regulation. Limitations include single baseline zinc measurement, observational design, and use of elevated BP rather than diagnosed hypertension as the outcome.

Implications Medical Affairs : The identification of a U-shaped zinc-blood pressure relationship introduces a micronutrient angle to prehypertension pathophysiology with sex-specific patterns, adding nuance to the concept of precision prevention in cardiovascular risk. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this evidence enriches the narrative around comprehensive cardiovascular risk factor management, supporting educational programs for KOLs that address nutritional and metabolic determinants of blood pressure beyond pharmacological targets. While not directly actionable for pharmaceutical positioning, this type of mechanistic data supports the development of integrative cardiovascular risk communication tools and can underpin research partnerships with academic groups studying the intersection of nutrition and hypertension.

Using human genetics to understand the effect of modulating targets of antihypertensive drugs in pregnancy.

Revue : medRxiv (preprint) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-05-20 (indexe 2026-06-04)

Resultats principaux : This drug target Mendelian randomization (MR) study evaluated the effects of antihypertensive drug targets on adverse pregnancy outcomes using data from up to 671,922 pregnant women, instrumenting pharmacological perturbation with genetic variants near drug target genes for six antihypertensive drug classes. Genetically instrumented downregulation of blood pressure through beta-blocker (ADRB1) and calcium channel blocker (CACNB2) targets was associated with reduced risk of hypertensive disorders of pregnancy, including preeclampsia. CACNB2-instrumented BP lowering corresponded to a 7% (95% CI 5-9%) reduction in preeclampsia risk per 1 mmHg decrease, while ADRB1-mediated reductions showed directionally consistent but less precise estimates. Suggestive evidence of reduced risks was observed for miscarriage, preterm birth, small-for-gestational-age birth, and labour induction across multiple drug classes, though estimates were accompanied by substantial uncertainty. The study highlights important target-specific differences in safety profiles that are not captured by randomized trials due to ethical constraints on conducting controlled trials in pregnancy. Limitations include the preprint (non-peer-reviewed) status, inherent MR assumptions, and potential pleiotropy of genetic instruments.

Implications Medical Affairs : Genetic evidence supporting protective effects of beta-blockers and CCBs (specifically CACNB2-acting agents) on hypertensive disorders of pregnancy strengthens the scientific rationale for recommending these drug classes in pregnant hypertensive patients, an indication historically underserved by randomized controlled trial evidence. Medical Affairs can leverage this MR evidence to engage maternal-fetal medicine and obstetric hypertension KOLs, supporting the positioning of CCBs (particularly nifedipine) and beta-blockers as evidence-based options in this high-risk population. Target-specific differences in safety profiles identified here may inform future label update strategies and risk communication frameworks for antihypertensive agents used during pregnancy.

Dihydropyridine Calcium Channel Blocker-induced Prescribing Cascades: Signal Detection using High-throughput Sequence Symmetry Analysis.

Revue : medRxiv (preprint) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-05-20 (indexe 2026-06-04)

Resultats principaux : This pharmacoepidemiological study applied high-throughput sequence symmetry analysis (HTSSA) to Medicare claims data (2011-2020) in 388,862 new DHP-CCB initiators (mean age 76.6 years; 62.5% women; 92.3% with hypertension) to systematically screen 446 marker drug classes for potential prescribing cascades induced by CCB adverse effects. Of 446 screened drug classes, 82 had significantly elevated adjusted sequence ratios (aSR), and 24 were classified as potential prescribing cascades by clinical consensus. Strongest signals included systemic hemostatics (aSR 2.99, 95% CI 1.10-8.16), nasal preparations (aSR 1.99, 95% CI 1.47-2.70), and erectile dysfunction drugs (aSR 1.85, 95% CI 1.27-2.70). Clinically significant cascades ranked by population impact (number needed to harm) included sulfonamides (NNTH 104), electrolyte solutions (NNTH 216), and osmotically acting laxatives (NNTH 710). Known CCB adverse effects (ankle edema, constipation, gingival hyperplasia) were confirmed as drivers, while several new and underrecognized signals were identified. Limitations include preprint status, Medicare-only elderly population limiting generalizability, and potential indication confounding in HTSSA methodology.

Implications Medical Affairs : Identification of 24 potential DHP-CCB-induced prescribing cascades in a large Medicare cohort has direct pharmacovigilance implications, supporting proactive safety monitoring initiatives and reinforcing label-aligned adverse effect communication to prescribers of CCBs in elderly patients. The high number needed to harm for sulfonamide and laxative cascades suggests these are population-level contributors to polypharmacy burden in older hypertensive patients, warranting enhanced clinical guidance and prescriber education. Medical Affairs teams can engage with health economics and outcomes research partners to quantify the total indirect burden of CCB side-effect cascades, informing competitive differentiation strategies and potentially supporting the case for CCBs with more favorable tolerability profiles.

Comparative Effectiveness and Safety of Three Oral Antihypertensive Therapies on Maternal and Infant Outcomes in a California Medicaid Population, 2016-2020.

Revue : Pharmacoepidemiology and Drug Safety | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This target trial emulation study used California Medicaid claims (2016-2020) linked to the SOMI registry to compare labetalol (n=1,496), extended-release nifedipine (n=132), and methyldopa (n=653) initiated at ≤23 weeks gestation in patients with chronic hypertension, with a composite outcome of preeclampsia with severe features, preterm birth <35 weeks, placental abruption, and fetal or neonatal death. Composite outcome incidence was 23% for labetalol, 19% for methyldopa, and 24% for nifedipine. Adjusted risk ratios showed methyldopa vs. labetalol aRR 0.82 (95% CI 0.68-1.00), nifedipine vs. labetalol aRR 1.01 (95% CI 0.72-1.41), and nifedipine vs. methyldopa aRR 1.22 (95% CI 0.85-1.76). For the safety outcome (small for gestational age birth), results were similar and non-significant across comparisons. Nifedipine and labetalol were found to be equivalent in effectiveness and safety in this real-world setting, while methyldopa showed marginally better composite outcome performance at the threshold of statistical significance. Limitations include the small nifedipine cohort (n=132), potential residual confounding from unmeasured comorbidities, and limited generalizability beyond a Medicaid population.

Implications Medical Affairs : Real-world equivalence of nifedipine (a CCB) and labetalol in early-pregnancy chronic hypertension, combined with the marginal benefit of methyldopa, provides comparative effectiveness data that directly informs treatment algorithm discussions for obstetric hypertension — an indication where ACE inhibitors and ARBs are contraindicated. Medical Affairs can engage obstetric, perinatal, and hypertension KOLs to discuss these nuanced findings and specifically position nifedipine's role as a first-line CCB option when labetalol is unavailable or poorly tolerated in pregnant patients. The very limited nifedipine sample size (n=132) relative to labetalol (n=1,496) in this study highlights a persistent evidence gap that represents an opportunity to advocate for and potentially sponsor larger dedicated CCB trials in obstetric hypertension.

Influence of combination therapy with SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 receptor agonists on the management of blood pressure in Japanese patients with diabetes.

Revue : Hypertension Research | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This post-hoc analysis of the RECAP study examined 431 type 2 diabetes patients with baseline hypertension receiving SGLT2 inhibitor and GLP-1 receptor agonist (GLP-1RA) combination therapy, stratified by the order of agent initiation (GLP-1RA-preceding, n=207; SGLT2i-preceding, n=224). The achievement rate of target office blood pressure (<130/80 mmHg) increased significantly from 25.4% at treatment baseline to 33.3% after combination therapy initiation (p=0.003), representing a meaningful clinical improvement. Office BP decreased from 141.8/82.4 mmHg at baseline to 131.7/79.4 mmHg after combination therapy (both p<0.001), and home systolic BP also significantly decreased (p=0.03). Propensity score-based inverse probability weighting analysis showed no significant differences in BP outcomes or target achievement rates based on which agent was initiated first. Subgroup findings suggest that morning hypertension may be specifically addressed by this combination, though this was exploratory. Limitations include the post-hoc design, small predominantly Japanese sample, absence of a control arm, and limited generalizability beyond an East Asian diabetic population.

Implications Medical Affairs : Evidence that SGLT2i-GLP-1RA combination therapy significantly improves BP control in diabetic hypertensive patients reinforces the multi-benefit cardiovascular profile of these drug classes, which is increasingly central to cardiometabolic market positioning. Medical Affairs can utilize this data to strengthen the "beyond glucose lowering" narrative for SGLT2 inhibitors, emphasizing blood pressure control as an additional therapeutic benefit in patients with T2D and co-existing hypertension. Engagement with diabetology, nephrology, and hypertension KOLs in Asia-Pacific markets — where this study's population is particularly representative — will be strategically valuable for regional advisory board discussions and co-publication strategies.

Translation, adaptation, and validation of a Moroccan version of the H-SCALE (Hypertension Self-Care Activity Level effects) for hypertensive patients.

Revue : BMC Primary Care | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This validation study developed and psychometrically tested a Moroccan Arabic-dialect version of the H-SCALE questionnaire in 120 hypertensive patients from four primary health care centers in Settat, Morocco (71.7% female, mean age 56.64 years). The validation process included forward-backward translation, expert content validity assessment (n=6), face validity testing (n=30), and full psychometric evaluation in an independent sample. Exploratory factor analysis revealed a six-factor structure explaining 53% of total variance (KMO=0.682, Bartlett's test p<0.001), aligned with the original H-SCALE dimensions. Internal consistency was satisfactory (Cronbach's alpha=0.79) and test-retest reliability was good (ICC=0.80, domain-specific coefficients 0.76-0.95). Treatment adherence (p=0.01) and weight management scores (p<0.001) were significantly higher among patients with controlled blood pressure, providing preliminary construct-related validity evidence. Limitations include small sample size, single-city recruitment, and potential recall bias from interview-administered format.

Implications Medical Affairs : Availability of a validated Arabic-dialect tool for measuring hypertension self-care in Morocco fills a critical gap in North African cardiovascular research infrastructure, enabling more rigorous real-world evidence generation and adherence studies in Arabic-speaking populations. Medical Affairs can leverage validated self-care outcome measures in this region to design culturally adapted patient support programs and adherence interventions aligned with local language and healthcare contexts, which is particularly relevant for multinational pharmaceutical strategies in the Maghreb. Decades of Research in the Chronic Disease Self-Management Program: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Long-Term Health Outcomes Across Countries.

Revue : American Journal of Public Health | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-04

Resultats principaux : This comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis examined outcomes of the Chronic Disease Self-Management Program (CDSMP) from 1978-2024, with quantitative meta-analysis covering October 2009-2024 to complement a prior 2013 synthesis; from 6,565 retrieved articles, 228 were included in the review and 20 in the meta-analysis. Participant populations frequently included hypertension, heart disease, cardiovascular disease, and stroke as primary conditions. At 6 months post-intervention, CDSMP was associated with significant improvements in pain (mean difference 0.53, 95% CI 0.24-0.83), depression (mean difference 1.47, 95% CI 1.04-1.90), fatigue (MD 0.46, 95% CI 0.28-0.65), and sleep problems (MD 0.57, 95% CI 0.28-0.85). Individuals with multimorbidity (including hypertension combined with other chronic conditions) showed greater benefit from the program than those with single conditions. Key barriers to implementation identified across studies included securing sustainable funding and ensuring effective participant recruitment and retention. Limitations include heterogeneity of study designs and outcome measures across included studies and variable CDSMP adaptation quality across countries.

Implications Medical Affairs : Long-term evidence that structured self-management programs significantly reduce pain, depression, fatigue, and sleep disturbance in patients with hypertension and cardiovascular multimorbidity provides a strong evidence base for Medical Affairs advocacy of patient support programs that go beyond medication management. This data supports positioning pharmacological therapy within broader integrated care pathways that include behavioral and self-management interventions, aligning with payer and health system priorities around adherence, quality of life, and total cost of care. Selective loss of Cx40 and conduction abnormalities underlie enhanced atrial fibrillation susceptibility in a HFpEF model in male mice.

Revue : Physiological Reports | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06

Resultats principaux : This preclinical murine study investigated atrial electrophysiological remodeling in a heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) model induced by combined high-fat diet and L-NAME administration in male mice, a metabolic model that specifically incorporates hypertension as a key component. The model successfully reproduced HFpEF features including obesity, hypertension, preserved systolic function, diastolic dysfunction, and impaired exercise capacity, allowing study of AF vulnerability in this cardiometabolic context. Transesophageal atrial pacing demonstrated a marked increase in AF inducibility, and ex vivo optical mapping showed prolonged action potential duration, global conduction slowing, and increased conduction heterogeneity in the left atrium — all in the absence of atrial fibrosis, inflammation, or structural enlargement. Both gene and protein expression analyses identified selective downregulation of connexin 40 (Cx40) while connexin 43 expression was preserved, with immunohistochemistry confirming altered Cx40 localization in atrial myocardium; this gap junction dysfunction precedes structural remodeling as the primary electrophysiological AF substrate. Subgroup analysis was not applicable to this mechanistic animal study; key limitations include the murine model design limiting direct clinical translation and the exclusive use of male animals, which precludes understanding of sex-specific differences in HFpEF-associated AF mechanisms.

Implications Medical Affairs : Identification of Cx40 downregulation as the primary mechanism linking hypertension and metabolic risk to AF vulnerability in HFpEF opens a novel electrophysiological pathway with therapeutic targeting potential that extends beyond conventional antihypertensive strategies. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this mechanistic finding could support an expanded narrative for antihypertensive therapy that connects blood pressure control to gap junction preservation and atrial arrhythmia prevention, representing a unique scientific angle for product differentiation in the cardiovascular market. Engaging cardiac electrophysiology and HFpEF research KOLs around this evidence — particularly those investigating the intersection of metabolic risk, hypertension, and AF — could underpin collaborative translational research programs bridging hypertension management with arrhythmia prevention strategies.
