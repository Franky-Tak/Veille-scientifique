Cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic syndrome stage modifies the efficacy of intensive blood pressure control on cognitive outcomes: A post hoc analysis of SPRINT MIND.

Revue : Alzheimer's & Dementia | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : July 2026

Resultats principaux : This post-hoc analysis of the SPRINT MIND randomised controlled trial stratified 8,563 participants with hypertension into non-advanced CKM syndrome (Stage 2, n=5,632) and advanced CKM syndrome (Stages 3–4, n=2,931) to determine whether cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic (CKM) staging modifies the cognitive benefits of intensive blood pressure control. In non-advanced CKM patients, intensive BP lowering significantly reduced the risk of probable dementia (HR 0.62, 95% CI 0.46–0.85) and the composite outcome of probable dementia or mild cognitive impairment (HR 0.74, 95% CI 0.62–0.89). No cognitive benefit was observed in advanced CKM patients (probable dementia HR 1.15; composite HR 0.97), with a statistically significant interaction between treatment arm and CKM stage for the dementia endpoint (p=0.009). No significant subgroup interactions were observed for MCI alone. Limitations include the post-hoc, non-prespecified design which limits causal inference, reliance on SPRINT variables that may not fully capture all CKM dimensions, and limited follow-up duration with potential differential dropout between stages.

Implications Medical Affairs : This analysis introduces critical nuance to current hypertension guidelines by demonstrating that the neuroprotective benefit of intensive BP targets is confined to patients without advanced cardiorenal-metabolic disease, which may necessitate CKM staging before defining individualised treatment targets in clinical practice. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this finding has direct positioning relevance for antihypertensive agents in the context of comorbidity burden, strengthening the case for biomarker-stratified treatment approaches that could differentiate products in a competitive landscape. KOL engagement should prioritise interdisciplinary teams—cardiologists, neurologists, and nephrologists—to build consensus on how CKM staging can be operationalised in routine hypertension management and to identify design elements for a prospective confirmatory trial.

Risk factors for unanticipated hypertension during emergence from general anesthesia in elderly surgical patients: A retrospective cohort analysis.

Revue : Medicine (Baltimore) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : June 26, 2026

Resultats principaux : This retrospective cohort study enrolled 840 elderly patients recovering from general anaesthesia in a single post-anaesthesia care unit (PACU) in China, of whom 212 (25.2%) developed unanticipated hypertension—defined as acute BP elevation in patients with a normal preoperative baseline—during anaesthetic emergence. Conditional multivariate logistic regression identified nine independent risk factors: advanced age (OR=2.016, 95% CI 1.458–2.824), female sex (OR=1.494), absence of preoperative antihypertensive medication (OR=1.886), orthopedic surgery type (OR=2.006), intraoperative hypertension (OR=1.587), urinary catheterisation (OR=3.674), absence of patient-controlled analgesia (OR=2.774), postoperative pain (OR=5.692), and postoperative agitation (OR=1.878). Patients who experienced unanticipated hypertension had significantly higher rates of delayed extubation and prolonged PACU stays, indicating measurable perioperative impact. Subgroup analyses were not conducted due to sample size constraints. Limitations include the retrospective single-centre design at a Chinese institution, absence of long-term follow-up data, and potential unmeasured confounders such as pre-existing white-coat hypertension or subclinical cardiovascular disease.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study identifies a clinically significant and underrecognised hypertension phenotype—acute peri-operative BP elevation in normotensive elderly patients—which carries measurable adverse perioperative consequences and represents a potential indication for targeted pharmacological prophylaxis. For Medical Affairs, this evidence can support discussions with anaesthesiology and surgical KOLs regarding the inclusion of antihypertensive agents in structured perioperative care protocols, particularly for elderly orthopaedic patients. A prospective investigation into whether preemptive pharmacological strategies in high-risk patients reduce perioperative BP events and associated complications would be a high-value research partnership opportunity.

Association between weekend catch-up sleep and metabolic syndrome: A cross-sectional study.

Revue : Medicine (Baltimore) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : June 26, 2026

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study analysed 7,658 U.S. adults from NHANES 2017–2020, categorising participants into five weekend catch-up sleep (WCS) groups (decreased, no change, short 0–1 h, moderate 1–<2 h, and long ≥2 h) and evaluating associations with metabolic syndrome (MetS) using multivariate logistic regression. Moderate WCS (1–<2 hours; OR=0.72, 95% CI 0.53–0.98) and long WCS (≥2 hours; OR=0.71, 95% CI 0.52–0.98) were associated with significantly lower odds of MetS. This protective effect was primarily driven by a robust negative association with hypertension (moderate WCS: OR=0.64, 95% CI 0.46–0.89; long WCS: OR=0.60, 95% CI 0.43–0.83). Exploratory subgroup analyses suggested stronger effects in males, Mexican Americans, individuals sleeping 6–9 hours/night on weekdays, and those sedentary for ≥8 hours/day, although interaction tests were not statistically significant. A restricted cubic spline model revealed a nonlinear relationship between WCS and MetS risk; key limitations include the cross-sectional design precluding causal inference, reliance on self-reported sleep data, and inability to adjust fully for sleep disorders such as obstructive sleep apnoea.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings position sleep compensatory behaviours—specifically weekend catch-up sleep—as a potentially modifiable lifestyle determinant of hypertension risk, with hypertension emerging as the primary MetS component driving the observed association, suggesting targeted sleep hygiene messaging could be incorporated into hypertension prevention programmes. For Medical Affairs, this data supports the rationale for cross-disciplinary collaborations with sleep medicine KOLs and preventive cardiologists to develop integrated lifestyle modification programmes that address sleep quality alongside dietary and exercise interventions in hypertension management guidelines. The nonlinear dose-response relationship and differential effects by sex and ethnicity identified in exploratory analyses warrant prospective, pre-specified validation studies that could generate evidence informing personalised hypertension prevention messaging for diverse patient populations.

Clinicopathological profile, risk factors and outcome of adult biopsy-proven infection-related glomerulonephritis: A single centre prospective study.

Revue : JPMA. The Journal of the Pakistan Medical Association | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : June 2026

Resultats principaux : This prospective, cross-sectional, observational study at Ayub Teaching Hospital, Pakistan, analysed 180 renal biopsies over approximately 20 months (April 2023–December 2024), identifying 22 cases (12.2%) of biopsy-proven infection-related glomerulonephritis (IRGN), predominantly in males (63.6%) with a mean age of 36.23±19.79 years. Renal dysfunction was marked at presentation, with mean serum creatinine 3.30±3.11 mg/dL and nephrotic-range proteinuria 3.82±3.05 g/day, accompanied by hypoalbuminaemia; haematuria (77.2%) and leucocyturia (54.5%) were the most frequent manifestations. Histopathology showed neutrophilic infiltration (86.6%), endocapillary (81.8%) and mesangial (77.2%) proliferation, with dominant C3 deposits on immunofluorescence in all cases. Hypertension was the key comorbidity in 36.4% of cases and, together with chronic histological changes (interstitial fibrosis in 27.2%), correlated with progression to chronic kidney disease (9.1%) or end-stage renal disease (9.1%), while 81.8% achieved full recovery. Limitations include the small IRGN cohort (n=22), single-centre design in a low-resource setting, and short follow-up, all restricting generalisability.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study reinforces hypertension as both a prevalent comorbidity and an independent prognostic driver in infection-related glomerulonephritis, underscoring the need for aggressive and early blood pressure management alongside nephrology care in patients with renal inflammatory disease. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this evidence strengthens the case for integrating antihypertensive treatment algorithms into nephrology guidelines for IRGN management, particularly in low- and middle-income countries where infectious burden remains high and renal sequelae are common. KOL engagement with nephrologists in endemic regions could support investigator-initiated research to establish optimal BP targets and preferred antihypertensive classes in the specific context of renal inflammatory disease and post-infectious nephropathy.

Association between perceived stress and metabolic parameters: a systematic review and meta-analysis.

Revue : Diabetology & Metabolic Syndrome | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : June 27, 2026

Resultats principaux : This systematic review and meta-analysis synthesised evidence from 30 studies comprising 12,491 participants, examining the association between perceived stress measured via the Perceived Stress Scale (PSS-10 or PSS-14) and metabolic parameters associated with metabolic syndrome (MetS) using random-effects meta-regression with standardised stress scores. Contrary to prevailing assumptions, most metabolic parameters—including BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure, fasting glucose, and triglycerides—showed no statistically significant association with perceived stress across studies. The primary positive finding was a significant association between perceived stress and insulin resistance as measured by HOMA-IR (β=0.463, 95% CI 0.159–0.767), an exploratory finding sensitive to individual study inclusion. Borderline associations were observed for HDL-C and total cholesterol, but these did not reach statistical significance. Substantial heterogeneity was present across all analyses; limitations include cross-study variability in design, PSS version, metabolic outcome measurement, and population characteristics, along with the subjective nature of the PSS instrument which may not capture objective physiological stress burden.

Implications Medical Affairs : The lack of a statistically significant association between perceived psychological stress (PSS) and blood pressure challenges simplistic narratives positioning stress reduction as a direct antihypertensive intervention, indicating that the relationship between psychosocial stress and hypertension likely operates through more complex biological mediators than subjective stress perception alone. For Medical Affairs, this nuanced evidence base should inform KOL communications and patient education materials to avoid overstating the efficacy of psychological stress management as a standalone hypertension intervention, while acknowledging its potential role in broader cardiometabolic risk reduction through insulin resistance pathways. Future research investments should prioritise longitudinal studies combining objective stress biomarkers (cortisol, HRV, allostatic load indices) with standardised BP measurements to better characterise the stress-hypertension mechanistic pathway and identify potential pharmacological or behavioural modulation targets.

Cardiometabolic Risk in U.S. Pregnant Women and Associations with Food Insecurity, Diet Quality, and Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children Participation.

Revue : The Journal of Nutrition | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : June 27, 2026

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study leveraged NHANES data from 1999–2023 to characterise cardiometabolic risk factors in 1,538 pregnant U.S. women aged 20–44 years using survey-weighted multivariable linear regression models adjusted for age, race/ethnicity, gravidity, income, education, and marital status. Cardiometabolic risk burden was high: pre-pregnancy overweight or obesity was found in 51.9%, elevated fasting glucose in 29.3%, and elevated blood pressure or hypertension in 10.7% of participants. Food insecurity was associated with significantly lower HDL-C (β=-4.02, SE=2.03, p=0.05) but not with blood pressure, CRP, or fasting glucose levels. Higher Healthy Eating Index-2020 (HEI-2020) scores were associated with higher HDL-C (β=1.45, SE=0.71, p=0.04), but showed no significant association with blood pressure, CRP, or fasting glucose. WIC programme participation modified the positive association between HEI-2020 scores and systolic blood pressure; study limitations include cross-sectional design precluding causal inference, self-reported dietary data, and single-point BP measurements that may not meet pregnancy-specific hypertension diagnostic criteria.

Implications Medical Affairs : The 10.7% prevalence of elevated blood pressure or hypertension among U.S. pregnant women—combined with its lack of direct association with dietary quality indices in this study—highlights the multifactorial and inadequately addressed nature of hypertension in pregnancy, an area of high unmet medical need given existing pharmacological constraints in this population. From a Medical Affairs perspective, these data can support advocacy with OB/GYN and maternal-fetal medicine KOLs regarding the urgent need for evidence-based antihypertensive strategies specifically validated for pregnant populations, potentially positioning safer antihypertensive agents as key components of maternal cardiovascular care pathways. The modified association between diet quality and systolic BP through WIC participation suggests that structured nutrition assistance programmes may serve as an actionable policy lever for hypertension prevention in high-risk maternal populations, warranting investigator-initiated research partnerships.

Age-dependent performance of obesity measures in screening for cardiometabolic risk over the lifespan.

Revue : Public Health | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : June 27, 2026

Resultats principaux : This large cross-sectional study analysed 58,712 participants from NHANES 1999–2020 (aged 12–85 years) to evaluate age-specific associations of BMI and waist-to-height ratio (WHtR) with cardiometabolic risk including hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and diabetes, using logistic regression, ROC curves, and population attributable fraction analyses. ROC analysis demonstrated that the discriminative performance of both BMI and WHtR for identifying cardiometabolic risk declined progressively with increasing age, with lower AUCs in older age groups. Nonlinear, J-shaped cross-sectional associations of BMI and WHtR with cardiometabolic risk were observed, with risk plateauing at higher adiposity levels particularly in older adults. Exploratory age- and sex-specific screening thresholds derived using Youden's index showed relatively balanced sensitivity and specificity, but conventional thresholds (BMI ≥25 or ≥30 kg/m²) displayed variable performance across age strata. Limitations include the cross-sectional design precluding longitudinal causal inference, the exploratory and unvalidated nature of proposed thresholds, and potential residual confounding from unmeasured lifestyle variables.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study provides robust evidence that conventional obesity thresholds underperform in identifying cardiometabolic risk— including hypertension—in older adults, arguing for age- and sex-stratified screening approaches that could meaningfully improve early detection and treatment eligibility in ageing populations. For Medical Affairs, this evidence reinforces the case for advocating with guideline committees and preventive cardiology KOLs for more nuanced patient profiling frameworks that move beyond BMI as a single screening metric, potentially enabling earlier identification and pharmacological management of hypertension-risk patients. Prospective validation of age- and sex-specific anthropometric thresholds through collaborative research could generate clinically actionable evidence that supports guideline evolution and strengthens the scientific narrative for targeted antihypertensive intervention in older, potentially underdiagnosed populations.

Associations between PM2.5 exposure and upper tract urothelial carcinoma in a Taiwanese case-control study.

Revue : International Urology and Nephrology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : June 27, 2026

Resultats principaux : This retrospective case-control study from the Kaohsiung Medical University Hospital Research Database matched 74 upper tract urothelial carcinoma (UTUC) cases with 296 controls (1:4 ratio) using propensity score matching for age and sex, examining PM2.5 exposure across multiple timeframes (index date and 1, 2, 3, and 5 years prior). UTUC patients exhibited significantly higher PM2.5 concentrations across all evaluated exposure periods (all p<0.01) compared with matched controls. Multivariable conditional logistic regression adjusted for age, sex, smoking, diabetes, hypertension, and hyperlipidaemia showed that higher PM2.5 concentrations were independently associated with increased odds of UTUC (adjusted OR ranging from 1.436 to 2.719 per IQR increase in PM2.5 across timeframes). Sensitivity analyses excluding smokers and after additional adjustment for alpha-blocker use confirmed consistent findings. Notable limitations include the small case number (n=74), single-centre design, ecologic PM2.5 exposure estimates lacking individual-level measurement, and potential residual confounding from established Taiwanese UTUC risk factors including aristolochic acid and arsenic exposure.

Implications Medical Affairs : Although this study primarily addresses UTUC oncogenesis rather than hypertension, it contributes to the evidence base linking PM2.5 air pollution—adjusted for hypertension as a comorbidity—to multisystem pathological outcomes, reinforcing the concept that environmental exposures drive concurrent chronic disease burdens including cardiovascular risk. From a Medical Affairs standpoint, this data can be integrated into broader environmental health communications highlighting the intersection of air pollution, hypertension as a modifying comorbidity, and downstream organ-specific oncological risk, supporting holistic disease prevention narratives with KOLs in nephrology, urology, and environmental medicine. Prospective cohort studies examining PM2.5 exposure trajectories in hypertensive patients specifically—including effects on blood pressure dynamics and multimorbidity incidence—would strengthen the scientific foundation for environmental monitoring recommendations within chronic disease management programmes.

Nutrients and bioactive compounds in nuts: implications for human health.

Revue : Food Chemistry | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : June 2, 2026

Resultats principaux : This comprehensive narrative review synthesises evidence on the nutritional composition and bioactive profiles of tree nuts and peanuts—including unsaturated fatty acids, fibre, minerals, vitamins, polyphenols, and phytosterols—drawing on both observational cohort data and randomised clinical trials. A substantial and consistent body of evidence from multiple study designs associates regular nut consumption with reduced risk of hypertension, elevated LDL-cholesterol, obesity, cardiovascular diseases (particularly coronary heart disease), and certain cancers, with benefits linked to polyphenol–gut microbiota interactions and favourable modulation of lipid and inflammatory pathways. The review documents that nut consumption is associated with lower all-cause mortality and contributions to weight management and healthy ageing across diverse populations. Processing effects on nutrient bioaccessibility—particularly for polyphenols—are examined, and the emerging potential of nut co-products as functional ingredients is discussed. As a narrative review, key limitations include inherent selection bias in literature included, inability to pool effect sizes quantitatively, and heterogeneity in the underlying evidence base regarding nut types, doses, preparation methods, and study populations.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review consolidates the scientific rationale for dietary nut consumption as a blood pressure-lowering and cardiometabolic risk-reduction strategy, reinforcing lifestyle modification as an essential and evidence-supported complement to pharmacological antihypertensive therapy rather than an alternative. For Medical Affairs, these findings support the development of patient education resources and KOL communications in preventive cardiology, dietetics, and primary care that position structured dietary interventions—including nut consumption—within multimodal hypertension management frameworks alongside guideline-recommended medications. Investment in rigorous randomised clinical trials specifically targeting systolic and diastolic blood pressure as co-primary endpoints in well-characterised hypertensive populations consuming defined nut preparations would provide the level of evidence needed to support specific health claims for nut consumption in hypertension management.
