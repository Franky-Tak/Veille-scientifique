Role of illicit drug use in Type B aortic dissection and trial participation.

Revue : Seminars in Vascular Surgery | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-02-11

Resultats principaux : This is a systematic literature review investigating the clinical presentation, medical and surgical management of stimulant-positive patients (cocaine and amphetamine users) presenting with uncomplicated Type B aortic dissection (TBAD). Cocaine and amphetamine use is strongly associated with TBAD, disproportionately affecting younger patients who present with more complicated disease and experience significantly greater postoperative morbidity compared to non-stimulant users. Stimulant use precipitates acute sympathetic activation, labile hypertension, and increased aortic wall stress, all of which complicate both medical management (antihypertensive therapy) and procedural management (thoracic endovascular aortic repair, TEVAR). Stimulant-positive patients are frequently underrepresented or entirely excluded from prospective trials evaluating optimal medical therapy, TEVAR, and perioperative management, due to ethical concerns, medicolegal risk, stigma, and barriers to follow-up, resulting in reliance on retrospective data and an absence of standardized management guidance. The authors recommend that perioperative management should be guided by clinical evidence of intoxication rather than toxicology results alone, with nonurgent procedures deferred only in cases of active intoxication. Early multidisciplinary involvement including addiction medicine, social work, and case management is advocated to address barriers to medication adherence, reduce recurrence risk, and improve continuity of care. A key limitation of this review is its reliance on retrospective observational data, given the systematic exclusion of this population from prospective trials, limiting the strength of evidence-based recommendations.

Implications Medical Affairs : The systematic under-representation of stimulant-using TBAD patients in clinical trials highlights a major evidence gap that currently prevents guideline development for antihypertensive agent selection and procedural timing in this high-risk, high-hypertension-burden population, representing an unmet need that Medical Affairs teams should flag for regulatory and scientific engagement. From a product positioning perspective, antihypertensive drugs with rapid onset, favorable hemodynamic profiles, and limited drug interaction risks (particularly with agents metabolized by stimulant pathways) should be evaluated in this context, with real-world evidence generation programs targeting vascular surgery and emergency cardiology centers. Building a KOL network at the intersection of vascular surgery, addiction medicine, and cardiology to co-develop consensus screening and treatment algorithms for stimulant-associated TBAD would provide a meaningful platform for Medical Affairs engagement and could support future label expansion discussions with regulatory agencies.

Evidence-informed guidance for the clinical use of oral semaglutide in obesity management.

Revue : Postgraduate Medicine | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-12

Resultats principaux : This expert guidance review covers the clinical use of oral semaglutide, the first oral glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonist (GLP-1 RA) approved for obesity management and cardiovascular risk reduction in adults, drawing on evidence from clinical trials including the OASIS 4 trial and the authors' clinical expertise. Oral semaglutide demonstrates weight loss efficacy comparable to subcutaneous GLP-1 RA therapies, accompanied by clinically meaningful improvements in cardiometabolic risk factors including blood pressure reduction, lipid profile improvement, and glycemic control. The review establishes strict administration requirements that are essential for bioavailability: tablets must be taken first thing in the morning on an empty stomach with no more than 120 mL of plain water, followed by a mandatory 30-minute fasting period before eating, drinking additional fluids, or ingesting other oral medications. Person-centered clinical consultations prior to treatment initiation are emphasized to ensure patients understand administration requirements, establish realistic expectations regarding treatment targets, and cover strategies to minimize, prepare for, and manage adverse events, primarily gastrointestinal in nature. The paper does not report quantitative blood pressure reduction figures from specific trial data but contextualizes cardiometabolic risk improvements as a key advantage of this agent class in patients with obesity-related hypertension. A key limitation is that evidence is predominantly derived from randomized controlled trials with stringent inclusion criteria, which may limit generalizability to real-world populations with greater comorbidity burdens, including uncontrolled or treatment-resistant hypertension.

Implications Medical Affairs : The dual approval of oral semaglutide for both obesity management and cardiovascular risk reduction, which includes blood pressure benefits, positions it as a significant entrant in the cardiometabolic space with direct implications for hypertension treatment algorithm positioning, potentially complementing or partially replacing traditional add-on antihypertensive therapies in patients with obesity-driven hypertension. Medical Affairs teams should monitor emerging real-world evidence on blood pressure as an endpoint from the OASIS 4 program and consider commissioning health economics analyses to quantify the blood pressure benefit in hypertensive obese patients, strengthening the value proposition versus injectable GLP-1 RAs and standard antihypertensive combinations. Engaging KOLs in endocrinology, primary care, and cardiology through advisory boards focused on oral versus injectable GLP-1 RA differentiation, adherence optimization, and patient profiling (particularly hypertensive patients with obesity) will be critical to shaping prescribing habits and achieving appropriate guideline inclusion in cardiometabolic risk management pathways.
