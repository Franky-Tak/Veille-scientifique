Genetic Risk for Alzheimer Disease, Midlife Hypertension, and Dementia: The ARIC Neurocognitive Study.

Revue : Neurology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-01

Resultats principaux : The Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities (ARIC) Study is a large, ongoing prospective cohort study enrolling 8,931 White and 2,666 Black participants free of dementia at age 55, with median follow-up of 26.6 and 23.8 years respectively, specifically designed to assess whether genetic risk for Alzheimer disease (AD) modifies the association between midlife hypertension and incident dementia. The primary and secondary endpoints were incident dementia and dementia-free survival to age 80, assessed through cognitive testing, informant interviews, and hospitalization codes. Midlife hypertension was significantly associated with dementia incidence across all three AD genetic risk groups (low, medium, high) among White participants, with hazard ratios of 1.29 (95% CI 1.07-1.55), 1.34 (95% CI 1.13-1.58), and 1.19 (95% CI 1.03-1.38) respectively, and among Black participants at high genetic risk only (HR 1.31; 95% CI 1.04-1.66). Individuals with hypertension had a 0-2% higher absolute probability of developing dementia by age 80 and a 6-13% lower probability of dementia-free survival, with no statistically significant differences in hypertension-dementia associations across genetic risk groups, indicating genetic risk does not modify this association. Key limitations include insufficient statistical power to detect significant associations in low/medium genetic risk Black participants and residual confounding from unmeasured lifestyle and clinical variables in this observational study.

Implications Medical Affairs : This landmark Neurology study reinforces that antihypertensive treatment confers universal dementia risk reduction regardless of Alzheimer genetic predisposition, providing strong evidence to support guideline updates advocating aggressive BP management as a dementia prevention strategy in all adult hypertensive patients. For Medical Affairs, these findings substantially broaden the value proposition of antihypertensive therapies beyond cardiovascular endpoints to include neurodegenerative disease prevention, creating compelling messaging for prescribers in neurology and geriatrics alongside cardiologists. The differentiated findings by race underscore an actionable KOL strategy priority: designing adequately powered, racially diverse clinical trials to clarify hypertension-dementia interactions across genetic backgrounds, enabling more targeted therapeutic positioning.

Drinking water salinity and blood pressure in coastal Bangladesh: follow-up of a population-based cohort in a climate-vulnerable region.

Revue : BMJ open | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-01

Resultats principaux : This follow-up cohort study re-enrolled 740 non-pregnant women in rural coastal Bangladesh in 2018, originally recruited during pregnancy between 2009 and 2011, to investigate the association between drinking-water sodium concentration and blood pressure in a region severely affected by saltwater intrusion and climate-driven water insecurity. The primary outcome was blood pressure measured by a standardized protocol, with the main exposure being sodium concentration in household drinking water, which in groundwater/tubewell users reached a mean of 933 mg/L (SD 372), with approximately 22% of women consuming water contributing up to 94% of the WHO maximum daily sodium intake. Sodium concentrations increased in 7 of 9 administrative unions between 2009 and 2018 (p=0.013), indicating a worsening trend linked to climate change; exposure to drinking water with >1000 mg/L sodium versus <200 mg/L was associated with 2.3-fold higher odds of elevated diastolic blood pressure (adjusted OR 2.30; 95% CI 1.40-3.77; p=0.001), with a non-linear threshold effect near 1000 mg/L. No statistically significant association was detected for systolic blood pressure, which may reflect the predominantly diastolic effect of dietary sodium in this population or measurement variability. Study limitations include the single-sex cohort of women, reliance on household rather than individual consumption measurements, and the cross-sectional blood pressure assessment in the 2018 follow-up limiting longitudinal inference.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study identifies drinking water salinity as a growing, climate-driven environmental risk factor for diastolic hypertension in vulnerable coastal populations, directly relevant to the expanding LMIC market for antihypertensive therapies in South and Southeast Asia where climate change is expected to worsen saltwater intrusion. Medical Affairs teams should leverage these findings to position antihypertensive agents as essential responses to environmental determinants of hypertension, supporting advocacy efforts for population-level interventions that include water quality regulation alongside pharmacological management. Strategic KOL engagement with WHO policy experts, public health leaders in Bangladesh and coastal Asian nations, and climate-health researchers would enable the development of joint evidence-generation programs linking environmental salt exposure monitoring with hypertension treatment outcomes.

Antihypertensive Efficacy, Safety, and Adherence After Switching From a Free Combination of Bisoprolol/Metoprolol and Amlodipine to Bisoprolol/Amlodipine FDC Tablets (5/5 mg) in Chinese Patients With Hypertension.

Revue : Journal of clinical hypertension (Greenwich, Conn.) | Tier : Tier 3 | IF estime : 3-10

Date de publication : 2026-07

Resultats principaux : This multicenter prospective trial enrolled 78 Chinese hypertension patients (mean age 49±12.4 years, 73.1% male) inadequately controlled after 4 weeks of free-combination beta-blocker/amlodipine therapy, evaluating the efficacy and safety of switching to a fixed-dose combination (FDC) bisoprolol/amlodipine 5/5 mg tablet over 12 weeks, with inclusion requiring office SBP ≥140 mmHg and/or DBP ≥90 mmHg plus resting heart rate ≥70 bpm. The primary endpoint was achievement of clinical blood pressure and heart rate targets, assessed via office BP at weeks 4, 8, and 12, home BP at weeks 0, 1, 5, and 9, and 24-hour ambulatory BP monitoring at baseline and week 11. As early as 1 week after switching, home SBP decreased by 6.6 mmHg, DBP by 4.2 mmHg, and resting heart rate by 5.5 bpm (all p<0.0001); by 12 weeks, office SBP dropped by 13.9 mmHg, DBP by 11.2 mmHg, and resting heart rate by 11.3 bpm (all p<0.0001). Clinic Time in Target Range (TTR) improved dramatically from 31.3% to 79.2% (absolute increase 47.9 percentage points, p<0.0001), with the proportion achieving TTR >70% rising from 19.2% to 66.7%, and ambulatory BP and heart rate also improved significantly at 11 weeks (p<0.001). Limitations include the single-arm design without a parallel control group, small sample size of 78 patients, and short 12-week follow-up precluding conclusions about long-term cardiovascular event outcomes.

Implications Medical Affairs : The robust and rapid BP and heart rate reductions achieved with bisoprolol/amlodipine FDC in Chinese patients failing free-combination therapy provide compelling real-world evidence supporting FDC strategies as the preferred intensification approach, directly relevant to current hypertension guideline recommendations favoring simplified regimens to improve adherence. Medical Affairs should prioritize this evidence to position bisoprolol/amlodipine FDC as a superior treatment escalation option for East Asian hypertensive patients with elevated resting heart rate, a clinically differentiated target population often underserved by current guideline algorithms. A priority KOL strategy should focus on Chinese and broader Asian hypertension society leaders to advocate for TTR as a key BP control endpoint in updated national guidelines and to sponsor a larger RCT confirming long-term cardiovascular outcome benefits of FDC versus free-combination therapy.

Association of Triglyceride-Glucose Index, Triglyceride/HDL Cholesterol Ratio and Metabolic Score for Insulin Resistance With Asymptomatic Organ Damage in Previously Untreated Patients With Hypertension.

Revue : Journal of clinical hypertension (Greenwich, Conn.) | Tier : Tier 3 | IF estime : 3-10

Date de publication : 2026-07

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study in Greece examined three insulin resistance (IR) indices — TyG index, TG/HDL-C ratio, and METS-IR — in 614 newly diagnosed, previously untreated hypertensive patients to determine their association with asymptomatic target organ damage (TOD), as measured by left ventricular mass index (LVMI), carotid intima-media thickness (CCA-IMT), and estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), using ROC analysis, logistic and linear regression, and restricted cubic splines. After adjusting for confounders, high TG/HDL-C (OR 1.50; 95% CI 1.03-2.18) and METS-IR (OR 2.17; 95% CI 1.50-3.13) were independently associated with increased risk of left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH), while only high METS-IR predicted carotid plaque (OR 2.07; 95% CI 1.17-3.68) and only high TG/HDL-C predicted renal damage (OR 2.33; 95% CI 1.02-5.36). All three IR indices were significantly associated with CCA-IMT in linear regression (p<0.05), and restricted cubic spline analysis revealed a significant non-linear association between TyG index and CCA-IMT (p for non-linearity=0.009), suggesting threshold effects in arterial damage. Subgroup analysis was not reported, and the cross-sectional nature prevents causal inference; the hospital-based cohort may not represent the broader hypertensive population, and the absence of longitudinal follow-up limits conclusions about IR-driven TOD progression.

Implications Medical Affairs : By demonstrating that insulin resistance biomarkers independently predict subclinical cardiac, vascular, and renal organ damage at hypertension diagnosis, this study supports the inclusion of IR indices in initial cardiovascular risk stratification protocols, potentially strengthening the case for early combination antihypertensive and metabolic therapy in high-IR patients. Medical Affairs should leverage these findings to position combined cardiometabolic agents — such as SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 receptor agonists alongside antihypertensives — as scientifically justified for the large subset of hypertensive patients with concurrent insulin resistance, a growing and undertreated population. A targeted KOL strategy engaging Greek, European, and international endocrinology and cardiology leaders should advocate for prospective studies examining whether early IR-guided treatment selection reduces organ damage progression and long-term cardiovascular events in newly diagnosed hypertension.

Pre-pregnancy evaluation of essential hypertensive women with poor obstetric history.

Revue : Revista da Associacao Medica Brasileira (1992) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-29

Resultats principaux : This retrospective cohort study from a Turkish tertiary center compared 25 women with essential hypertension and poor obstetric history against 243 high-risk controls without essential hypertension, evaluating comorbidities and placenta-related metabolic, immunological, inflammatory, and vascular/thrombotic risk factors identified during structured preconception evaluation, with subsequent pregnancies then assessed for gestational outcomes. Women with essential hypertension had significantly higher overall comorbidity rates (68% vs. 39.9%, p=0.013), higher chronic inflammatory disease prevalence (28% vs. 5.8%, p=0.001), and substantially higher carbohydrate metabolism disorders (44% vs. 10.3%, p<0.001) compared to high-risk controls without hypertension. Despite these high-risk profiles, subsequent pregnancy outcomes were largely comparable between groups following systematic preconception management and risk factor treatment, with the notable exception of APGAR score <7 (p=0.016), suggesting residual perinatal risk even after intervention. These findings highlight the multimorbid nature of essential hypertension in reproductive-age women and the potential for preconception evaluation to mitigate obstetric risks even in complex patients. Key limitations include the very small essential hypertension group (n=25), retrospective design with inherent selection and recall biases, single-center tertiary hospital setting limiting generalizability, and short follow-up of only the immediately subsequent pregnancy.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study highlights the critical importance of structured preconception risk stratification in women with essential hypertension, revealing a high burden of carbohydrate metabolism disorders (44%) and chronic inflammatory conditions that compound obstetric risk beyond hypertension alone, supporting the need for multidisciplinary preconception clinics. From a Medical Affairs perspective, the frequent co-occurrence of essential hypertension with metabolic disorders in women of reproductive age identifies a strategic target population for combination antihypertensive and metabolic therapies with established safety profiles in pregnancy planning. KOL engagement should prioritize maternal-fetal medicine specialists, women's health cardiologists, and reproductive endocrinologists to develop evidence-based preconception hypertension management guidelines that include antihypertensive selection criteria optimized for women planning pregnancy.

Impact of physical activity associated with bariatric surgery on systemic arterial hypertension control: a systematic review.

Revue : Sao Paulo medical journal = Revista paulista de medicina | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-29

Resultats principaux : This PRISMA-registered systematic review (PROSPERO: CRD42024628299) screened 406 records and included 9 studies with a total of 504 participants to evaluate whether bariatric surgery (BS) combined with physical activity (PA) provides additional blood pressure (BP) reduction compared to BS alone in adults with obesity and systemic arterial hypertension. Study designs included randomized controlled trials, cohort, and cross-sectional studies, with follow-up durations ranging from 4 months to 5 years and PA types varying substantially across studies. Bariatric surgery alone was consistently associated with significant reductions in both systolic and diastolic BP, and when PA was added to BS, additional BP reductions were reported across most included studies. However, findings were characterized by significant heterogeneity and supported only by low to very low certainty of evidence according to GRADE criteria, limiting the strength of clinical recommendations. The review's key limitations are the small number of eligible studies, extensive heterogeneity in PA type, frequency, intensity, and duration across studies, short follow-up periods precluding assessment of long-term cardiovascular outcomes, and absence of standardized BP measurement protocols across studies.

Implications Medical Affairs : This systematic review identifies an important evidence gap regarding the additive antihypertensive benefit of combining PA with bariatric surgery, creating an opportunity for pharmaceutical and medical device sponsors to fund high-quality, long-term RCTs integrating pharmacological antihypertensive therapy with both interventions to generate definitive evidence. Medical Affairs teams should use these preliminary findings to advocate for multidisciplinary hypertension management protocols in post-bariatric surgery programs, positioning antihypertensive agents as essential complements to surgical and lifestyle interventions rather than alternatives. Strategic KOL partnerships with bariatric surgery centers, exercise physiology research groups, and obesity medicine specialists will be critical to design and sponsor landmark trials that could reshape clinical guidelines on BP management in obese hypertensive patients undergoing metabolic surgery.

Socioeconomic and lifestyle factors influencing non-communicable disease prevalence among rural Jamaican adults: a population-based study.

Revue : Rural and remote health | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-29

Resultats principaux : This population-based cross-sectional study analyzed secondary data from the Jamaica Health and Lifestyle Survey III (2016-2017), including 1,185 rural Jamaican adults aged 15 years and older, to examine the prevalence and socioeconomic and lifestyle determinants of hypertension, diabetes, and obesity using descriptive statistics, bivariate analyses, and multivariate logistic regression. Hypertension prevalence was 34.5%, obesity 28.9%, and diabetes 12.8% — a substantial NCD burden in a Caribbean LMIC rural setting — with older age emerging as the strongest predictor: adults aged 60+ had 4.8-fold (95% CI: 3.7-6.2) higher odds of hypertension compared to younger adults. Obesity and physical inactivity were independent risk factors for hypertension and diabetes, while secondary or higher education was protective against diabetes (but not independently examined for hypertension), and women were significantly more likely to be obese, highlighting important gender differences in NCD risk distribution. Subgroup findings revealed that the multifactorial NCD burden was particularly severe in older, less educated, and physically inactive adults, requiring targeted multisectoral community interventions. Limitations include the cross-sectional design preventing causal inference, reliance on secondary data from 2016-2017 which may not reflect current NCD trends, potential recall bias in self-reported variables, and possible under-representation of migrant populations from the study area.

Implications Medical Affairs : The high hypertension prevalence of 34.5% and strong associations with age, obesity, and physical inactivity in rural Jamaica underscore a significant unmet need for effective antihypertensive therapy access and health literacy programs in Caribbean LMIC settings, representing an emerging market priority. Medical Affairs should develop market access strategies specifically targeting affordable, simplified fixed-dose combination antihypertensive regimens suitable for low-resource primary care settings in Jamaica and the wider Caribbean region, where polypharmacy barriers are a major driver of poor BP control. Strategic KOL partnerships with Caribbean public health institutions, PAHO, and community health networks would enable co-design of regional pharmacoepidemiological studies and community-based BP screening programs that generate the real-world evidence needed to support formulary access for antihypertensive therapies across Caribbean markets.

Effect of antihypertensive medication reduction on short-term blood pressure control in older adults: calibration of OPTiMISE trial results to real-world primary care data.

Revue : Age and ageing | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-01

Resultats principaux : This methodologically novel study calibrated the OPTiMISE randomized trial (n=569, adults ≥80 years with controlled BP on ≥2 antihypertensives) to a representative English primary care population of 798,179 individuals (median age 84 years, 48% female) using inverse probability weighting (IPW) based on trial inclusion likelihood, with the primary endpoint being all-cause hospitalization or death. The real-world target population was substantially frailer than the OPTiMISE trial cohort, with 25% overtly frail compared to only 11% in the trial, raising critical questions about the external validity of trial findings in routine clinical practice. After IPW calibration, deprescribing one antihypertensive drug was not associated with higher risk of hospitalization or death (calibrated HR 0.94; 95% CI: 0.73-1.22), closely mirroring the long-term OPTiMISE follow-up result (HR 0.93; 95% CI: 0.76-1.12) despite the wider confidence interval reflecting the greater population heterogeneity. Subgroup analysis by frailty status was not separately reported, and blood pressure outcomes in the calibrated population were not directly measured. Key limitations are the retrospective observational calibration approach, the inability to assess BP outcomes in the calibrated population, and the wide confidence intervals that do not definitively rule out either benefit or harm in specific subgroups of very frail patients.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study provides compelling evidence supporting the safety of antihypertensive deprescribing in frail octogenarians in real-world primary care settings, which may accelerate guideline updates incorporating frailty assessment into treatment de-escalation algorithms for older hypertensive patients. From a Medical Affairs perspective, the finding that deprescribing is safe even in frail populations challenges traditional treat-to-target paradigms in the very elderly and supports individualized, patient-centered benefit-risk frameworks when positioning antihypertensive products in geriatric care markets. An actionable KOL strategy should engage geriatricians and primary care leaders to develop and validate frailty-integrated decision support tools for antihypertensive prescribing and deprescribing, positioning such tools as the clinical innovation that bridges trial evidence and real-world practice.

Are RAS inhibitors necessary during intensive immunosuppression for lupus nephritis? Real-world evidence from the STAR cohort.

Revue : Lupus science & medicine | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-30

Resultats principaux : This retrospective cohort study from the Chinese STAR (Treat-SLE-to-Target) registry evaluated real-world outcomes of adding RAS inhibitors (RASi) to standard induction immunosuppression in 314 patients with active lupus nephritis (LN), with 220 receiving ≥3 months of continuous RASi and 94 in the non-RASi group, using propensity score overlap weighting to balance baseline characteristics including higher baseline 24-hour urinary protein and hypertension prevalence in the RASi group. The primary endpoint was complete renal remission (CRR) at 12 months, and secondary endpoints included total renal remission (TRR) at 6 months, proteinuria reduction magnitude, and glucocorticoid dosage. Weighted analysis found no significant difference in CRR at 12 months (58.7% vs. 64.7%, p=0.428) or 6 months (43.1% vs. 53.4%, p=0.169), and surprisingly, 6-month TRR was significantly lower in the RASi group (59.0% vs. 76.8%, p=0.026). Proteinuria reduction percentage was comparable between groups, and RASi use did not accelerate glucocorticoid tapering or disease activity decline; multivariable analysis identified LN at SLE onset and baseline pyuria, but not RASi use, as independent predictors of CRR. Limitations include the retrospective registry design susceptible to indication bias despite propensity scoring, potential residual confounding, the non-randomized comparison, and the fact that RASi were used heterogeneously for both hemodynamic antiproteinuric and renoprotective indications.

Implications Medical Affairs : These real-world registry data fundamentally challenge current guidelines recommending routine RASi use during active LN induction, suggesting that RASi provide no additive renoprotective benefit — and may even delay early remission — when potent immunosuppression is the primary driver of kidney recovery. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this evidence creates a strategic opening to position targeted immunosuppressive biologics (e.g., voclosporin, belimumab, obinutuzumab) as more effective add-on therapies than RASi during LN induction, potentially reshaping treatment algorithms and the competitive antihypertensive-nephroprotection narrative in rheumatology nephrology. A critical KOL engagement priority is designing a prospective, multicenter RCT specifically comparing RASi versus non-RASi strategies during LN induction using validated renal remission endpoints, with nephrologists and rheumatologists in China, Europe, and North America as target investigator networks.

Real-world outcomes of Finerenone in patients with diabetic kidney disease in Saudi Arabia.

Revue : PloS one | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-01

Resultats principaux : This single-center retrospective cohort study from Saudi Arabia evaluated 67 adult patients with diabetic kidney disease (DKD) receiving finerenone as part of routine clinical care, assessing longitudinal changes in urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (uPCR), eGFR, and serum potassium using linear mixed-effects models, in a highly comorbid population (diabetes 89.6%, hypertension 97.0%, dyslipidemia 92.5%, heart failure 56.8%, coronary artery disease 40.3%). A significant time effect on uPCR was observed (F[3,193]=3.457; p=0.018), with a clinically meaningful mean reduction of 0.464 mg/mg after 6 months (95% CI: -0.895 to -0.034; p=0.027), confirming finerenone's antiproteinuric efficacy in real-world practice. However, eGFR declined at a rate of approximately -1.08 mL/min/1.73m² per month (95% CI: -1.74 to -0.41; p=0.002), corresponding to an annualized rate of -12.9 mL/min/1.73m² per year — substantially higher than the -1.43 mL/min/year seen in the FIDELIO-DKD trial, raising important safety concerns for clinical monitoring in complex real-world patients. Serum potassium increased modestly at early follow-up (+0.209 to +0.286 mmol/L at the first three readings, p<0.05) but plateaued thereafter (p>0.5), indicating a manageable and self-limiting hyperkalemia risk consistent with pivotal trial data. Key limitations include the small single-center sample (n=67), retrospective observational design without a comparator arm, and the exceptionally high comorbidity burden in this real-world cohort which may explain the accelerated eGFR decline and limits comparability with pivotal trials.

Implications Medical Affairs : These Saudi real-world data confirm finerenone's antiproteinuric efficacy in a highly comorbid DKD population, but the unexpectedly rapid eGFR decline (-12.9 mL/min/year) underscores the critical need for close renal monitoring and more careful patient selection criteria when initiating finerenone in patients with severe cardiorenal multimorbidity. Medical Affairs teams should use these findings to reinforce pharmacovigilance messaging around finerenone's renal safety monitoring requirements in real-world DKD populations with high hypertension prevalence, while also developing MENA-region specific medical education resources for nephrologists and diabetologists. KOL engagement with Saudi Arabian and broader Middle Eastern cardiorenal specialists should prioritize prospective multicenter registry studies generating robust regional safety and efficacy data on finerenone, addressing the gap between pivotal trial populations and the complex real-world DKD patients encountered in MENA clinical practice.

Factors affecting color vision: a systematic review.

Revue : International journal of ophthalmology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-18

Resultats principaux : This systematic review analyzed 50 eligible studies from 2014-2024 retrieved from six major databases (PubMed, Embase, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane Library, Google Scholar) to comprehensively summarize genetic, environmental, demographic, and systemic disease-related factors influencing color vision deficiency (CVD), with a specific emphasis on clinical implications for ophthalmology and optometry practice. The review found that CVD prevalence was markedly higher in males than females, and older age above 60 years was consistently identified as a risk factor for red-green CVD, alongside male sex and specific occupational characteristics such as UV radiation exposure. Systemic diseases including diabetes mellitus and hypertension, as well as optic nerve lesions, were confirmed as significant risk factors for acquired abnormal color vision beyond congenital and genetic factors, establishing hypertension as an independent driver of sensory organ damage. Occupational UV radiation exposure was also associated with CVD, suggesting important environmental-occupational dimensions to color vision health that have practical screening implications for certain professions. Significant inter-study heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis, and the predominantly cross-sectional nature of included studies limits causal inference regarding which hypertension mechanisms most directly impair retinal function and color processing.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review confirms hypertension as a significant risk factor for acquired color vision deficiency, providing novel evidence for an under-recognized extra-cardiovascular complication of uncontrolled hypertension that may be incorporated into ophthalmological monitoring recommendations within hypertension management guidelines. From a Medical Affairs perspective, these findings provide a compelling narrative for communicating the systemic sensory organ damage caused by uncontrolled hypertension, which can strengthen the clinical case for early and sustained antihypertensive therapy to prevent a wider spectrum of end-organ complications beyond traditional cardiac and renal outcomes. A strategic KOL opportunity exists to engage ophthalmologists and hypertension specialists jointly in prospective research evaluating whether effective antihypertensive treatment reverses or slows CVD progression, creating a novel real-world evidence niche that could differentiate antihypertensive products on sensory organ protection.

Global, regional, and national burden of tuberculosis and multidrug-resistant tuberculosis by HIV status, 1990-2023: a systematic analysis for the Global Burden of Disease Study 2023.

Revue : The Lancet. Infectious diseases | Tier : Tier 1 | IF estime : >20

Date de publication : 2026-07-01

Resultats principaux : This comprehensive systematic analysis from the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) Study 2023 quantified the burden of tuberculosis (TB) and multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) across 204 countries from 1990-2023, using cause of death ensemble modeling, DisMod-MR 2.1 for morbidity estimation, and comparative risk assessment frameworks with vital registration data, surveillance, verbal autopsy, and minimally invasive tissue sampling. In 2023, there were an estimated 9.11 million (95% UI: 8.04-10.3) incident TB cases, 1.22 million (0.98-1.49) deaths, and 54.6 million (43.8-65.5) DALYs globally; HIV-related TB contributed 781,000 cases and 210,000 deaths, while MDR-TB accounted for 466,000 cases and 102,000 deaths with substantial uncertainty. From 2015 to 2023, global TB incidence declined by 19.2% (17.8-20.5) and deaths by 22.6% (4.7-35.7), with progress highly uneven: sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia carried the highest mortality burdens, while Latin America and the Caribbean showed limited progress on both incidence and mortality. Critically, the analysis demonstrated that removing modifiable risk factors — specifically smoking, alcohol use, and high fasting plasma glucose (a proxy for diabetes/hypertension-related metabolic dysregulation) — would reduce global TB deaths to 768,000 and DALYs to 34.9 million, highlighting the major role of cardiometabolic risk factors in TB burden. Key limitations include wide uncertainty intervals in LMIC settings with limited vital registration infrastructure, reliance on modeled estimates for TB morbidity, and ecological attribution of risk factor contributions that cannot replace individual-level causality data.

Implications Medical Affairs : This landmark Lancet publication highlights the critical intersection between metabolic risk factors — including those tightly linked to hypertension such as high fasting plasma glucose, smoking, and cardiometabolic burden — and global TB mortality, reinforcing the need for integrated NCD and infectious disease management frameworks that include antihypertensive and antidiabetic therapies in high-TB-burden regions. Medical Affairs teams should leverage this Tier 1 publication to demonstrate the systemic downstream benefits of effective cardiometabolic risk management beyond cardiovascular disease, positioning comprehensive antihypertensive therapy as part of an integrated chronic disease prevention strategy in high-TB-burden LMIC markets. This GBD publication creates a high-profile KOL engagement opportunity at the intersection of global health, cardiology, and infectious disease to advocate for dual NCD-infectious disease integrated care programs that could create new market access pathways for antihypertensives in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study reveals that antihypertensive medication side effects — particularly sexual dysfunction — are a significant and culturally taboo barrier to medication adherence among men with diabetes and hypertension in Ghana, directly impacting the real-world effectiveness of antihypertensive therapy and highlighting a major quality-of-life gap that Medical Affairs must address. Implications Medical Affairs : This study's findings linking vitamin K intake to reduced depressive symptoms in an elderly cohort with high hypertension and frailty prevalence highlight the emerging role of nutritional factors in the mental health dimension of cardiometabolic disease management, an endpoint increasingly relevant to comprehensive hypertension outcome research.
