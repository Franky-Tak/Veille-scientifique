Optimizing Antihypertensive Care for Tanzanians Living With HIV: Effectiveness Outcomes From the COACH Pilot Trial.

Revue : Journal of Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndromes | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-08-01

Resultats principaux : This single-arm pilot trial enrolled 100 people living with HIV with uncontrolled hypertension across 2 public HIV clinics in Moshi, Tanzania, with 96 participants completing 6-month follow-up. The COACH intervention integrated community health worker-delivered hypertension education (6 monthly sessions), monthly blood pressure monitoring, subsidized antihypertensive medications, and a standardized treatment algorithm into existing HIV clinic workflows. The primary endpoint of blood pressure control (<140/90 mmHg) at 6 months was achieved in 76% of participants, with mean systolic blood pressure declining from 159.6 to 130.8 mmHg (mean change -28.9 mmHg; p<0.001) and diastolic blood pressure from 100.8 to 85.5 mmHg (-15.4 mmHg; p<0.001). Secondary outcomes showed dramatic improvements: hypertension knowledge scores increased from 15.0 to 20.4 (p<0.001) and self-reported medication adherence rose from 14.0% to 95.0% (p<0.001), while the proportion of participants with 5-year cardiovascular risk of at least 20% declined from 33% to 7% (p<0.001). Key limitations include the single-arm design without a randomized control group, small sample size of 100 participants, and restriction to a single geographic region in Tanzania, limiting generalizability to other low- and middle-income country settings.

Implications Medical Affairs : This trial demonstrates that integrating hypertension management into existing HIV care platforms is highly feasible and effective in sub-Saharan Africa, supporting calls from WHO and international guidelines to implement NCD co-management within HIV programs. The extraordinary improvement in adherence (from 14% to 95%) highlights that structural interventions combining CHW education, subsidized medications, and standardized protocols can dramatically increase antihypertensive use in resource-limited settings, creating a compelling opportunity for pharmaceutical companies to supply affordable antihypertensive agents through integrated care models. For Medical Affairs, this study provides a strong real-world evidence base for KOL engagement and advocacy around integrated NCD-HIV care programs, and should inform future sponsored implementation science research targeting high-burden sub-Saharan African markets.

Transforming Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension: Key Milestones and Future Perspectives.

Revue : Circulation | Tier : 1 | IF estime : ~38

Date de publication : 2026-07-13

Resultats principaux : This comprehensive state-of-the-art review in Circulation summarizes four decades of advances in pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH), covering pathobiology, epidemiology, diagnosis, and treatment. The pathobiology section highlights multi-pathway dysfunction including the prostacyclin, nitric oxide, endothelin-1, and bone morphogenetic protein/TGF-beta axes, with particular emphasis on the activin arm of TGF-beta signaling as the target of sotatercept — the first antiremodeling therapy approved for PAH — which demonstrated improvements in hemodynamics, right ventricular function, and reductions in adverse clinical outcomes in recent randomized trials. Epidemiologically, the PAH phenotype has shifted toward older adults with cardiovascular and pulmonary comorbidities, and the global burden shows geographic heterogeneity, with methamphetamine-associated PAH rising in North America and schistosomiasis/HIV-associated PAH prevalent in low- and middle-income countries. Current management guidelines recommend upfront combination oral therapy for most patients (with initial triple therapy including parenteral prostacyclin for high-risk individuals), with add-on sotatercept as a new option for patients not achieving low-risk status. Key remaining gaps include persistently delayed diagnosis, absence of personalized therapy approaches, limited management for comorbid phenotypes, and lack of effective treatments targeting right ventricular failure directly.

Implications Medical Affairs : The approval of sotatercept as the first antiremodeling agent represents a paradigm shift in PAH therapy, repositioning treatment goals from vasodilation alone toward vascular structural modification — a message with significant implications for market access, reimbursement strategy, and formulary positioning discussions with guideline-issuing bodies. For Medical Affairs teams, this Circulation review provides a high-impact synthesis for KOL engagement and scientific communication around the evolving PAH therapeutic landscape, particularly the move toward upfront combination and triple therapy strategies that may inform label-expansion and competitive differentiation discussions. The identified gaps in early detection, where AI-enabled tools applied to ECG, echocardiography, and electronic health records show promise, represent actionable areas for research collaboration and pipeline differentiation in the competitive PAH space.

Pre- and post-operative metabolic profile of patients with pheochromocytoma: A retrospective multicenter study.

Revue : Endocrine | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-13

Resultats principaux : This retrospective multicenter study included 117 patients diagnosed with pheochromocytoma at four Greek tertiary hospitals over 25 years (median age 51 years, 59% female), evaluating metabolic dysregulation before and after adrenalectomy. At diagnosis, the prevalence of diabetes/prediabetes was 58.1%, dyslipidemia 65.0%, and obesity 19.7%, representing a substantial and multifaceted cardiometabolic burden. Adrenergic phenotype was more common in older patients (p=0.004) and required significantly higher daily doses of phenoxybenzamine (p=0.01), while hypertensive patients had significantly larger tumors (p=0.01) with higher PASS (Pheochromocytoma of the Adrenal Gland Scaled Score) values (p=0.012). Following adrenalectomy (median follow-up 24 months in 93 patients), significant improvements were observed in hypertension prevalence and in serum HbA1c, fasting glucose, total cholesterol, and LDL levels (all p<0.05), despite the absence of significant change in body mass index. Key limitations include the retrospective design, single-country setting, variable follow-up duration across participants, and absence of a comparator group, which together limit causal inference.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings underscore that adrenalectomy for pheochromocytoma produces meaningful improvements in multiple cardiometabolic parameters including reversal of secondary hypertension, reinforcing the importance of early detection and biochemical workup in all patients presenting with resistant or unexplained hypertension and supporting the inclusion of secondary hypertension screening in updated guidelines. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this data supports comprehensive cardiovascular risk stratification tools in patients with secondary hypertension etiologies and highlights pheochromocytoma as a model for the intersection of endocrine and cardiovascular medicine where specialist education is needed. This study also provides real-world context for the pre-operative use of alpha-blockers such as phenoxybenzamine in pheochromocytoma-related hypertension management, representing an educational opportunity for Medical Affairs targeting endocrinologists and hypertension specialists.

Synergistic Effect of Hypertension and Smoking on Ischemic Stroke Risk: A Case-Control Study With Additive and Multiplicative Interaction Analysis.

Revue : Journal of Visualized Experiments (JoVE) | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-23

Resultats principaux : This single-center retrospective case-control study from China enrolled 312 cases with radiologically confirmed first-ever ischemic stroke and 624 age- and sex-matched controls (1:2 ratio), evaluating the independent and combined effects of smoking, alcohol consumption, and traditional cardiovascular risk factors on stroke risk. Current smoking (OR 2.34, 95% CI 1.82-3.01), heavy alcohol consumption exceeding 100 g/week (OR 1.89, 95% CI 1.34-2.67), hypertension (OR 3.21, 95% CI 2.54-4.06), and diabetes mellitus (OR 2.12, 95% CI 1.56-2.88) were each independently associated with ischemic stroke by conditional logistic regression with Firth penalization. Crucially, hypertension and current smoking demonstrated significant synergistic interaction on both multiplicative (interaction OR 1.58, 95% CI 1.12-2.24; p=0.009) and additive scales (relative excess risk due to interaction RERI=2.87, 95% CI 1.21-4.53; attributable proportion AP=0.32; synergy index SI=2.18, 95% CI 1.35-3.52), indicating a greater-than-additive increase in stroke risk for individuals with both exposures. The model showed good overall discrimination (AUC 0.82, 95% CI 0.79-0.85), and multiple imputation by chained equations was applied to handle missing data. Limitations include the single-center retrospective design with potential selection and recall bias, reliance on self-reported smoking and alcohol data, and limited generalizability beyond the Chinese population studied.

Implications Medical Affairs : The finding of synergistic supra-additive interaction between hypertension and smoking substantially elevates the risk profile for dually-exposed patients, providing a compelling evidence-based argument for intensified antihypertensive treatment targets in hypertensive smokers and supporting explicit co-targeting of smoking cessation in hypertension management guidelines. From a Medical Affairs standpoint, this data strengthens the clinical narrative for antihypertensive products by quantifying the synergistic stroke risk in this subgroup (attributable proportion 0.32), enabling targeted KOL and prescriber education around combined cardiovascular risk factor management and the importance of BP control in smokers. Future sponsored research should examine whether aggressive blood pressure control in hypertensive smokers attenuates the observed synergistic interaction effect — a meaningful unmet evidence gap that represents an opportunity for prospective study design in this high-risk population.

Effect of intermittent pneumatic compression on intracranial pressure in postoperative patients with severe traumatic brain injury.

Revue : Journal of International Medical Research | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-13

Resultats principaux : This prospective randomized comparative study enrolled 120 postoperative patients with severe traumatic brain injury admitted to a neurological intensive care unit, randomized into two groups of 60 each differing in the timing of intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) therapy — applied on postoperative days 1-3 in group A versus days 4-6 in group B — during a 6-day continuous intracranial pressure (ICP) monitoring period. The primary outcome was the impact of IPC timing on ICP dynamics, addressing a clinically important safety concern given that compression-related venous effects could theoretically raise ICP through hemodynamic transmission. In both groups, mean ICP, frequency of ICP episodes at or above 20 mmHg, and maximum daily ICP decreased progressively over time, with significant within-group improvements observed between the early and late postoperative observation periods. Critically, no significant between-group differences were found in any ICP parameter, confirming that the timing of IPC application does not differentially affect ICP trajectories in this population. No cases of lower extremity deep vein thrombosis were detected during the study period; limitations include the single-center design, modest sample size of 120 patients, and the 6-day observation window that precludes assessment of longer-term DVT or ICP outcomes.

Implications Medical Affairs : While this study focuses on intracranial hypertension rather than systemic arterial hypertension, the safety data regarding IPC in neurocritical care have indirect relevance for hypertension specialists managing patients with hypertensive encephalopathy or hypertension-related stroke who may simultaneously require DVT prophylaxis in an ICU setting. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this evidence supports the safe use of IPC as adjunct preventive therapy in post-neurosurgical patients with elevated ICP, without the concern of worsening intracranial hypertension, providing a data point relevant for educational content targeting intensivists and neurologists managing complex hypertensive emergencies. Future research should examine whether systemic blood pressure management protocols interact with IPC-related hemodynamic effects in patients with combined systemic and intracranial hypertension, which would be a clinically meaningful intersection point for Medical Affairs to support.

Age, Multidomain Lifestyle Intervention, and White Matter Integrity: Secondary Analysis of the POINTER Randomized Clinical Trial.

Revue : JAMA Network Open | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-01

Resultats principaux : The POINTER Imaging study was a prespecified secondary imaging analysis of the US POINTER randomized clinical trial enrolling 959 participants aged 60-79 years (mean age 68 years, SD 5; 61.9% female) at elevated cognitive decline risk, randomized to a structured multidomain lifestyle intervention (supervised exercise, dietary counseling, cognitive and social engagement, health coaching) versus a self-guided educational program, with annual MRI assessments at baseline, 12, and 24 months. Primary cerebrovascular MRI outcomes included global white matter free water (FW), fractional anisotropy (FA), peak width of skeletonized mean diffusivity (PSMD), ALPS index, white matter hyperintensity (WMH) volume, and incident cerebral microbleeds, with pre-specified age-group analyses in participants younger versus older than 70 years, and all analyses adjusted for sex, hypertension, diabetes, and smoking. A significant time x arm x age-group interaction was found for FW (beta=0.050; SE=0.018; p=0.006), with the structured intervention significantly attenuating the FW increase over time in participants under 70 years (beta=-0.031; SE=0.012; p=0.009), while no such benefit was observed in those aged 70 years or older (beta=0.019; SE=0.014; p=0.18). No intervention-related interactions were detected for WMH (p=0.90), FA (p=0.30), PSMD (p=0.60), or ALPS (p=0.79), indicating that FW is the most intervention-responsive cerebrovascular marker. Higher baseline FW predicted incident cerebral microbleeds (OR 1.63, 95% CI 1.24-2.14; p<0.001) and WMH progression, positioning baseline FW as a promising predictive biomarker for cerebrovascular injury progression.

Implications Medical Affairs : This study provides robust RCT evidence that structured multidomain lifestyle intervention can attenuate cerebral small-vessel disease progression in adults under 70 — an age group in which hypertension is a key modifiable driver — directly supporting guideline recommendations for lifestyle modification as first-line or adjunct therapy in hypertensive patients with cerebrovascular risk, and strengthening the evidence for earlier intervention. The age-dependent benefit (significant only below age 70) has important implications for Medical Affairs messaging around precision medicine, supporting the positioning of antihypertensive pharmacotherapy as a necessary complement to lifestyle programs in older hypertensive patients where lifestyle interventions alone show diminishing cerebrovascular impact. The validation of baseline FW as a predictive biomarker for subsequent cerebrovascular injury opens an opportunity for Medical Affairs to engage imaging and digital health partners in developing MRI-based risk-stratification tools for hypertensive patients with subclinical white matter changes, potentially supporting differentiated treatment selection strategies.

Integrating Chair Yoga and Manual Therapy Into Hypertension Management: Effects on Autonomic Regulation, Muscle Viscoelasticity, and Mental Well-Being.

Revue : Journal of Clinical Hypertension (Greenwich, Conn.) | Tier : 3 | IF estime : ~4

Date de publication : 2026-07

Resultats principaux : This single-blinded 12-week randomized controlled trial enrolled 75 hypertensive participants (48 females) across three groups: patient education alone (Group I, control), patient education plus chair-based yoga (Group II), and patient education plus manual therapy (Group III), with interventions conducted twice weekly for 45 minutes over 12 weeks. Primary cardiovascular outcomes included systolic and diastolic blood pressure and heart rate; secondary outcomes encompassed the 6-Minute Walk Test, muscle tone and stiffness assessed by Myoton PRO device, and validated measures of anxiety, depression, and mental well-being. Both chair yoga (Group II) and manual therapy (Group III) produced significant within-group reductions in systolic blood pressure (p=0.02 and p=0.03, respectively) and heart rate (p=0.04 and p=0.02), with significant group-by-time interactions favoring both active interventions over education alone. Manual therapy (Group III) specifically demonstrated significant reductions in muscle tone (p=0.03) and stiffness (p=0.04), while chair yoga (Group II) showed superior improvements in anxiety scores (p=0.04) and overall mental well-being compared to the other groups. Limitations include the single-blinded design, relatively small sample of 75 participants limiting statistical power, absence of long-term follow-up beyond 12 weeks, and lack of cardiovascular hard endpoint assessment (events, mortality).

Implications Medical Affairs : This RCT adds meaningful evidence to the non-pharmacological adjunct literature for hypertension management, demonstrating that both chair yoga and manual therapy produce clinically meaningful blood pressure reductions when added to standard education, which may inform guideline updates regarding structured exercise and mind-body interventions as adjuncts to antihypertensive pharmacotherapy. From a Medical Affairs standpoint, the psychosocial dimension of chair yoga's superior anxiety and mental well-being benefits is particularly relevant given growing recognition of psychosocial stress as a key modifiable hypertension risk factor, supporting differentiated educational initiatives targeting hypertension patients with comorbid anxiety or musculoskeletal conditions. The study's findings could be incorporated into co-promotion strategies or patient support programs alongside antihypertensive agents, and could support health economics arguments for comprehensive multimodal hypertension management programs in KOL engagement and payer discussions.

Coronary artery disease in smokers with low traditional risk burden: An exploratory analysis of potential genetic susceptibility.

Revue : Journal of Molecular and Cellular Cardiology Plus | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-01

Resultats principaux : This retrospective case-control association study from the GENEMACOR cohort enrolled 134 current smokers aged 35-50 years with LDL below 100 mg/dL who were explicitly non-diabetic and non-hypertensive, comprising 97 CAD cases (at least 70% coronary stenosis on angiography) and 37 CAD-free controls (83% male; mean age 48.9 years, SD 8.4), designed to isolate genetic susceptibility to CAD in the absence of major traditional risk factors. Nine genetic variants from 8 genes previously linked to CAD but not to traditional risk factors were genotyped by TaqMan real-time PCR, and bivariate analyses were followed by multivariate logistic regression to identify variants independently associated with CAD in this low-traditional-risk population. The intronic PHACTR1 polymorphism rs12526453 C>T was significantly more prevalent in CAD smokers (dominant model: 86.6% in cases vs 70.3% in controls; OR=2.73; p=0.028), and remained significant in multivariate regression (OR=3.02; p=0.038) alongside dyslipidemia (OR=3.58; p=0.004), obesity (OR=4.39; p=0.031), and family history of CAD (OR=5.56; p=0.031). The study's specific exclusion of hypertensive individuals provides a useful methodological control for isolating genetic effects but also limits direct application to hypertensive populations. Significant limitations include the small sample size of 134 subjects, single-center retrospective design, case-control limitations for temporal inference, and the exploratory nature of the genetic association findings requiring prospective replication.

Implications Medical Affairs : The identification of PHACTR1 rs12526453 as a potential genetic susceptibility marker for CAD in non-hypertensive low-LDL smokers highlights how residual cardiovascular risk persists even in the absence of traditional risk factors including hypertension, a finding with relevance for Medical Affairs teams developing messaging around the limitations of risk assessment based solely on conventional risk factor profiles. For hypertension product positioning, this study reinforces the concept that genetic background independently modifies cardiovascular risk, strengthening the case for personalized treatment approaches and comprehensive risk stratification in patients who may appear at low risk based on traditional criteria alone. Future studies should explicitly explore whether PHACTR1 variants interact with hypertension to further amplify CAD risk — a high-value research question that Medical Affairs could sponsor as part of a precision cardiovascular medicine agenda aimed at identifying patients most likely to benefit from early aggressive antihypertensive treatment.

Date de publication : 2026-07-14

Resultats principaux : This hospital-based cross-sectional study enrolled 199 adults from outpatient departments in Lahore, Pakistan (September 2023-June 2024) and investigated associations between serum fibroblast growth factor 1 (FGF1) levels, the rs152524 gene polymorphism, and metabolic risk factors including hypertension, obesity, and dyslipidemia. Females had significantly higher median FGF1 levels than males (333 pg/mL, IQR 250-467, versus 292 pg/mL, IQR 212-374; p=0.011), and multivariate regression analysis identified fasting blood glucose, LDL-cholesterol, and hypertension as the three principal independent predictors of FGF1 levels in the study population. The FGF1 rs152524 AA genotype was present in 38.7% of participants and was significantly associated with hypertension (p<0.001) and overweight/obesity (p=0.002), with AA carriers having the highest median FGF1 levels (351 pg/mL, IQR 263-546); the GG genotype was associated with higher HDL-cholesterol (36 IQR 32-42), suggesting a potentially protective effect. Allelic frequencies in the cohort were 0.5955 for the reference A allele and 0.4045 for the G allele, with risk increasing per A allele carried. Key limitations include hospital-based recruitment limiting population representativeness, small sample size of 199 subjects, cross-sectional design preventing causal inference, single-center setting in Pakistan, and the need for replication in larger population-based cohorts before clinical translation.

Implications Medical Affairs : This preliminary study implicates FGF1 and its rs152524 polymorphism in the pathogenesis of hypertension and metabolic syndrome, identifying a novel potential biomarker that could eventually inform genetic risk stratification for hypertensive patients in South Asian populations where hypertension prevalence is rising but genomic data remain scarce. From a Medical Affairs perspective, FGF1's independent predictive role for hypertension alongside glucose and LDL opens a potential research avenue for biomarker-guided antihypertensive treatment selection, representing an area where pharmaceutical companies could sponsor larger validation studies in diverse populations to establish clinical utility. The sex-based differences in FGF1 levels — with females showing higher levels correlating with greater metabolic risk — highlight sex-specific hypertension pathogenesis pathways relevant for developing gender-stratified treatment recommendations, an emerging area for KOL engagement in precision hypertension medicine.

Resultats principaux : This two-step Mendelian randomization (MR) study used GWAS summary statistics from the UK Biobank Proteomics (N=34,557) and the largest renal cell carcinoma (RCC) GWAS to date (29,020 cases) to systematically identify plasma proteins mediating the causal effects of 19 risk factors — including hypertension, obesity, and smoking — on RCC risk. Among 2,940 plasma proteins assessed, 2,339 were significantly associated with at least one of the 19 risk factors (p<1.7E-05), and of these, 33 showed significant effects on RCC (FDR<5%), with 28 mapping outside known RCC GWAS loci, suggesting novel molecular pathways. TYMP and USP28 (both inversely associated with RCC) showed immune-related and tumor-suppressive mechanisms, while UMOD — positively associated with RCC risk — may link hypertension-related renal tubular dysfunction to carcinogenesis. Hypertension was among the 19 risk factors included as an upstream determinant, making this study directly relevant to understanding plasma protein pathways through which elevated blood pressure may promote kidney cancer development; limitations include Mendelian randomization assumptions of instrument validity and potential pleiotropy.

Implications Medical Affairs : The identification of plasma proteins mediating hypertension's effect on RCC risk — particularly UMOD linking renal dysfunction to carcinogenesis — provides a novel molecular framework for understanding how inadequately controlled blood pressure may promote kidney cancer, extending the clinical rationale for antihypertensive treatment beyond cardiovascular protection to oncological risk reduction. For Medical Affairs teams, this mechanistic evidence suggests that aggressive blood pressure control may offer oncological protective benefits beyond direct cardiovascular outcomes, a differentiating value proposition that could be incorporated into scientific narratives for antihypertensive agents in high-risk patient populations such as those with chronic kidney disease or family history of RCC. The prioritized proteins TYMP, UMOD, and USP28 represent potential biomarkers or therapeutic targets warranting collaborative research between hypertension and oncology KOLs, creating an interdisciplinary publication and engagement opportunity with high-impact potential in both cardiology and oncology communities.

Gut-heart axis at high altitude: a dynamic mediator from hypoxic dysbiosis to adaptive cardioprotection.

Revue : Frontiers in Microbiology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-22

Resultats principaux : This narrative review synthesizes emerging evidence on the gut microbiota's dynamic role within the gut-heart axis at high altitude, examining how hypoxic environments drive acute microbial dysbiosis that promotes cardiovascular pathology — including hypertension, atherosclerosis, and heart failure — before adaptive microbiome remodeling occurs in chronic high-altitude residents. At the metabolic level, acute hypoxia-driven dysbiosis reduces short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, increases trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) accumulation, promotes accumulation of energy metabolism intermediates (lactic acid, succinic acid), and dysregulates secondary bile acid metabolism — all pathways mechanistically linked to hypertension pathogenesis. At the immune-inflammatory level, impaired intestinal barrier integrity under hypoxia leads to lipopolysaccharide (LPS) translocation, which combined with HIF-1alpha overexpression drives systemic inflammation and vascular remodeling that promotes atherosclerosis, hypertension, and heart failure. In contrast, adaptive microbiome remodeling in chronic high-altitude dwellers appears cardioprotective through enhanced myocardial energy metabolism (SCFA-mediated), reinforced intestinal barrier function, anti-inflammatory immune reprogramming, and blood pressure stabilization. Proposed therapeutic strategies — high-fiber dietary modulation, probiotic/prebiotic/synbiotic supplementation, fecal microbiota transplantation, and metabolite-directed therapies — represent emerging intervention targets, though controlled trial evidence in high-altitude populations remains limited.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review positions gut microbiota as a previously underappreciated mechanistic mediator of hypertension in high-altitude environments, which has implications for broadening hypertension risk factor recognition to include environmental altitude exposure and microbiome composition — factors that could inform tailored guideline adaptation for the estimated 140 million people living permanently above 2,500 m worldwide. From a Medical Affairs perspective, the emerging microbiome-hypertension connection at high altitude represents a potential area for pipeline investment or research partnership, as microbiome-targeted therapies including probiotics and SCFA-directed approaches may complement antihypertensive pharmacotherapy in altitude-dwelling populations and address residual blood pressure risk not captured by standard treatment algorithms. Engagement with research institutions studying gut-heart axis dynamics in hypoxic environments could yield Medical Affairs content differentiation, with particular market relevance in high-altitude regions including the Andean countries, the Tibetan Plateau, and parts of Central Asia and East Africa.

Prevalence and Factors of Gallstone Disease in the High-Altitude Tribal Population of Spiti Valley, Northern India: A Population-Based Cross-Sectional Study.

Revue : Cureus | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-12

Resultats principaux : This community-based cross-sectional study in Spiti Valley, Himachal Pradesh, India used stratified random sampling to enroll 450 native adults aged 30-70 years across three altitude strata (<3500 m, 3501-4000 m, and >4000 m, n=150 per stratum), with all participants undergoing fasting abdominal ultrasonography to determine gallstone prevalence and identify associated risk factors. Overall gallstone prevalence was 21.3% (96/450), with crude prevalence rates of 24.0%, 24.7%, and 15.3% at low, mid, and high altitude strata respectively; the across-strata difference did not reach statistical significance (p=0.089). Multivariable logistic regression identified age (adjusted OR 1.04 per year, 95% CI 1.01-1.06; p=0.001), female sex (aOR 2.04, 95% CI 1.19-3.51; p=0.010), and fatty liver (aOR 1.79, 95% CI 1.05-3.06; p=0.032) as independent predictors of gallstone disease, while altitude group was not independently associated after adjustment. Hypertension was recorded as a covariate in the study protocol but did not emerge as an independent predictor of gallstone disease in this high-altitude Himalayan population, providing useful negative epidemiological data on the hypertension-gallstone relationship. Key limitations include the cross-sectional design precluding causal inference, single-region setting limiting generalizability, potential sampling bias in a remote tribal community, and the absence of dietary, physical activity, and lipid profile data.

Implications Medical Affairs : While gallstone disease falls outside the arterial hypertension therapeutic domain, this study's high-altitude tribal population setting and inclusion of hypertension as a covariate provide useful epidemiological context for cardiometabolic comorbidity burden in underrepresented Himalayan populations where hypertension control data and cardiovascular disease prevalence remain poorly characterized. The high overall gallstone prevalence (21.3%) in this population alongside the absence of significant altitude-hypertension interaction with gallstone risk adds to a growing literature suggesting that distinct metabolic phenotypes exist in indigenous high-altitude populations — data potentially relevant for framing Medical Affairs research priorities in underserved geographies. This population represents an uncharacterized segment with potentially distinct cardiovascular and metabolic pharmacological needs, and Medical Affairs teams could advocate for dedicated hypertension prevalence and treatment studies in high-altitude indigenous populations as part of a global equity and access research agenda.
