Comparing the Documentation of Phosphatidylethanol and Alcohol History in Hypertension Management in Primary Care: A Cross-Sectional Study.

Revue : Journal of Primary Care & Community Health | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-02

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study conducted at a Swedish primary care centre enrolled 1,826 patients with hypertension (mean age 71 years; 51.5% women), categorising them as having controlled BP (<140/90 mmHg, 35%), uncontrolled BP (50%), or apparent treatment-resistant hypertension (aTRH, 16%). The study compared two alcohol documentation methods: phosphatidylethanol (PEth) blood biomarker testing versus self-reported alcohol history (free-text entries or AUDIT questionnaire). PEth was documented more frequently than alcohol history (56.0% vs 39.9%), while women, older patients, and those with diabetes were most likely to lack any documentation of alcohol use. Hazardous alcohol use was identified in 18.4% of patients by PEth versus only 7.1% by alcohol history, demonstrating a greater than twofold underestimation when relying on self-report alone. No statistically significant differences in hazardous alcohol prevalence were observed across BP control groups (chi-square, ANOVA), but men had higher PEth-defined hazardous use than women, a difference not captured by alcohol history. Key limitations include the single-centre cross-sectional design, potential selection bias from a centre already using PEth routinely, and the inability to establish causality between alcohol use and BP control.

Implications Medical Affairs : These findings highlight that hazardous alcohol use, a well-established modifiable risk factor for hypertension and treatment resistance, is substantially underrecognised in primary care when relying solely on self-reported data, with implications for incorporating biomarker-based alcohol screening into hypertension management protocols and future guideline updates. From a Medical Affairs perspective, this evidence supports positioning antihypertensive therapies within a broader multimodal risk factor management framework, particularly in treatment-resistant hypertension where undetected alcohol use may be a confounding variable. KOL collaboration with primary care and addiction medicine specialists could accelerate the development and dissemination of integrated screening protocols combining PEth testing with antihypertensive treatment optimisation strategies.

Traditional Chinese Medicine Syndrome Patterns and Associated Factors in Adults with Type 2 Diabetes and Metabolic Syndrome: A Data-Driven Analysis.

Revue : Frontiers in Endocrinology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-17

Resultats principaux : This cross-sectional study enrolled 505 adults with comorbid type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) and metabolic syndrome (MetS) in Hong Kong to identify TCM syndrome patient subgroups using data-driven approaches including principal component analysis (PCA) and latent class analysis (LCA). PCA derived five syndrome constructs: Phlegm and Blood Stasis (25.54%), Qi and Yin Deficiency (22.97%), Yin and Yang Deficiency (22.97%), Kidney Essence Deficiency (10.89%), and Spleen and Kidney Yang Deficiency (17.62%), while LCA identified five patient subgroups with Liver and Kidney Yin Deficiency being the most prevalent (36.24%). Syndrome patterns were significantly associated with multiple clinical factors (all p<0.05), including hypertension, triglyceride levels, basal metabolic rate, alcohol and caffeine consumption, and patient-reported outcomes such as fatigue severity (Fatigue Severity Scale) and sleep quality (Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index). Hypertension emerged as a significant determinant of TCM syndrome classification, underscoring its role in shaping the broader cardiometabolic phenotype in these patients. Limitations include the cross-sectional design of a single Hong Kong cohort with limited international generalisability, and the exploratory nature of the statistical methodology which cannot establish causal pathways.

Implications Medical Affairs : Although this trial explores TCM-based patient stratification rather than conventional antihypertensive pharmacotherapy, the robust association between hypertension and metabolic syndrome clusters provides additional epidemiological support for integrated cardiometabolic management approaches that address both BP and metabolic risk simultaneously. For Medical Affairs, the identification of distinct patient subgroups by comorbidity burden, including hypertension, supports the development of targeted patient segmentation strategies and outcomes research agendas in complex cardiometabolic populations. Engaging KOLs in integrative medicine and metabolic syndrome research could provide valuable insights into real-world treatment patterns and unmet needs that may inform antihypertensive product positioning in multimorbid patient populations.

Celiac Disease and Cardiovascular Disease: Epidemiology, Mechanisms, and Clinical Challenges.

Revue : Cardiology in Review | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-04

Resultats principaux : This narrative review synthesises epidemiological and mechanistic evidence on the relationship between celiac disease (CD) and cardiovascular disease (CVD), drawing on recent observational cohort studies and systematic reviews. Individuals with CD demonstrate a modest but statistically significant increased risk of ischemic heart disease, myocardial infarction, and venous thromboembolism, despite a paradoxically lower prevalence of traditional risk factors including obesity, hypertension, and smoking, a phenomenon termed the cardiovascular risk factor paradox. Proposed non-traditional mechanisms driving excess CVD risk include chronic systemic inflammation, immune dysregulation, nutritional deficiencies (folate, B vitamins, iron), intestinal malabsorption, and the metabolic consequences of a gluten-free diet, which can paradoxically increase glycaemic index and saturated fat intake. Emerging paediatric data indicate early onset of non-traditional cardiovascular risk in children with CD, and some cardiac manifestations such as arrhythmias and cardiomyopathy may be reversible with strict gluten-free diet adherence. Significant research gaps remain, including the absence of CD-specific cardiovascular risk prediction tools, limited randomised trial data on dietary quality, and incomplete understanding of genetic and autoimmune contributions to CVD in CD.

Implications Medical Affairs : This review challenges the conventional assumption that lower rates of hypertension in celiac disease confer cardiovascular protection, underscoring that systemic inflammation and non-traditional risk pathways can drive significant cardiovascular risk independent of blood pressure status, with implications for broadening cardiovascular risk screening criteria in immune-mediated disorders. For Medical Affairs, these findings support messaging around the multifactorial nature of cardiovascular risk and the importance of comprehensive risk factor management beyond BP control, particularly in autoimmune and gastrointestinal disease populations with atypical risk profiles. KOL engagement spanning gastroenterology, rheumatology, and cardiology could facilitate collaborative research initiatives exploring the role of anti-inflammatory strategies and optimised antihypertensive management in reducing residual cardiovascular risk in celiac and related autoimmune conditions.

Diabetes among Indigenous Orang Asli Adults in Peninsular Malaysia: Prevalence and Associated Factors from Orang Asli Health Survey 2022.

Revue : PLoS ONE | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-02

Resultats principaux : The Orang Asli Health Survey 2022 was a nationwide cross-sectional survey using a two-stage stratified sampling design that included 9,206 Indigenous Orang Asli adults across nine states of Peninsular Malaysia, stratified by locality (urban, fringe, remote) and tribal group (Senoi, Proto-Malay, Negrito). The overall prevalence of type 2 diabetes was 16.1% (95% CI: 14.3-17.9%), with significantly higher rates among females (16.8%), adults aged ≥60 years (28.1%), urban residents (21.1%), and individuals with comorbid hypertension (24.2%) or hypercholesterolaemia (21.7%). Multivariable logistic regression identified hypertension as an independent predictor of diabetes (AOR 1.42; 95% CI: 1.03-1.95), with hypercholesterolaemia showing the strongest association (AOR 2.17; 95% CI: 1.70-2.76). Urbanisation was also independently associated with diabetes in fringe (AOR 1.63; 95% CI: 1.13-2.35) and urban (AOR 1.73; 95% CI: 1.07-2.79) localities, reflecting lifestyle transitions driven by sedentarisation and dietary shifts. Key limitations include the cross-sectional design precluding causal inference, potential underestimation of diabetes prevalence in remote communities with limited healthcare access, and reliance on self-reported physician diagnosis in addition to fasting glucose measurements.

Implications Medical Affairs : The strong independent association between hypertension and diabetes in this underserved Indigenous population reinforces the epidemiological evidence for integrated cardiometabolic risk management, supporting guideline recommendations for combined screening and treatment of hypertension and dysglycaemia. From a Medical Affairs perspective, these data highlight a substantial unmet need in Indigenous and low-resource Southeast Asian populations, where high rates of comorbid hypertension and diabetes represent an opportunity to advocate for access to evidence-based antihypertensive therapies within comprehensive metabolic risk programmes. Partnerships with public health authorities and KOLs in endocrinology and preventive cardiology in Malaysia and the broader ASEAN region could support the development of culturally adapted cardiovascular risk reduction strategies and strengthen product positioning in emerging markets.

Metabolic Syndrome and Risk of Atrial Fibrillation in Chronic Kidney Disease.

Revue : American Journal of Nephrology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-02

Resultats principaux : This prospective cohort analysis included 4,641 participants from the Chronic Renal Insufficiency Cohort (CRIC) without baseline atrial fibrillation (AF), with a mean age of 59±11 years (44% female, 42% Black). Over a mean follow-up of 9.3 years, 616 participants (13%) were hospitalised with AF, and a higher metabolic syndrome score (Adult Treatment Panel III criteria) was independently associated with AF hospitalisation (adjusted hazard ratio [aHR] per unit: 1.16; 95% CI: 1.08-1.24). Hypertension was one of the most robustly and consistently associated individual metabolic syndrome components with AF risk (aHR 1.61; 95% CI: 1.09-2.39), retaining significance after adjustment for all other metabolic syndrome components in exploratory models. Elevated waist circumference was also independently associated with AF (aHR 1.29; 95% CI: 1.05-1.60), and markers of visceral adiposity including waist-to-height ratio (aHR per 0.1 unit: 1.23; 95% CI: 1.14-1.34) and fat mass index (aHR per 5 kg/m²: 1.22; 95% CI: 1.12-1.34) were independently predictive. Limitations include reliance on hospitalisation as the AF ascertainment endpoint, which likely undercounts paroxysmal AF episodes, potential residual confounding by unmeasured covariates, and the observational nature of the study precluding causal conclusions.

Implications Medical Affairs : This large prospective CKD cohort study solidifies hypertension as a critical independent modifiable risk factor for atrial fibrillation, beyond its role within the broader metabolic syndrome construct, providing compelling evidence for aggressive BP management in the high-risk nephrology population. For Medical Affairs, these findings support positioning antihypertensive therapies within AF prevention narratives in CKD patients with metabolic syndrome, an emerging and commercially relevant indication area given the rising prevalence of CKD and the substantial morbidity associated with AF in this population. Engaging KOLs in nephrology, cardiac electrophysiology, and metabolic medicine to explore controlled trial designs examining BP targets and AF incidence reduction in CKD could generate influential data supporting both guideline evolution and product differentiation strategies.

Metabolomics and Cardiovascular Risk Factors in Autoimmune-Mediated Connective Tissue Diseases: An Exploratory, Hypothesis-Generating Study.

Revue : BMC Rheumatology | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-07-01

Resultats principaux : This exploratory cross-sectional study recruited 76 patients with autoimmune connective tissue diseases (ACTD) from a tertiary rheumatology centre, including systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE, n=33), systemic sclerosis (SSc, n=18), Sjögren's disease (SD, n=16), and idiopathic inflammatory myopathies (IIM, n=9), and performed serum metabolomic profiling using ¹H-NMR spectroscopy identifying 113 quantified metabolites. Lipid metabolites showed the strongest and most consistent associations with cardiovascular risk (CVR) parameters across ACTD subtypes, with glucocorticoid therapy particularly strongly linked to lipid metabolic alterations in SLE and SSc. Arterial hypertension demonstrated minimal metabolomic differentiation across most ACTD subtypes, with the notable exception of IIM, suggesting that hypertension-associated cardiovascular risk in these diseases may operate through pathways that are not readily captured by standard metabolomic signatures. The Framingham CVR score showed highly variable metabolomic correlates: 93 metabolites correlated with Framingham score in IIM but only 11 in SLE, 2 in SD, and 1 in SSc, reflecting substantial disease-specific heterogeneity. Limitations include the very small per-disease-subtype sample sizes (n=9 to 33), single-centre design, cross-sectional methodology, and the exploratory hypothesis-generating intent of the study which limits interpretive confidence.

Implications Medical Affairs : The finding that arterial hypertension shows limited metabolomic differentiation in most ACTD subtypes suggests that conventional risk factor management may be insufficient to fully capture or address the elevated cardiovascular risk in autoimmune populations, highlighting the need for disease-specific risk stratification tools beyond standard BP measurement. For Medical Affairs, this study draws attention to a population where current antihypertensive frameworks may be inadequate and where novel biomarker-guided approaches could represent an unmet clinical need, opening avenues for targeted research collaborations. Engaging metabolomics experts and rheumatology KOLs in longitudinal study designs could help validate disease-specific CVR biomarkers and inform innovative combination strategies pairing antihypertensive management with immunomodulatory treatments in ACTD populations.

Risk Factors for Age-Related Macular Degeneration: An Umbrella Analysis of Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses.

Revue : Frontiers in Medicine | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-17

Resultats principaux : This umbrella review systematically searched PubMed, Web of Science, Embase, and the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews to April 2025 to identify systematic reviews and meta-analyses examining risk factors for age-related macular degeneration (AMD), ultimately analysing 53 distinct risk factors. Of these, 30 risk factors showed statistically significant associations with AMD risk, while 23 did not, with methodological quality assessed using the AMSTAR tool and evidence graded via the GRADE framework. Hypertension emerged as a consistently identified systemic risk factor for AMD, alongside established factors such as age, smoking, diet, and diabetes; three risk factors reached moderate evidence quality while most were classified as low or very low quality. Circulating biomarkers including carotenoids (protective), C-reactive protein (harmful), and high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C) were also associated with AMD risk, with varying evidence strength. Key limitations include the inherent risk of duplicate publications and overlapping populations across constituent meta-analyses, predominantly low-quality underlying evidence from observational studies, and the inability to address reverse causation or publication bias comprehensively.

Implications Medical Affairs : The identification of hypertension as a consistent risk factor for AMD in this comprehensive umbrella review provides an additional clinical argument for rigorous blood pressure control, extending the protective rationale for antihypertensive therapy beyond cardiovascular endpoints to include visual system preservation. For Medical Affairs, this evidence supports developing cross-specialty messaging for ophthalmology KOLs around the systemic benefits of BP management, reinforcing that hypertension-associated end-organ damage extends to the retinal microvasculature, an angle that remains underexplored in physician-facing communications. Future investigator-initiated research examining whether specific antihypertensive drug classes (particularly those with antioxidant or anti-inflammatory properties) differentially modulate AMD progression could generate high-impact data and expand the evidence-based KOL engagement strategy.

A Healthy Mind in a Healthy Body: The Challenges of Physical Health in First Episode Psychosis.

Revue : Psychiatriki | Tier : NA | IF estime : NA

Date de publication : 2026-06-08

Resultats principaux : This editorial from Greek academic psychiatry centres reviews evidence on physical health challenges in first episode psychosis (FEP), with a focus on metabolic syndrome (MetS) and cardiovascular risk, contextualising clinical management within Early Intervention in Psychosis (EIP) services. Individuals with schizophrenia have a documented life expectancy 10-20 years shorter than the general population, predominantly driven by cardiovascular disease associated with MetS, obesity, diabetes, sedentary behaviour, and smoking. MetS prevalence was reportedly 13.2% higher in antipsychotic-naive FEP patients compared to the general population, with elevated blood pressure, waist circumference, and fasting glucose documented even before antipsychotic initiation in ultra-high-risk (UHR) individuals. Second-generation antipsychotic initiation was associated with clinically significant weight gain (≥7% of initial body weight) in 33-61% of FEP patients within the first 12 weeks of treatment, progressively worsening metabolic parameters over the first year. Hypertension is explicitly listed as a core monitoring target in international guidelines, including the Lester Positive Cardiometabolic Resource algorithm used by the iphys initiative in the UK, with BP measurement recommended at baseline and every three months thereafter. The editorial is limited by its narrative scope, absence of new primary data, and reliance on previously published evidence without formal systematic synthesis.

Implications Medical Affairs : This editorial reinforces that hypertension screening and management must begin at the earliest stages of psychotic illness, including before antipsychotic initiation, recognising psychiatric patients as a high-priority but often overlooked population for cardiovascular risk reduction. From a Medical Affairs perspective, the substantial and often iatrogenic cardiometabolic burden in FEP patients represents a positioning opportunity for antihypertensive agents with favourable metabolic profiles, which could complement atypical antipsychotic regimens without exacerbating metabolic complications. Collaborative KOL programmes across psychiatry and cardiology to develop and disseminate integrated cardiometabolic monitoring protocols for FEP patients could generate high-impact cross-specialty publications and strengthen the evidence base for holistic cardiovascular risk management in this vulnerable population.
